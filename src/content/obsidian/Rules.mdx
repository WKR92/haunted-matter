# Minimum rules

## For Whom

**Haunted Matter** is for people who choose drama over simulation, narrative over mechanics, and decision-making over supernatural power. There are no levels, classes, builds, great powers, or balance. There is risk, pseudo-realism, fluidity, coherent rules, narrative thinking, real consequences, mud, blood, and pressure. It requires an understanding of the rules, but it provides concrete mechanics that help keep the story under control. This is a system for playing out a story — dark and fast drama-action — low-magic crunchy-narrative dark survival fantasy in a mystical and brutal world where the struggle to survive comes before morality.

It combines traditional elements (the **GM** has a story), PbtA, BitD (fiction first, degrees of success, **P** taking part in creating details of the world, etc.), and OSR (resource management, survival, lethality).

The system aims to realize one specific belief: narrative matters most, but you cannot create a deep moral story through improvisation alone, so the framework of the story is provided by the GM, while the **P** can move freely within it, filling it with detail.

I call this _play to reveal_ as opposed to _play to find out_ — the players co-create the world, but within the boundaries of the narrative axis prepared by the GM. They do not create the story on the fly, but develop it through decisions that reveal further facts, secrets, consequences, and meanings. They add details that give the story depth and direction without breaking its structure.  
For example: the **GM** knows exactly what is behind the building’s door, but if a player says they are circling around it and trying to find a second entrance — and the roll allows it, and it does not disrupt the story’s axis (the GM’s decision) — then another way in truly exists and affects the story’s meaning.  
The **GM** has the structure (scenes, clocks, factions, consequences), but _what is revealed and how it is interpreted_ depends on the players’ actions.

This is not a scenario with elements of improvisation — the structure does not provide answers or ways to overcome problems — it provides challenges and meaning that the **P** must deal with on their own.

The world has its own logic, tensions, and directions of development even before the **P** appear. The game is about discovering that meaning and managing its consequences through decisions — it is the **P** choices that shape the story.

HM is not an adventure game in the classic sense, even though adventure is an integral part of it. It is a systemic game — about friction between forces, tensions between factions, and political-social drama. The **P** actions are not episodes, but interventions in a web of dependencies that reacts and changes.

HM assumes that the characters are good or neutral and aims to put their morality, worldview, faith, and convictions to the test by immersing them in drama with no good way out.

Every rule included here can and should be changed in order to make the narrative and the flow of play smoother, and to make sure everyone at the table has fun. The **GM** has the final say.

## How to play

The game takes the form of a conversation, where points of tension are resolved through dice rolls. It’s a collaborative, interactive storytelling experience.

The **GM** (Game Master) places the **P** (Players) in specific scenes within the world through description, and the players — imagining themselves as real individuals within that reality — act as if they were truly there.

**Example:**  
**GM**: The incense smoke stings your eyes. Thousands of chanting throats fill the air of a crystal cavern so vast its ceiling is lost in shadow. A priest, standing in the center of a runic circle, slits the throat of a screaming doe, its blood splattering over the offerings laid before the god. The ritual is almost complete — the darkness will open, revealing a pastel landscape of a surreal dream realm — the faithful will soon begin to dream while awake. But there’s unrest in the crowd. It’s your duty to keep order here; you swore it. Nothing can interrupt the ceremony. Something unseen moves among the kneeling worshipers nearby. They’re stirring, afraid to rise. What do you do?  
**P**: I want to position myself so I can intercept whatever’s moving there as fast as possible, without disrupting the prayer — even if it means stepping over people or brushing past them.  
**GM**: Okay. That’ll be a roll for Dexterity, with a penalty, since you’re trying to move both carefully and quickly.  
**P**: I got a **partial success**.  
**GM**: You make it to the spot where the intruder is about to emerge, but your awkward movement breaks the rhythm of the chant. You’ll need to take a **splinter** if you want the harmony to hold.  
**P**: I’ll take the **splinter**.  
**GM**: Alright. You hear the melody of the prayer return to its steady rhythm — and at that moment, something steps into view that makes your insides twist. You see a young **Uurundi**, a sacred creature worshiped across nations, whose bellies grow blue orbs of energy that power ancient technology. They only live in their native lands. Its presence here is a violation of faith and law. How did it get in? Someone must have let it in on purpose. You don’t have time to wonder — it’s a provocation. You’re the first to see it, a Priest of Dream, and it’s your duty to act. You must choose. If you ignore it, the foreign guests will accuse you of breaking the pact. If you expose it, the ritual will collapse into chaos. Which sanctity do you protect?  
**P**: I take the risk. I remove my cloak, cover the creature, and calmly walk toward the exit as if nothing happened.  
**GM**: That’s a Performance test — you’re acting for everyone present. Since you’ve covered the **Uurundi**, you cancel the penalty you’d normally have on this roll.  
**P**: I got a **failure**.  
**GM**: ...  
**P**: I’ll use a **fate die**... rolled a 6! I change the narration: I manage to lead the **Uurundi** out, and those who saw it I convince with whispers that it was a fragment of dreamstuff slipping into the waking world.  
And so on.

The goal of the game is not to win — it’s to tell a story. The **GM** is neither against the players nor on their side. Everyone at the table builds the narrative together.

## Legend

- P — player, players, player's, player's character, etc.
- **GM** — game master.
- action — any action a character takes on their turn, such as an attack, using a power, movement, or interacting with the environment.
- arcana — magical items.
- easy, standard, strong, powerful — the power scale of enemies.
- scar — a permanent effect left by critical injuries.
- trait — a character’s basic aptitude (physical, mental, social, or specialization) that determines the dice pool for tests.
- short rest — one hour of downtime that allows you to remove 2 fatigue; also allows downtime actions.
- long rest — 8 hours of rest; restores 5 fatigue and allows downtime actions.
- discomfort — hunger or biting cold that prevent rest.
- equipment — everything the character carries; a P has an appropriate number of item slots.
- slot — a unit of space in the inventory; shared with HP and fatigue.
- main die — one of the dice in a test (set apart), whose result may trigger an additional effect.
- HP — hit points; abstract resistance to harm, not physical wounds, refreshed after a scene.
- fate dice — extra dice that can be used to alter the narrative or added to a test’s dice pool.
- critical injury dice — used to avoid death when a P’s HP drops below 0; losing them means a real wound.
- condition — a short-term combat effect (e.g. STUNNED, BLEEDING) inflicted by weapons or powers.
- critical success — rolling two 6s; grants the maximum effect and an additional fate die.
- critical failure — rolling two 1s; an automatic failure and a splinter for the character.
- magic — using arcana, nature, or religion to manipulate matter; costs fatigue.
- identity — a unique role, nature, or profession of a character (e.g. necromancer, monk, master swordsman) that defines what special actions and effects they can introduce into the fiction.
- madness — represents the loss of mental stability; destabilizes tests; at 5 points, the character loses control.
- armor — the number of damage dice that can be blocked; physical or magical.
- full success — a result of 6 on a die; the action proceeds without complications.
- partial success — a result of 4–5; the action succeeds, but with a penalty or cost.
- failure — a result of 1–3; the **GM** describes the negative outcome of the action.
- destiny point — a one-time chance to avoid death; leaves trauma behind.
- advancement points — a reward for progress; used to improve the character, develop traits, buy powers, etc.
- reaction — an enemy’s response to a player’s action.
- reputation — the level of relationship with a faction/area; positive and negative values have their reflection in the behavior of the locals.
- status — a long-term negative effect, e.g. WOUNDED, HALLUCINATIONS, WEAKENED; makes tests harder.
- disaadventage — a penalty to a roll (–1 die) or a reduction of the maximum possible result.
- advantage — a bonus to a roll (+1 die); cancels a disaadventage or increases the chance of success.
- natural, dominant, privileged, executive, threatening authority — the degree of influence held by an individual or group, affecting a P’s narrative possibilities.
- splinter — a temporary weakness of the character; activates on a 1 on the main die, making the result worse.
- clock — a progress or threat tracker that triggers a specific effect once filled.
- corruption clock — represents the degree to which the soul has been tainted by magic or alchemy; leads to mutation.
- fatigue — the main cost of actions; takes up slots, reduces HP, and causes disaadventages.
- zone — an area on the battlefield; a unit of space where action takes place.
- downtime action — after 3 days in a larger settlement, **P** may use their free time in various ways.
- inevitable damage — cannot be avoided.
- moves — special tests that help **P** interact with the world; they build the narrative.
- utility item — ordinary items with helpful uses, such as a torch.
- elixirs — drinks with powerful magical-chemical properties.
- decoctions — liquids that grant special effects, but at a certain cost.
- tactical gear — simple technology that makes it easier to overcome obstacles.
- technology — complex mechanisms with highly useful properties — powered by energy orbs.
- energy orbs — bio-batteries for technology, produced from the blue fluids secreted by the transparent underbellies of the sacred animals of the Uurundi.
- wonders of the Jaruga — flora and fauna mutated by the power of the river-god, with astonishing traits.
- healing items — salves, balms, and other items that remove fatigue, statuses, and similar negative effects from **P**.
- exotic material — unusual substances or parts of monster bodies — a component used to upgrade items.
- runes — grant an item one-time magical properties.
- scrolls — store pure magical potential.
- resource/component/material — ingredients needed to create items.
- treasure — the equivalent of valuable loot, such as a golden goblet, tapestry, jewelry, etc.
- armor piercing X: X successes always hit; armor can only block the ones after that.
- wealth level — an abstract representation of a P’s financial means.
- market — a place of trade that **P** may visit once per stay in a settlement.

## Core Mechanics

- Fiction first, mechanics second. Say what you want to do, and the **GM** will decide whether it is possible and which mechanic to use.
- Almost all tests are made with a pool of d6s.
- Number of dice = the level of the **trait** being used (usually 1–4).
- **Main die** (in a different color):
  - **5–6**: the **P** may do something extra for free, such as interrupting an effect in progress (6), unless they pay **fatigue** (5).
  - **1–2**: the enemy may do something extra.
- The result of a test is the highest value rolled on any die:
  - **6** – full success.
  - **4–5** – success with a penalty (the **GM** describes the consequences).
  - **1–3** – failure (the **GM** describes the consequences).
- Minimum number of dice: 1. Maximum: 6 (unless something allows otherwise).
- A **P** may roll **fewer** dice than their **trait** would allow, if they want to.
- Help from another **P**: +1 die, and the helper takes **fatigue**. Only **P** may help.
- Another attempt at the same action after a failure: costs **fatigue** (e.g. wrestling, picking a lock) — each additional attempt costs 1 more **fatigue**.
- The **GM** may modify a **P** roll (+/- dice) depending on the situation.
- If a test is **general** (e.g. perception in open terrain), it is made by at most **2 people**, to limit stat abuse.
- **Critical failure** (2 or more ones) — automatic failure, and the **P** gains a **splinter**.
- **Critical success** (2 sixes) — automatic success, and the **P** gains d6 **fate**.
- 1 six on the dice cancels out 2 ones, 2 sixes cancel out 3 ones, and so on — but the result is then treated as one tier lower.
  - If a **P** rolls, for example, 1, 1, and 6 -> partial success
  - If a **P** rolls, for example, 1, 1, and 6, 6 -> full success
- If a **P** has plenty of time and there is no pressure, they do not need to roll — they succeed automatically. Tests should be made when the stakes matter.
- Fate:
  - A pool of d6s that a **P** may use whenever they want to try to influence the narrative (in line with the logic of the fiction and in agreement with the GM, who has the final say).
  - For example, a **P** wants to make it so that what just happened to them or the party did not happen, reinforcements arrive, something happens differently from what the **GM** said, etc.
  - To succeed, at least one die from that pool (they may use as many as they want, and as many as they have) must come up 6.
  - Every **P** may try to alter the narrative, even several at once at the same moment, but before rolling they must declare how many **fate dice** they are using, and they cannot later spend additional ones on the same change if they failed before.
  - You cannot have more than 5 **fate dice**.
  - A **P** gains a **fate die** on a critical success or as a reward from the GM.
  - The **GM** has their own **fate dice**, which they may use narratively against the **P**, or to impose **disaadventages** on them, or to give factions **geopolitics points** — they gain them when a **P** would receive a **splinter** but already has 3.
- Advantages/disaadventages:
  - **Advantage**: +1 die to the roll; **Disaadventage**: –1 die to the roll.
  - They stack and cancel each other out (e.g. +2 and –1 = +1).
  - If a **disaadventage** would remove the last die, the **P** instead rolls 1 die, but the maximum result is reduced by 1 (scalable).
  - **Enemies**:
    - **Disaadventage**: when the enemy deals damage, subtract 1 die of the **P** failure.
      - If there is only 1 die — the enemy only changes the fiction (see the _Combat_ chapter).
    - **Advantage**: the enemy adds 1 failure die to the pool accumulated by the **P**, treated like the others (it may be reduced by armor).
- Destiny points:
  - Everyone has 1 **destiny point** at the start of the game. If you die, you may miraculously survive by spending such a point. You then lie unconscious, but stable. **Destiny points** can be bought with **advancement points**.
  - Losing a destiny point gives the **P** a permanent trauma connected to what almost killed them.
- Madness points:
  - The **GM** awards them for insane actions, especially ones that go against the character’s values.
  - Each **madness point** is **1d10** added to every test, except those in which the mad character is passive, such as **critical injuries**, falling, etc.
  - Results on madness dice have **higher priority** than results on normal d6s:
    - **1** = **critical failure**, regardless of other results.
    - **10** = **critical success**, regardless of other results (unless a 1 was rolled on another madness die).
  - If you have 2 **madness points** and roll both 1 and 10, you always get a **critical failure**.
  - When you have **3 madness points**, your character **loses their mind** — they pass under the GM’s control.
  - They can be treated by a barber-surgeon — 1 downtime action for 1 point.
- If there is no other way to determine the outcome of some unexpected or undefined situation — for example, the **P** send a messenger into dangerous territory and you want to see whether they arrived, or you need to determine whether an NPC noticed some detail — the **GM** rolls d6: 1–3: negative result, 4–6: positive result.
- For the **GM** — do not worry if you forget some rule; it happens to everyone. Just try to use it next time, that is all — in the heat of play, the most important thing is that everyone at the table is having fun and that the narrative holds their attention.
- For the **GM** — if you are not sure which **trait** to choose for a roll, or how to resolve a situation: ask the **P** and decide together.
- For the **GM** — if a **P** is testing something that matters but is not crucial, for example whether the **P** noticed a message carved into a tree that may point them in some direction — a partial success has no consequences, but on a full success you may reveal something more, for example: it seems to you that NPC X said something similar earlier.

## Traits

- A **P** has a set level in each **trait** — from 1 to 4 — and rolls that many dice when making related tests.
- You can have at most 2 **traits** developed to level 4, and 6 developed to level 3.

### Physical

- **melee combat** – striking, defending, reading an opponent
- **aiming** – shooting a bow, throwing a spear, hitting a target at range
- **resilience** – enduring pain, poison, hunger, fatigue
- **athletics** – climbing, pushing, pulling, jumping, overcoming resistance
- **agility** – balance, avoiding blows, acrobatic maneuvers
- **sleight of hand** – stealing, handling small objects, disarming traps, picking locks
- **stealth** – moving silently, hiding, ambushes

### Mental

- **arcana** – understanding and using magic, alchemy, handling artifacts
- **mind** – deduction, analysis, memory, knowledge of history or science, general knowledge
- **investigation** – spotting details, examining a crime scene, discovering traps
- **nature** – identifying plants and animals, tracks, navigating terrain, nature magic
- **religion** – rituals, symbols, recognizing cults and dogmas, divine magic
- **willpower** – resisting fear, mental pressure, mental resilience

### Social

- **intuition** – reading emotions, sensing lies, reading the mood
- **deception** – lying, pretending, creating false identities
- **intimidation** – threats, pressure, physical or verbal dominance
- **performance** – speeches, singing, gestures, playing instruments, holding a crowd’s attention
- **persuasion** – negotiation, convincing others, defusing conflict
- **influence** – connections, pushing events forward, asking around for information

### Specializations

- **perception** – noticing hidden things, sounds, movement, sensory awareness
- **medicine** – dressing wounds, diagnosing illness, stabilizing the injured
- **animal handling** – taming, calming, training, giving commands
- **survival** – tracking, making fire, building shelter, finding the way, exploration
- **technology** – repairing mechanisms, opening locks, constructing tools
- **herbalism** – making mixtures, identifying and gathering herbs, poisons

## Clocks and Building Tension

#### Clocks — what are they?

- Clocks are the main narrative and mechanical indicator of the **P** progress and, at the same time, the world’s response to their actions. They can measure both small and large events — from mistakes an NPC might forgive, to the amount of corruption needed for a sick person to turn into a demon, to the moment a faction makes its move.
- Clocks are simply a tracker of consequences — both positive and negative. They are used to measure their progress, for example:
  - how many people the Ps must convince to change their minds in order to truly affect the social situation.
  - how many failures the Ps can suffer before someone realizes they broke into the judge’s house.
  - how many weak points of the boss the Ps have wounded.
  - how close the bounty hunters are to finding the Ps who have a warrant out for them.
  - how many rests the Ps can take in the dungeon before the beast finds them.
  - how many successes the Ps must gather to escape a trap in time.
  - how many influential figures they must convince to pass a new law

#### How clocks work

- A clock can have as many segments as needed. Usually no fewer than 4 and no more than 8. The **GM** decides.
- The **GM** decides by how many segments the **P** actions / the situation advances the clock — usually 1, unless the circumstances are exceptional or you are using jump clocks (see below).
- What exactly advances the clock also depends on the GM, for example:
  - the result of a roll
  - a specific event / action
  - the passage of time
- The **GM** creates a clock whenever a consequence should not happen immediately, but instead build up gradually.
- The **GM** decides whether a clock is visible or not. It can also be semi-visible — the **P** do not know how many stages it has, but the **GM** informs them every time it advances it.
- Filling a clock means a specific and irreversible consequence / change in the world — the guards discover the **P**, the ritual ends, the enemy exposes a weak point, etc.
- Jump clocks — an alternative for raising tension even further — each time the clock is about to advance, roll a number of d6 equal to the number of remaining segments in the clock and advance it once for every 1–3 rolled on those dice, or 1–2 if you want it to advance more slowly.
  - Useful in situations that are not fully predictable, such as:
    - an enemy is searching for the **P**
    - the spread of a disease
    - rumors in the city
  - **Narrative sequence**, the main story tool, works on the basis of jump clocks (see _Other Mechanics_)

#### Example clock mechanics

##### Immediate pressure

- each new round brings **fatigue**.
- at the start of each round, the **GM** rolls d6 — if it comes up 1, time runs out.
- in every round, the boss’s base damage increases by 1.
- the **P** may take only as many actions before being caught as the number of successes they gained on the roll.
- Each failure advances the clock by 2, each partial success by 1.
- if the **P** do not score 3 full successes within 5 rounds, they fail to escape the collapsing tower.

##### Clocks in combat

- the **P** are protecting someone — if that person is hit by X damage dice, they die.
- if the enemies set 4 houses on fire, the entire village the **P** promised to save will burn down.
- each round without solving the problem causes the **GM** to roll d6 — on a 4+, a new enemy appears.
- the boss cannot be killed until the clock is filled — its weak point must be exposed.
- the **P** must destroy the ritual totems before the clock fills — otherwise the enemy will be reborn.
- Each round advances the clock — when it fills, reinforcements arrive.
- the more hits the boss takes, the stronger its final form becomes.
- the boss has a devastating attack — every failure (1–3) on the dice in a P’s roll advances the clock toward it going off by 1.

##### Social clocks

- the number of objectionable behaviors such as insults, disrespect, or violating values before the community turns hostile.
- the number of successes gathered while arguing one’s case needed to gain the king’s trust.
- How many times you can lie before someone starts checking up on you.
- how many questions I can ask before those present see through my disguise.
- how many messages that violate the alliance can reach the neighbor before the cooperation collapses.
- how many failures in diplomacy will ruin the plan.

##### Passage of time

- after three in-game hours, whose passing is accelerated by short rests, night falls and the **P** may discover a secret visible only in moonlight.
- how many scenes the **P** may complete before it is too late to reach the site of important events.
- how many long rests will cause the **P** to fail to reach the volcanic eruption in time.
- how many loud events the **P** must leave behind before the bounty hunters find them.
- how many units the **P** must recruit before they are ready to retake the temple from the invaders.

##### During exploration

- how many actions the **P** may take in a magic-corrupted area before it begins to affect them.
- how many **survival** tests they may fail before getting hopelessly lost.
- each rest in the desert restores one less **fatigue**.
- how many successes the **P** must gain to survive the night in the wilderness without consequences.
- how close they are to discovering the secret path — each full success advances the clock.
- how many loud noises may happen in the ruins before they awaken something that should not wake.
- how many tracks the **P** must gather to locate the hidden entrance to the underground.
- how many times the **P** may use the same makeshift crossing before it collapses into the river.
- how many rounds the **P** may spend disarming a trap before the mechanism activates.

## HP, Inventory, and Fatigue

### **Slots**

- Every **P** has **10 shared slots** for **HP**, **equipment**, and **fatigue**. The number of slots represents both the P’s carrying capacity and their HP.
- HP refreshes after a scene, if the circumstances allow it — every 4 restored HP turns into 1 **fatigue**.
- Each **fatigue** takes up 1 slot — reducing both HP and carrying capacity (you must discard something if there is no room). HP and carrying capacity covered by **fatigue** do not refresh until that **fatigue** is removed.

---

### **Fatigue**

- If you have **6+ fatigue**: you suffer **disaadventage**.
- **10 fatigue**: you fall unconscious and receive:
  - **+1 madness point**
  - **permanent trauma** (determined by the GM, e.g. fear, hypersensitivity).
- You may take 2 **fatigue** to add 1 die to a roll once. In that case, the **fatigue** is assigned **after the roll**.
- Sources of fatigue: prolonged exertion, critical injuries, extreme conditions, magical effects, the cost of partial successes, etc.
- Enemies cannot have **fatigue** — if they would ever receive it, they instead take 1 **inevitable damage**.
- If you rest and return to a place where **fatigue** forced you to discard equipment, you may collect it again — other **P** may also pick up your items if they have room in their packs — but it has to make sense; for example, if the party lost items in a fight, then covered their tracks and went off to rest somewhere farther away, they have no reason to return.
- **Fatigue** cannot cause you to lose an item in an illogical way, for example you will not throw off chainmail while running in a chase — when **fatigue** covers the equipment slots occupied by such items, those items remain in place — but those slots can only be covered last.

---

### **Rest**

- **Short (1h)**: –1 **fatigue**.
- **Long (8h)**: –5 **fatigue**
  - In a dangerous place: a group **survival** test, where failure means removing 2 less **fatigue**.
  - If a **P** crafts an item during the rest, they remove 2 less **fatigue** and cannot use a healing item.
- **Discomfort**: desperate hunger, extreme cold, being soaked, etc. prevent rest. Each morning spent with discomfort: +1 **fatigue**.
- After 24h without a long rest, Ps gain **fatigue**, and after 48h they gain a **mental status**.
- During longer time skips, for example when some activity takes the **P** two weeks and you fast-forward the narrative, **P** may take at most 1 long rest per week, and short rests do not apply in that case.

---

### **Inventory**

- **Small/light item**: ½ slot; **Standard**: 1 slot; **Large/heavy** (e.g. a two-handed weapon, a large shield): 2 slots
- ½ slots are used only to track items; a **P** can never lose less than 1 slot of HP/**fatigue**.
- **Small things** (berries, coins): take up no space. If there are many small things, for example a sack of berries, the space they take increases proportionally.
- **Ammunition / kits (e.g. lockpicks)**: 20 pieces = 1 unit; the **P** tracks usage.
- **Ranged weapon**: takes up space together with its ammunition (it is not counted separately).
- **Food rations**:
  - **Large**: 1 slot, enough for 3 days.
  - **Small**: ½ slot, enough for 1 day.
  - If the **P** are not in a settlement and are not using rented rooms, they must eat a food ration each day and remove it from their equipment; otherwise they gain **discomfort** from hunger.
- **Arcana**: take up no slots and cannot be lost.
- During the cold season, **P** must acquire warm clothes (a standard item, taking up one slot), or they suffer **disaadventage** on all rolls.

---

### **Upgrading Items**

- Items can be upgradedfate die up to 2 times using **exotic materials** (e.g. hydra scales = +1 magical armor, basilisk venom = a melee weapon inflicts a **condition** (choose / roll which one) on a 5 on the main die).
- An upgrade is done at a **market** as part of the party’s **wealth level**.
- In addition to the **exotic material**, 1 standard **material** is required for the upgrade.
- Upgrades on a given item cannot be swapped out or removed.
- **Arcana** can be upgraded.
- You cannot have more than 4 upgrades total (unless the **P** bought that option with **advancement points**) — if that happens, the **P** chooses which ones function and which do not.
- The **P** says what their upgraded item looks like.

## Statuses

- If a **P** would receive the same status again, roll a different one. If you roll one the **P** already has again, a catastrophe occurs — instead of a status, the **P** suffers permanent damage — roll twice on the **Scar Table** and choose the worse result.
- **Statuses** are the main tool that allows you to **meaningfully weaken P** beyond damage alone.
- **Fatigue** and **statuses** are the **only lasting effects** that impact players. Everything else should lead to them or reinforce them.
- **P** should **fear statuses**. They are **common**, **long-lasting**, and **hard to remove**.
- When used well, statuses build the **feeling of a brutal, pseudo-realistic world** in which **failure has consequences**, and every decision can carry real costs.
- Enemies cannot have **statuses** — if they would ever receive one, they instead take 3 inevitable damage. They can, however, have **conditions**.

### Physical

1. **WEAKENED** - **disaadventage** on rolls for physical **traits**.
2. **WOUNDED** - whenever you get a partial success or a failure on the dice, you gain 1 **fatigue**.
3. **TORN OPEN** – your wounds are so deep that each damage die deals +1 damage to you.
4. **ACHING** – each failure on a test for physical **traits** costs you one critical injury die.
5. **SHOCKED** - you cannot benefit from armor.
6. **CUT OPEN** - each 1 rolled on the dice lowers the highest result in the pool by 2 - dice changed this way do not count toward criticals.

### Mental

1. **FRAGILE** – doubles **fatigue** received.
2. **NERVOUS** – **disaadventage** on rolls for social **traits** and **specializations**.
3. **HALLUCINATING** – you cannot achieve full successes.
4. **DISTRACTED** – **disaadventage** on rolls for mental **traits**.
5. **BROKEN** - you cannot roll more than two dice - this applies to all tests.
6. **UNSURE** - a result of 4 on the dice counts as a failure.

## Splinters

- When the **GM** does not know how, or does not want, to trigger an immediate narrative consequence against a **P** on their partial success or failure, they may give that **P** a **splinter**.
  - The **GM** should not rely on splinters in moments of major narrative weight.
- A critical failure (two ones) inflicts a splinter.
- **Max 3 splinters** per character. Players track and record them themselves.
  - If a **P** would receive a **splinter** but already has 3, the **GM** gains a **fate die**.
- A **splinter triggers** when a **P** rolls **1 on the main die** of a test (16%) — once triggered, it disappears.
- Effect: **reduce the result by 1 tier** (full → partial, partial → failure).
- A **splinter** never triggers on failures.
- A **splinter** never triggers when rolling 1 die.
- **Splinters** (all at once) can be removed by:
  - a worthy sacrifice offered to the gods at an altar — something unusual or valuable.
  - spending part of a long rest on prayer or meditation + a **willpower/religion** test:
    - full success - remove all **splinters**.
    - partial success - remove one **splinter**.
  - a donation to the church (**treasure** for each **splinter**).
  - a **downtime action**.
- When a **splinter** triggers, the **P** describes what got in the way of their action.
- Splinters persist between scenes.
- Narratively, a splinter is a kind of deep stress written into the body — invisible and constantly threatening collapse, yet only giving way at an unexpected moment of greatest weakness.

## Identities

**Identity** is a short label describing a character’s dominant style of action, for example:  
_Silent_, _Acrobat_, _Berserker_, _Defender_, _Necromancer_, _Pyromancer_, _Seducer_, _Inventor_, _Official_, _Flying_, _Walking on Walls_.

An identity does not have to be defined in 1 word.

An identity is not a list of abilities or powers.  
It does not grant new actions. It does not change damage, statuses, or the core rules of the system.

An identity affects only how a **P** may try to change the fiction and how likely that is to work.

A character may have at most 6 identities.

### Identity and fiction

Purchased identities say who you are in the fiction. Are you an _Assassin_? Everyone understands that you can move quietly. Do you have no identity suggesting that you know how to hide? A description of unnoticed action will not be accepted, or the roll will be burdened with **disaadventages**.

Do you not have the identity of someone exceptionally strong? No one will believe you can move a boulder blocking a passage — you will have to test with **disaadventages**.

Do you have the identity of an _Inventor_? Everyone will believe it would be natural for you to quickly construct something small that solves a non-key problem, like a lever to pry open a hatch with no room to get your fingers under it.

Identities are small social agreements between everyone at the table. Through them, you commit to playing your character in line with both all the possibilities and all the limitations they impose on you. This applies not only to declared actions, but also to roleplaying the character.

Identities do not have rigid boundaries, so the **GM** always has the final say on what they truly allow in the fiction. For example, the **GM** may say no to an _Inventor_ who claims they have an item that will disarm the trap protecting a door.

An identity can never be a loophole for bypassing key obstacles — such as a trap on a door, the lack of an easy route onto a rocky ledge, or the stance of a key NPC in a given matter.

### Supernatural identities

- Every supernatural identity requires a sensible narrative justification accepted by the GM.
- Choosing any supernatural identity immediately triggers the restrictions of the supernatural:
  - a **P** may have at most one **trait** developed to level 4 (you cannot buy a supernatural identity if you already have two **traits** at level 4),
  - all future identities purchased (except for the allowed second supernatural one from a different category) must be natural.
  - A strong supernatural identity counts toward the overall limit of 6 identities and reduces that limit by 1, to 5.
- You may have only 1 strong supernatural identity and 1 weaker supernatural one.
- A strong supernatural identity costs twice as much as a natural identity.
- The distinction between a strong and a weaker supernatural identity lies in the scope it covers (for example, _Druid_ can manipulate nature in many ways, while _Telepath_ can only communicate through thought; creating your copy and acting from two places at once, or shapeshifting, are strong effects, but speaking with animals or foreseeing simple omens are not).
- Weaker supernatural identities may be extensions of stronger ones — for example, a strong one allows you to create one clone of yourself, a weaker one allows a second, or a mage controlling one element begins controlling another.
- Avoid overly broad supernatural identities — for example, _Mage_, who can do everything — go for something more like _Illusionist_, _Necromancer_, etc. — rather _Aeromancer_ (controlling air) than _Master of the Elements_.

### Scope of identities

An identity must be narrow and specific. It describes one dominant mode of action, not a full character archetype. It may include several similar effects, but all of them must follow the same logic and style. It should describe a style of action, social position, profession, a lasting unusual trait of the character, or something similar.

An identity cannot:

- cover many roles at once,
- combine combat, magic, and social influence in one label,
- replace a character class or its full set of competencies.

If an identity answers more than one of the questions below, it is too broad and must be split:

- How do you fight?
- How do you act outside combat?
- How do you influence people?
- Is it supernatural?

Each identity should clearly indicate what kind of advantage it gives in the fiction (for example intimidation, space control, mobility, concealment, resilience, social influence). This is not a strict mechanic or the only possibility, just a narrative direction.

Examples:

Too broad:

- _Paladin_ → member of an order + warrior + authority + supernatural
- _Monk_ → mobile + fights unarmed + mystical + unbreakable will
- _Mage_ → can basically do everything

Correct:

- _Terrifying_ → forces submission, fear, withdrawal
- _Defender_ → takes danger onto themselves, shields others
- _Inventor_ → creates tools that solve problems
- _Heavily Armored_ → can withstand many blows, holds position
- _Commander_ → coordinates the actions of others
- _Druid_ → _manipulates plants and animals in a supernatural way_ (a specific niche instead of plants + animals + elements + transformations + communication + healing, etc.)

Two identities of the same **P** cannot grant access to the same kind of advantage in the fiction in a similar way, for example _mobile_ and _fast_.

When creating an identity, a **P** must be able to clearly say in what situation that identity clearly does not work. If they cannot do that, the identity is too broad and requires clarification.

**P** should also make sure that the scopes of their identities do not overlap too much, so they do not steal scenes from one another.

Examples of fully developed characters:

Natural:

- _Silent_ → avoids detection, knows how to hide
- _Acrobat_ → changes position, bypasses obstacles
- _Burglar_ → handles locks and traps
- _Observer_ → notices details and threats
- _Actor_ → can impersonate others, hard to recognize
- _Liar_ → can make others believe the wildest nonsense

Supernatural:

- _Shapeshifter_ → strong supernatural → changes body, gains the physical capabilities of the form
- _Fortune Teller_ → weaker supernatural → reads signs and predicts dangers
- _Hunter_ → tracks targets, finds and covers tracks
- _Alert_ → always ready to react, cannot be surprised by either another person or a trap
- _Perfect Sense of Smell_ → can pick up signals unavailable to others through scent

### Activating identities

If you act clearly and consistently in line with your identity, building an advantage in the fiction through it, you may activate it before making a test. The activation must follow from the description declaring the action.

Activation costs:

- **1 fatigue** the first time that identity is used in a given scene,
- Each further use of the same identity in the same scene costs **1 more fatigue**.
- The cost is tracked separately for each identity.

Activation does not grant any mechanical bonuses, but it allows you to change the fiction in a way available only to that identity: Want to take an enemy’s damage onto yourself? You must be a _Defender_ (or someone similar).

An action that anyone can do does not require activating any identity. Anyone can, for example, shove someone.

Exceptions — if the situation truly favors a given identity, a **P** may argue that its activation should give them **advantage** — but identity is not an **advantage** factory, and such situations must be clear and rare.

You may activate more than one identity in the same turn if they do not exclude one another, for example someone silent and someone heavily armed. Each activated identity costs **fatigue** separately. The tested **trait** always follows from the declared action, not from the identity.

If a supernatural identity **does not involve casting a spell**, it does not require using the **MAGIC** move in combat — for example, a _Shapeshifter_ in beast form may perform ordinary **moves** (for example **VIOLENCE**, **RISK**, **DEFENSE**, **PRESSURE**) using the capabilities of their new form — for details see the _Combat_ chapter.

Identities also allow **P** to look for their openings in the fiction, for example an _Official_ argues that they could order the scribes they have met to send a letter because of their position.

### Range and space

An identity does not change the range of action.

All actions requiring range follow the rules below:

- Actions requiring physical contact work only within the same zone.
- Ranged attacks work according to the weapon’s rules or within 2 zones if not stated otherwise.
- All other actions requiring an effect on a target (magic, control, physical manipulation, summoning, etc.) have a range of up to 2 zones.

If the target is farther away, the action is impossible unless another system rule clearly states otherwise (for example **RISK** in combat or spell parameters — see the _Magic_ chapter).

An identity does not extend range.

### Scope and limits

An identity determines the kinds of effects a character can produce, but their scale always depends on the situation in the fiction.

The player declares intent, and the **GM** determines the realistic scope of the effect at that moment, taking into account available resources, surroundings, and the scale of the scene. If the declared effect exceeds the character’s capabilities, the **GM** may:

- limit its scale,
- ask for a change,
- or require **RISK**.

Identities do not allow you to bypass costs or create effects larger than the fiction justifies.

Magical effects never eliminate enemies instantly.  
Declarations such as _I implode his body_, _I turn him to stone_ or _I take away his breath_ either:

- work only narratively and enemies may defend themselves against their effects,
- or mechanically inflict a **wound** (and in that case may indeed eliminate easy/standard enemies.

The scope of cast spells has its own rules (see the _Magic_ chapter) and is built using the **Spell Parameters Table**.

Identities that allow different forms, for example _shapeshifter_, have a concrete limit on how many such forms a **P** may use, equal to the level of the **trait** assigned to that identity.

#### Examples:

Necromancer: _I raise a wall of bones._  
GM: _You have two fresh bodies. You can create a barricade the width of a doorway. You do not have the resources for more._

Aeromancer: _I summon a great tornado._  
GM: _You are able to create a vortex the size of a tree. When you try to maintain the power longer to enlarge it, you feel that a wave of corruption is about to flood your body and you will die, so you let it go._

Druid: _I trap all the enemies in cages of vines growing from the ground._  
GM: _Each additional target beyond the first costs you 1 success from your roll._

### Identities and testing traits

Using identities is usually accompanied by **trait** tests. The tested **trait** usually follows from the description of the P’s action, for example an _Assassin_ leaping between shadows and slipping past guards will test **stealth**.

Spells are usually cast with **arcana**, **nature**, or **religion**.

Some magical / supernatural effects fit other **traits** better than **arcana** or **nature**, however, for example a _Shapeshifter_ may use **survival**, and an Invisible character may use _stealth_.

The **trait** used for a specific supernatural identity should be determined when it is purchased between the **P** and the **GM** and can never be changed — for example a _Druid_ always tests their spells with **nature**, and a warrior character who can trigger supernatural effects with shouts always tests **willpower** when using them.

Some identity effects will have a number of uses / a duration. In that case, the number of successes on the dice +1 determines how many times the effect may be used. For example, when a _Shapeshifter_ transforms, they may perform as many actions (flight, attack, a specific task — depending on the form) in the new form as the number of successes (4–6 on the dice) they rolled on a **survival/nature** test (depending on which **trait** was set at purchase) before returning to their original form. In that case, no matter what the **P** does in combat while using an identity with a number of uses / duration, their next test is made with the **trait** that determines that number of uses / duration, and that same test also determines the result of their first action counted within that limit. Other examples of identities that may require a similar solution: a monk who maintains a great spiritual figure growing from within them through **willpower**; a time manipulator; a berserker in a frenzy.

The **fatigue** cost is paid only for the initiating roll for such an identity, not for every use it allows. A use granted by a similar identity does not guarantee success — if it involves risky action, a test of the appropriate **trait** must be made. Identities with a number of uses / duration may be used only once per scene.

### Dramaturgy

- When a natural **P** develops a second **trait** to level 4, they may from then on exchange every 2 **fate dice** for 1 point of **Dramaturgy**, which allows them to make an additional change to the fiction in a given scene on demand without spending an action or move (in addition) and without cost, for example _I tear that item out of his hands_, _I climb the wall_, _I pass unnoticed_, _I force the door open_.
- Such an effect applies only to the acting **P**, may include only one additional action, must make sense, have range, be natural, and be relatively realistic.
- A Dramaturgy effect cannot bypass obvious risk → a **P** cannot say: _I disarm the trap_ or _I humiliate the king_ to avoid consequences.
- You may have at most 2 such points at once.
- In the _narrative mechanic_, only 1 **P** may use only 1 such point.

### Acting outside identities

If a character acts in a way clearly contrary to all identities they possess (and still plausible), the **GM** may impose **disaadventage** — for example, someone without an identity that allows them to hide tries to vanish from the enemies’ sight in plain view.

Not every action requires an identity. **Disaadventage** appears only when the action is in obvious contradiction with the character’s declared style.

### Notes

An identity does not grant a constant advantage.  
It only allows acting in the appropriate style by burning resources.

The more often you use the same identity in a scene, the higher the cost.  
**Fatigue** remains the main currency of risk.

Identity defines the way a character acts, not their list of abilities.

## Combat

### General Rules

- Combat lasts 3 rounds. A round consists of one turn for each P.
- To win, the **P** must advance the **victory clock** by 3 × the number of players.
  - Each round, at its end, one **P** may choose to take a **catastrophe** upon themselves (see below) to reduce the number of victory clock segments required to win by 1.
  - Combat may end earlier if the **P** kill all enemies, fall themselves, or decide to flee.
- **P** do not deal damage, they advance the **victory clock**.
- There is no enemy clock — the opponents deal damage to the **P** and may kill them before they can win.
  - If a **P** HP falls below 0, they roll for **critical injuries**.
- Before combat begins, you establish what the conflict is about and the clear consequences of victory and defeat.
  - Usually, a fight should have an objective beyond slaughter itself — for example protecting an NPC, creating an escape route, reaching a relic first, etc.
    - Fulfilling the objective of the fight may grant additional clock movement — for example +1 to the clock for every round in which an important NPC took no damage — the **GM** decides.
    - Sometimes the **GM** may extend combat by additional turns if its objective / outcome is narratively important / the Ps somehow secured that / there is no other way out — in that case, the **victory clock** threshold required to win increases by the number of Ps for each additional turn.
  - If the fight is, however, about wiping out the other side, then after winning the Ps may describe how they kill or capture their enemies, though those enemies may still try to flee.
- There is no initiative — the **P** always go first, the first to act is whoever has an idea, and then according to the rhythm of the clock — the order never changes unless the fiction forces it. If the **P** are surprised, they suffer **disaadventage** on all rolls on their first turn.
- This is a narrative procedure reflecting pressure. Everything is based on the fiction and resolved within it. Actions do not have to be mirrored directly in mechanics, but they are part of the story that builds the scene and truly affects what either side can do — if you blocked a door, no one can pass through it until they move you or you let them through.
  - Usually on your turn you may move and do one thing — in any order.
  - You declare your action with a specific intent by describing what you do — you do not use individual attacks, powers, etc., but tell the story of what exactly your character does, like in a film (within the logic and limits of the fiction) — for example _I run under the beast’s tail toward the NPC and grab their clothes_ (action), _so that no one can throw them into the chasm_ (intent) — only after such a declaration does the **GM** choose the appropriate **move** for it (see below), which works mechanically — for example the beast was not looking your way right now, so running past it was not that risky, but it was still dangerous, so that is **PRESSURE** → the **P** rolls the appropriate **trait**, in this case athletics, and succeeds → the mechanics of the **PRESSURE** move apply, along with a change in the fiction → now no one can push the NPC toward the chasm until they deal with the fact that the **P** is securing them.
  - A change in the fiction is a new circumstance that must be taken into account in all later declarations by all sides — if it affects what they are doing.
  - If the intent of the action is unclear, the **GM** asks about it or steers the narration appropriately.
  - Imagine that combat is not an exchange of hit points, but the shared telling of a very fast scene that keeps changing. A “change in the fiction” is simply a new situation in that scene that becomes true, and from that moment on everyone must take it into account. If someone flipped a table, the table is now lying there and blocking the view. If someone got shoved against a wall, they are really there against the wall and cannot pretend to be somewhere else. If an enemy slashed you with a sword and forced you a step back, there is now distance between you and you must say how you close it before you can hit them again. This is not a penalty or bonus from a table — it is just the world changed by your actions. The mechanics (clock, damage, **disaadventage**) only determine how well something went, but the scene itself keeps living and the next decisions must fit it. If something happened in the description, it exists — and from then on every move must account for that new reality.
- Declarations of changes in the fiction are resolved through special **moves**.
  - A **P** usually makes one **move** on their turn.
  - Types of **moves**:
    - **VIOLENCE** → direct confrontation
      - Partial success: you advance the clock by 1, but failures (1–3) hit you as damage.
      - Full success: same as on a partial success + inflict a wound
        - enemies: **easy** and **standard** die after one wound, **strong** after 2, **powerful** after 3.
        - escalation → if in one turn a wound was inflicted and another at least partial **VIOLENCE** success occurred in any order, advance the clock by 2.
      - If the **VIOLENCE** action has no clear intent in the description, or the intent is only to inflict a wound, it is assumed that the attack **opens up** the enemy → the first next action against that enemy may introduce an additional change in the fiction (for example not only a shove, but a shove and a knockdown), if it is logical. Once used, the effect disappears.
    - **RISK** → a hazard for greater gain with a chance of a greater cost, for example you rush into a zone where enemies outnumber you, into fire, take attacks on yourself, act while hidden
      - Partial success: you advance the victory clock by 2, but failures (1–3) hit you as **inevitable damage**.
      - Full success: same as on a partial success, but the damage resulting from failed dice is not inevitable and may be blocked by armor.
    - **DEFENSE** → protection and securing → for example I cover myself or someone in the same zone with my shield
      - Partial success: you do not advance the clock, but only ones on the dice deal damage to the person you defend, though increased by 1 for each die.
      - Full success: same as on a partial success, but you advance the clock by 1 instead of 0.
    - **PRESSURE** → an action not aimed strictly at the enemy, for example using an item, tearing away a shield, pinning down an opponent
      - Partial success: you advance the clock by 1, but failures (1–2) hit you as damage.
      - Full success: same as on a partial success + choose one:
        - Cancel all damage from this action,
        - The enemy cannot change the fiction in this reaction to your action.
      - It also works as setting up an action — for example _I shove away anyone who comes close_.
    - **SUPPORT** → you help an ally
      - Partial success: you do not advance the clock and failures (1–2) hit you as damage, but you give your ally one of the following:
        - On their next turn they have **advantage**, and if they get a full success, they advance the clock by an additional 2
        - You remove 2 **fatigue** from them.
      - Full success: same as on a partial success + cancel all damage from this action.
    - **MAGIC** → breaking the rules of reality using spells (see the _Magic_ chapter)
      - Partial success: you do not advance the clock, but you change the fiction in a way unavailable to others; failures (1–3) hit you as damage.
      - Full success: same as on a partial success + you advance the clock by 2 instead of 0.
      - Spells can never manipulate the clock on a meta level — for example I cast a spell to move the clock.
  - Failure always means: unsuccessful impact on the fiction, no advance of the **victory clock**, damage to the **P** for each 1–3 on the dice, and the clock moves back by 1.
  - Critical failure always means -1 to the clock and rolling a **catastrophe**, while critical success means +1 to the clock (in addition to the movement from the **move** itself) and **advantage**.
    - When a **P** uses magic in combat, their critical failure triggers **catastrophe** and **corruption** at the same time (see the _Magic_ chapter) — only one of those effects may affect the **P** at the same moment — the **GM** decides which, and should follow what the fiction suggests.
  - **Advantage** — choose 1:
    - Inflict a wound.
    - Remove 1 fatigue from yourself.
    - Change the situation in the fiction for free right now, as if you had done one additional thing.
    - The first next activated enemy **power** does not activate.
- The following moves should create a shared story.
- A **move** follows from the dominant intent of the action, not from distance or obstacles.
  - _I charge up the stairs and leap-slash the knight with my sword to drive him out of the passage_ → that is **VIOLENCE**.
  - _I run up to the alchemist and untie the cord holding his elixirs so they fall to the ground and shatter_ → that is **PRESSURE**.
  - _I ignore the fire, I know I have to try to kill him before he escapes_ → that is **RISK**.
- If the described action is complicated or includes an obstacle, it is performed as **RISK** or with an appropriate number of **disaadventages** (the **P** decides) → the description includes climbing a slippery wall + an attack? → that is either **RISK** or **VIOLENCE** with **disaadventage**.
- Every **move** changes the fiction. However, only the main goal of the action causes the mechanical effect. Any additional elements of the description (always only one and never added afterward) enter the fiction only on a full success and do not generate automatic numerical bonuses unless they follow directly from the rules. Such an extra change in the fiction still introduces real consequences for the scene and for how the narrative unfolds in later stages of the fight.
  - Example: _I kick a stool so it hits the guard in the legs and throws him off balance, then I slash him_ -> Dominant intent? Hurt him, so **VIOLENCE**. The knockdown is the means, not the goal, and it happens only on a full success, and even if it happens it gives a mechanical advantage only after the turn ends, meaning from the start of the next P’s turn, who may exploit their favorable position over the fallen enemy to give themselves **advantage**.
- If a **P** decides only to move within the scene, because for example the goal is too far away, that still needs an intent, for example:
  - _I move closer to block his escape route._
  - _I circle him from the side to cut him off from the mage._
  - _I get under his bow so he cannot shoot._
  - _I stay glued to him to force him into melee._
- There are no enemy turns. They act only in reaction to the **P** moves.
- Combat is a fluid, shared construction of the scene’s image. Every player action must truly change something in the imagined situation — position, access, cover, threat, advantage. After the roll, the effect enters the shared narrative and becomes the new starting state. The enemy always reacts in the fiction (moves, separates, pushes back, shields, flees), unless a full **PRESSURE** success deprives them of that reaction; the reaction never negates the player’s effect, but further transforms the situation.
  - Example: a player shoves an opponent — that opponent falls, cuts them with a knife in the process, and drags a table down with them; now the table is an obstacle and cover, so to reach the enemy you must jump it, go around it, or move it, risking exposure to attack. The mechanics work only inside this changing scene — there are no “pure attacks,” only actions shaping the next decisions.
- If a **P** rolled a partial success and there are no failures on the dice that would deal damage to them, the enemy only changes the fiction, without dealing damage.
- Space → combat takes place in zones, which are nothing more than an approximate division of space such as part of a room up to the pillars and its far half, a high balcony, etc. There are no distances inside zones. In one zone, everyone can reach everyone else to attack them in melee.
  - You may move one zone on your turn and act normally — unless something states otherwise;
  - You may move two, but then your change in the fiction must result only from the movement itself, for example I cut off his path so he cannot get to the NPC;
  - You may also force an action requiring contact in a zone no farther than 2 away from you by using the **RISK** move for it, or by taking an appropriate number of **disaadventages**, depending on how complicated the task is.
  - Enemies follow the same rules, and additionally any enemy who did not react in a given round may, at the end of that round, move one zone without acting.
- **Fatigue** gained in combat cannot force you to discard a weapon or armor you are actively using — when **fatigue** covers the equipment slots occupied by those items, they remain in place — but those slots may only be covered last.
- Once on your turn, you may draw / switch an item / weapon for free.
- Using an item — for example drinking an elixir, activating arcana, or putting on / taking off a helmet — costs a move or an action as **PRESSURE**.
- **P** may always withdraw to flee, if that makes sense. Especially when they feel they will not win.
- If an effect is meant to be global (for example something tries to inflict fear on everyone in a zone) — the **P** with the highest fitting **trait** defends, but with **disaadventage**.
- **Ranged combat**:
  - Attacking with a ranged weapon (not a thrown one) has **disaadventage** if the fiction says its user is in close combat / has little space / is on horseback, etc.
  - The maximum range of ranged weapons is 2 zones, unless some rule overrides it. Magic with a ranged effect follows the same rules.
  - Thrown weapons have a range of 1 zone beyond the one the attacker is in (they cannot be thrown farther), but they do not suffer penalties from close combat / little space / horseback, etc.
    - Throwing a weapon not designed for throwing is always done with one d6.
- It is possible that in some matters the **GM** will have to improvise the rules — that is normal — in such a case they should always follow what serves the narrative best.

### Catastrophes

A **catastrophe** affects the **P** during whose turn it was activated — play it out narratively as an extra scene between the **P** turns.

| No. | Effect                                                                                     |
| --- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1   | Roll for **critical injuries**                                                             |
| 2   | The weapon you are using (even arcana) breaks down — it is ineffective until you repair it |
| 3   | **Physical** status                                                                        |
| 4   | **Mental** status                                                                          |
| 5   | **Scar**                                                                                   |
| 6   | You permanently lose 1 armor (even from arcana) (one you have not used yet) (this stacks)  |

### How to declare actions in combat

Do not say _what you are doing mechanically_.  
Say **what you want to change in the scene**.

Not:  
_I attack._  
Instead:  
_I want to push him away from the door._  
_I want to wound him so he stops pressing forward._  
_I want him to no longer be able to use that shield._  
_I want to give B an opening for a clean strike._

The mechanics will trigger on their own based on your **dominant intent**.

Every move changes the scene.  
After the roll, something in the world is different — position, access, threat, control, tempo.

If after your action the world looks the same as it did before, then your description did not make the intent clear enough. The **GM** will then ask: What is different now than a moment ago? And you must clarify the change in the fictional situation.

#### Examples:

##### “I leap onto the beast and tear out its fang so it cannot bite like that anymore.”

Intent: limit the threat → **PRESSURE**

What changes in the fiction?

- The beast loses one of its main advantages.
- Its attacks are less brutal.
- It may start retreating or go into a frenzy.
- It is wounded and furious.

This does not give +1.  
It changes how the **GM** describes its attacks from now on and what it can realistically do.

---

##### “I tear out the beast’s fang and drive it back into its throat.”

Intent: hurt it → **VIOLENCE**

Change in the fiction:

- The beast is badly wounded.
- It staggered back.
- Blood is flooding the floor, making it slippery.
- It may lunge desperately.

Here the mechanical effect is a wound.  
In the fiction, the beast is in a worse state.

---

##### “I jump onto the beast’s face, tear out its fang, and shield myself with it from a claw strike.”

Intent: survive a hazard → **RISK**

Change in the fiction:

- You are very close to it.
- If something goes wrong, you will get hit hard.
- If it works, you gain temporary dominance.

This is an aggressive, cinematic entry.

---

##### “I tear out its fang and throw it to B so he can use it as a weapon.”

Intent: create an opportunity → **SUPPORT**

Change in the fiction:

- B has a new opportunity.
- The beast is momentarily confused.
- The team’s dynamic changes.

### Running enemies as the GM

- Usually, the last roll a **P** makes on their turn should determine the enemy’s reaction — though **P** will rarely test more than once per turn.
- Enemies cannot have **fatigue** — if they would gain any, they instead suffer **disaadventage** or the **victory clock** increases by 1.
- Enemies do not have full stats — if you need to make a test for them (for example **agility**, **willpower**, **nature**), assume a default **trait** value depending on their power scale:
  - **Easy/minion** – **1 die**, 1 armor;
  - **Standard enemy** (for example a guard, thug, wolf) – **2 dice**, 2 armor;
  - **Strong enemy** (for example a veteran, gang leader, knight) – **3 dice**, 3 armor
  - **Powerful enemy/boss** (for example a demon, monster, master) – **4 dice**, 4 armor.
- You may give each enemy **special traits** (always active — for example 2 moves, flies, higher damage, immune to fire) and **powers** (activated by a result of 2–3 on the **main die**, at which point a **power** is chosen from the list — for example doubles the damage from that roll, knocks down everyone in the zone, destroys armor, removes a die from some **trait** until a visit to the barber-surgeon, etc. — enemies may of course also have **powers** available to **P**).
  - **Special traits** should be available only to **strong** and **powerful** enemies — max two.
  - The maximum number of **powers** should depend on the enemy’s strength: **easy/minion** – 0, **standard** – 1, **strong** – 2, **powerful** – 3.
- Example enemy:
  - **Hunger of Slaughter** - a **powerful** monster - Damage: 2 per die (teeth and claws) - armor piercing 2 | Armor: 4 | Special: 2 moves; every 4 of its damage that would move the **Enemy Clock** in one turn removes one wound from it | Powers: deals an additional 3 damage to everyone in the zone, or destroys all the target’s armor (before applying damage), or poisons the target, forcing them on their next turn to take a stabilizing action or lose consciousness.
  - Behavior: It resembles a skinless wolf. It has no eyes, only nostrils — it smells blood and fatigue. It moves in a shaky, spasmodic way, as if it cannot control its own body. It can imitate the voices of the victims it devoured. When someone dies near it, it trembles in ecstasy. It is the result of alchemical experiments.
- Enemies gathered in one place act together narratively — for example two archers from the same zone fire at a **P** in the next zone at the same time, even though mechanically they only use the damage dice from one **P** roll; or 3 knifemen cut into a **P** in a coordinated assault, supporting one another, even though mechanically they use the damage dice from one **P** roll.
- Some enemies form an organized group that moves and acts together — like a formation of armed soldiers, a crowd, or a swarm.
- Which enemy reacts:
  - If the result of an action happens in a specific zone → an enemy from that zone reacts.
  - If there are several enemies in that zone → the strongest of them reacts → if all of them have already reacted, the nearest ranged one reacts.
  - If the action does not concern a specific zone / the action’s target died and there is no one else in their zone → a ranged enemy reacts.
  - If a **catastrophe** triggered → the first next reaction always belongs to the strongest enemy.
  - Each enemy / group may react once per round → if a given enemy has already reacted: choose another according to this procedure (unless all have already reacted, in which case the cycle starts over) → the reaction limit of strong and powerful enemies resets when the **victory clock** is advanced by more than 1 in one turn.
- Who does the enemy attack?
  - If a **P** in its zone acted against it → it acts against that **P**.
  - If there are weakened targets in its zone (for example prone / lower armor) or something narratively important, the enemy may ignore the rule telling it to react against the **P** who acted and choose the optimal target.
  - If there is no **P** in its zone → it moves toward the nearest **P** and attacks the first one within range.
  - If several are within range → the **GM** chooses the most obvious target according to the current scene. If the choice is not obvious → it attacks the one with the least armor → if that still does not settle it: randomly.
  - After the **victory clock** is advanced by more than 1 in one turn, the enemy may ignore the above rules and choose any target (whatever best serves the narrative according to the GM).

### Fights against absurd threats and resolving battles

Supernatural beings, such as dream Nightmares or Giants, are as extremely rare as they are dangerous. Sometimes the **P** find themselves in a situation where they must or choose to face such a challenge beyond their strength. Whether winning such a conflict is possible, or whether only escape or buying time is realistic, depends on the GM, but every such case should be resolved as a **narrative sequence** or a **chase** (see _Full Rules - Other Mechanics_). Depending on the scale of the threat, the **GM** may also rule that all rolls in those story procedures suffer **disaadventage**.

The same should be done if the scale of the fight goes beyond a local clash and involves many sides / numerous units — in that case, it is worth looking at _Sequential Narrative on a Time Scale_.

## Damage

### Hit points and recovery

- HP = the ability to avoid harm (they are not physical wounds).
- After a scene, a **P** restores all HP from slots not covered by **fatigue**, but for every 4 HP restored they gain 1 **fatigue**.
- HP covered by **fatigue** does not recover.
- **Temporary HP**: lasts until the end of the scene; it does not recover.
- Losing HP below 0 = **critical injuries**.

---

### Special cases

- **Armor piercing X**:
  - **P** → X advances of the **victory clock** always go through; enemy armor may only block the rest.
  - Enemy X → failure dice always deal damage; a P’s armor may only block the rest.
- **Inevitable damage**: cannot be blocked in any way.

---

### Critical injuries

- Actual bodily damage.
- The **P** rolls their **critical injury dice**:
  - **6**: you lose 1 **critical injury die** and keep going with 1 HP.
  - **4–5**: you gain a **physical** and a **mental** **status**, but keep going with 1 HP.
  - **1–3**: you die (or lose a **destiny point** and collapse unconscious). You can only be saved by a 5–6 on a **medicine** test (or **fate dice**) until your next trun, and only one person may attempt it — if they succeed, and this happens in combat, you may act again with 1 HP.
  - Each time you suffer **critical injuries**, but your condition is stable, you may choose not to take part in the scene anymore — this lowers the party’s chance of success, but you will not suffer further harm.
- **Critical injury dice** return only after being treated with **oil/incense**, and only during a long rest.
- If a **P** would lose a **critical injury die**, but cannot because they have none left, they die.

---

#### Damage from falling

- **Low height** (up to the 1st floor):  
   → roll a **physical status**.
- **Great height** (2–3 floors):  
   → roll for **critical injuries**.
- **Very great height** (4+ floors):  
   → roll for **critical injuries** with **disaadventage**.

---

### Damage types

- Physical
- Magical / elemental

All of them may be blocked by armor.  
A single attack may deal only one type of damage.

---

### Resistance and vulnerability to a given damage type

**Damage resistance** works so that you may always discard 1 failure when determining the amount of damage tied to that type of damage.

**Damage vulnerability** works so that when determining the amount of damage tied to that type of damage, you are treated as if an additional failure had hit you. You may discard it normally with armor if nothing prevents that.

**Immunity** means complete immunity to a given type of damage, but it is extremely rare. An example would be immunity to poisons or diseases in dwarves.

---

### Scars

When a P’s HP drops to exactly zero, or they lose all **critical injury dice**, they gain a **scar** — roll on the **Scar Table**. You cannot gain more than 1 scar during an encounter.

The **P** says what their scar looks like.

**Scar Table - all effects stack - roll 1d6 - invent a scar on your body.**

| No. | Effect                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| --- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | Your bones have been weakened so much that you permanently lose 1 from every additional **victory clock** advance in combat that you manage to gain — unless you undergo rehabilitation under a barber-surgeon’s supervision — **downtime action**. |
| 2   | Your body has been crushed — you gain 1 permanent **fatigue** — unless you undergo rehabilitation under a barber-surgeon’s supervision — **downtime action**.                                                                                       |
| 3   | Your mangled body weakens you and you permanently lose a **critical injury die** — unless you undergo rehabilitation under a barber-surgeon’s supervision — **downtime action**.                                                                    |
| 4   | You should have been dead long ago — you gain a permanent **splinter** — you may treat it with a barber-surgeon — **downtime action**.                                                                                                              |
| 5   | You bear so many wounds that you finally learned something from your mistakes — you gain 2 **advancement points** — but you pay for it with madness — you gain a **madness point** — you may treat it with a barber-surgeon — **downtime action**.  |
| 6   | Thick scar tissue hardens your skin, giving you 1 permanent HP/fatigue/equipment slot (up to the limit of 14) or a **destiny point** — your choice.                                                                                                 |

## Weapons and Armor

### Weapons

#### Specialization

- There are 8 weapon types: **one-handed**, **two-handed**, **dual-wielding**, **bows**, **crossbows**, **thrown**, **firearms**, **martial arts**.
- A **P** starts with **2 specializations**, and may buy more with **advancement points**.
- **Without specialization**: you always attack with only 1 die.

---

#### Melee weapons

- **One-handed** (e.g. sword, mace):
  - You may hold a **shield**, **light source**, or **utility item** in the other hand.
  - Fighting **with two one-handed weapons**:
    - If your action is **RISK**, you can block 1 die of damage with armor regardless of the result.
    - If you act from hiding on a full success, you may advance the clock by an additional 1
- **Two-handed** (e.g. axe, hammer) - when acting 1 on 1 in a zone, you may advance the clock by an additional 1 on a full success.

---

#### Ranged weapons

- They can be used to attack into other **zones** - base range 2, unless stated otherwise.
- Shooting from a mount = **disaadventage**.

#### Bare hands and improvised weapons

- **Bare-handed**: always only 1 die in direct confrontation.
- **Improvised weapon** (broken bottle, broom handle, etc.): has no effects and gives **disaadventage**.

---

#### On-hit effects

- Sometimes a weapon may inflict special effects on a hit - they are activated by appropriate **main die** results (usually 4–5).

### Armor

#### Physical armor

- Armor is the number of failures a **P** may discard from their attack test, thereby reducing enemy physical damage. 1 armor blocks 1 failure die. An armor point used this way is spent for the scene.
- It blocks all material and external damage, including for example the effect of magical fire, but not **mental, internal, immaterial** damage, etc.
- **Limit**: 4 damage blocked per 1 point of armor.
- **Maximum 6 armor**.
- Enemy armor works differently: it does not block damage (because **P** do not deal damage) or wounds (which Ps may inflict with **VIOLENCE**), but rather clock movement beyond the base movement resulting from using a **move**, 1 to 1 → for example, a **P** uses **VIOLENCE** so they move the clock by 1 and have an additional 2 movement from that **move’s** escalation being activated → those additional movements may be blocked, 1 armor point for each, after which the enemy armor used that way is spent.
- Armor used to block damage / additional **victory clock** movement may only be used by the creature directly affected by the current action.

#### Magical armor

- Increases the limit of failures that can be blocked from 6 to **10**.
- Also works against **mental, internal, immaterial** damage, etc.
- Does not impose penalties on tests.
- Appears only in **arcana / exotic materials**.
- **Maximum 4 magical armor**.

#### Refreshing armor

- At the end of a scene, a **P** regains all armor.
- **Temporary armor**: lasts until the end of the scene (unless the effect states otherwise), then disappears.

#### Armor penalties

- **Agility**: limits tests related to **agility, stealth, sleight of hand**, as well as some **athletics** tests (e.g. swimming, climbing) and **magic/nature** tests requiring gestures. The **GM** decides according to logic.
- **Visibility**: limits **perception** and **aiming** tests.
- Penalty = **–1 to the highest die** in tests affected by the limitation (for each point of penalty).

## Magic

### Narrative assumptions

- Magic is a **rare, generally unwelcome, and sometimes illegal** force, known only to a few.
- Wizards are nearly extinct, and magic is for the most part rituals or isolated strange abilities, not spells.
- Magic is treated as something **disturbing and incomprehensible** — common knowledge about it is distorted, even among its users. Ordinary people know it exists, but they have no idea what it is or where it comes from — just as they know unimaginably wealthy people exist, but cannot say how they accumulate their vast fortunes.
- A **P** casting spells should **justify it narratively** and **hide their power** if possible.
- **Scrolls / runes / elixirs** are rare — you usually cannot buy them at a **market**, only find or create them.
- If someone can cast spells, what they can do is limited only by the supernatural **identity** they chose — within that scope, everything is allowed as long as it remains on a realistic scale and the **GM** does not need to rein it in.

### Spellcasting mechanics

- Every spell cast costs **1 fatigue**.
- Only **P** with purchased strong supernatural identities can cast spells. And only within their scope — for example, a _Necromancer_ can manipulate dead bodies and perhaps some kind of negative energy, but cannot create a wall of fire.
- When casting spells, you roll the linked **trait**: usually **arcana** or **nature**.
- **Religion** instead of **arcana / nature**:
  - Casting a spell does not cost **fatigue** and does not cause corruption, but it works only on a full success. On a partial success when casting with religion, the spell fails, but the enemy always reacts as if they had only 1 damage die.
- If you achieve at least a partial success, you gain 1 additional success to spend on spell parameters.
- In combat, casting spells is effectively always the same as using the **MAGIC** move.
- Enemies’ magical abilities trigger as their powers on a 2–3 on the **main die**.
- Your spells harm you and your allies just like anyone else — if you raise a wall of fire, you cannot stand in it safely.
- The **GM** may ask for a change or reject an unrealistic / excessive effect that does not fit the narrative — _see Identities - Scope and limits_.
- A spell should have one clear intent — for example _I release a vortex of air to knock down the shooter on the balcony_.
- If the spell’s intent does not directly interfere with the mechanics, it simply works if the **P** rolled at least a partial success.
- The intent of the spell declaration establishes what its mechanical effect will apply to before the roll — for example, you cannot declare healing and then change it into granting armor.
- The final effect of a spell is built from parameters expressed as success costs (4–6 on the dice) according to the **Spell Parameters Table** — the **GM** and **P** agree together on the spell’s final effect, though the **GM** always has the final say.
  - The **P** declares the general intent of the spell, which after the roll may be adjusted depending on how well the casting went, based on the number of successes they have to spend on spell parameters.

#### Spell Parameters Table

| parameter                                           | 0                               | 1                                                   | 2                                                                   | 3                                                                               | 4 + mental status                                                                                |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Scale**                                           | on yourself / 1 target          | 2 targets in one place / zone (if without a target) | 3 targets in one place / 2 zones (if without a target)              | 5 targets in one place or 3 in different places / 3 zones (if without a target) | the whole scene, everyone in the scene - cancels range                                           |
| **Duration**                                        | instant / flash / 1 use         | a longer moment (2 rounds) / 2 uses                 | scene / 3 uses                                                      | one hour of real time / 4 uses                                                  | whole session                                                                                    |
| **Range**                                           | close (up to 2 zones)           | nearby (up to 3 zones)                              | far (up to 4 zones)                                                 | very far (up to 5 zones)                                                        | within sight                                                                                     |
| **Complexity**                                      | a minor manipulation of reality | a simple magical effect (gust, roots, flame)        | a strong effect requiring control (flight, invisibility, lightning) | radical manipulation of reality (teleportation, passing through a wall)         | absurdities (I summon a giant, an earthquake crushes the whole building, I turn him into a frog) |
| Mechanical effect - cancels duration and complexity | -                               | HP                                                  | armor, **fatigue**, movement / shifting between zones               | **status**, **splinter**, an effect lasting over time - like poisoning          | wound                                                                                            |

- The **GM** always has the final say on how complex a spell effect is.
- Each parameter other than the mechanical effect may be purchased only once.
- If a spell is meant to cause a direct mechanical effect (HP, wound, armor, fatigue, status, etc.), use the **Mechanical effect** row from the table and ignore **Duration** entirely (all mechanical effects work immediately and permanently) and **Complexity** (it is expressed by the cost of the mechanical effect).
- Duration is divided into periods and uses — uses serve effects such as shapeshifting — for example, a _Shapeshifter_ rolls **survival** to determine how many actions they may perform in the new form before changing back, but it may also be used as an alternative to time-based effects, for example a spellcaster grants themselves flight and prefers to say they may use it up to 2 times (no matter when), instead of having unlimited uses over a longer moment.
- Area spells always affect only a single zone. You cannot spend successes to increase their area. For example, a vortex released into a zone with a balcony can never knock over anyone outside it.
- Examples of spells:
  - _Roots grow from the ground and wrap around the guards_ — 3 successes — Scale 1 (2 targets), Duration 1 (2 rounds), Range 0 (2 zones), Complexity 1 (roots) — 2 armed enemies in the zone become immobilized.
  - _I rise into the air and fly to the rocky ledge_ — 4 successes — Scale 0 (on yourself), Duration 2 (scene), Complexity 2, range does not matter — you can fly for the whole scene.
  - _Lightning strikes the monster from the sky to stun it and, if possible, wound it_ — 4 successes — Scale 0 (1 target), Range 0 (2 zones), Mechanical effect 4 (wound) — the target suffers a wound.
  - _A veil of mist fills the street so we can hide our machine_ — 3 successes — Scale 1 (zone without a target), Duration 1 (a longer moment), Complexity 1 (shadow) — thick mist obscures vision throughout the zone for around a quarter of an hour, giving you time to move the device unnoticed.

### Side effects

#### Magical catastrophe

- A **magical catastrophe** is a rare, extreme event in which a magic user’s action slips out of control and causes irreversible consequences for the immediate surroundings and the local region. It is not a “failed use of power,” but a moment when reality reacts in a disproportionate, brutal, and lasting way. The effects of a catastrophe remain in the world as a scar upon the place and cannot be undone by simple means.
- Framing — overall, there is very little magic in the world, it inspires widespread terror, and when it is used, it is mostly through rituals, which due to their time-consuming nature and stability do not cause catastrophes, which is why they happen so rarely. If the **P** play with magic more often, they are exceptions in the world, not the norm, and they are risking more than just their own fate.
  - There are ways in the world to secure arcana, scrolls, and elixirs, but it is time-consuming and costly. One can also learn to reduce their chance of causing catastrophe with their spells, but it is hard practice.
- Magical catastrophe may be caused not only by casting spells (**arcana, nature, religion traits**), but also by strong supernatural **identities** (such as _Shapeshifter_), arcana (magical items), scrolls, and elixirs. However, only the first use of a given source of magic in a scene may end in catastrophe — for example, if a **P** performs the **VIOLENCE** move with the same arcana turn after turn, they may trigger catastrophe only on its first use, but if they switch weapons to another **arcana**, its first use also has a chance to trigger a magical catastrophe.
  - If arcana causes a magical catastrophe, it is destroyed.
  - Only the first spell in a scene cast with a given **trait** may trigger a magical catastrophe.
- A magical catastrophe happens only if the **P** rolls at least 3 dice and gets 3 ones.

##### Example:

I saw the effects of a “blessing of fertility” scroll cast upon refugees after the plague near the Salt Trough by an old woman determined to save her bloodline. It was meant to preserve the population. It worked within hours. Women began giving birth immediately — without contractions, without pause, standing up, on the ground, on the road. Their bodies were not ready. Pelves cracked like clay, skin tore from the inside. The children came out alive, but too many, too fast. One woman gave birth to five in a single day, the sixth tore her belly open and remained trapped inside her — alive, choking, pulling from within. Attempts to help only hastened the next births. Men were not spared either: their bodies began to produce organs they should never have had — with no outlet, no function, only painful masses that grew and burst. The children no one managed to carry away clutched their own umbilical cords in the mud like ropes, all screaming together like one drawn-out echo. They screamed until they ran out of breath or someone silenced them. The spell did not end with the mother’s death — the body kept trying to give birth, even dead. Whole families had to be burned together, because flame was the only thing magic could not “repair.” To this day the earth there stays warm, and after rain small bones rise to the surface. No one counts them anymore. And the best part is that more infants actually survived than people died back then. They live now as one great salt family.

#### Corruption Clock

- A critical failure (2 ones) forces the **P** to roll d6 on the Corruption Table.
- Every time a **P** rolls on that table, they invent a physical corruption for themselves — it may be small — fitting the rolled effect.
- Demons in the world of Zaya are the result of severe corruption caused by spellcasting or alchemy — twisted souls unable to free themselves from their twisted bodies.
- The effects of corruption may be reversed with a **downtime action** by offering sacrifices to the gods — cost: 3 treasures.

#### Social Clock

- Every use of magic in front of ordinary people advances the **Social Clock**.
- At level 3, the party loses 1 **reputation** level with the local faction / people of that area.
- The clock may be **reset** by doing a favor for that faction / those people.

### Scrolls

- Magical scrolls do not contain specific spells — they are a formula releasing pure potential, with which the user (even without magical skill) may do whatever they want.
- Anyone can cast from a scroll.
- Scrolls are used the same way spells are cast — the user gains 3 arcana dice and may add 1 more for 1 **fatigue**.
- Using one costs 1 **fatigue**.

### Runes

- Runes grant unusual properties to items they are merged with.
- Merging a rune with an item is trivial — it is enough to press it to the object so it soaks into the material and leaves its mark there until used — though this must be done at least 24 hours before use so that the rune has time to fill the item with power.
- Every rune is single-use. It is activated by touch for 1 **fatigue**. After use it fades and vanishes from the item.
- A rune can be activated by anyone using the item it is merged with.
- When a rune is activated, it allows the item’s user to give it temporary (you have 2 rounds) magical properties, for example:
  - a spear turns into lightning
  - armor grants invisibility
  - boots allow walking on walls
  - a cloak turns into wings
- Through runes, you can change the fiction in ways available only to magic, though a rune’s effect must always follow from the properties of the item it is merged with — for example, a hammer will not summon lightning.
- Rune effects should be limited — they may never exceed complexity level 2 from the Spell Parameters Table, that is: a strong effect requiring control (flight, invisibility, lightning).
- The **GM** always has the final say on whether the rune effect declared by the **P** takes effect.

### How magic works - for the GM only

Do not read this if you are not the **GM**, so you do not spoil the fun of play for yourself and others.

In the world of **Zaya**, everything — from living beings to stones and particles of air — has a soul. A soul is a mindless force that, from birth, travels through all shapes, until at the end it joins the Pillar of Existence and expands the space of the world — see _Cosmology_. The soul is therefore also the force that creates reality. Magic consists in tearing souls out of their forms and manipulating them according to one’s will. Only a few can do it. On a large scale, this causes the corruption of matter. Practically no one, including mages, knows how magic truly works, and it is one of the greatest secrets of this world.

\**Arcana (magical items) are things into which wandering souls strayed by mistake during their journey (practically no one knows that arcana are *haunted\*).

### Giving Arcana to players

- Arcana are the main source of the **P** power, so the **GM** should hand them out sparingly.
- Arcana are rare, and **P** should have relatively few of them.
- The arcana below are only examples — the **GM** and **P** may invent their own similar ones — but the **GM** always has the final say on whether a given item fits their story or is not too powerful.
- If an arcana turns out to be too powerful for your table, talk among yourselves about whether it should be weakened or replaced so that it does not damage play.
- For the **GM** — when designing adventures, sometimes you will want to add arcana that fit that specific story, but you would not want the **P** to have access to them for the whole campaign — in that case, remember that it is best to make such items consumable, for example by giving them 2–3 uses, possibly with a way to restore those uses.
- The best way to balance arcana is by giving them a limited number of uses.
- Totems are worn on the back.
- A **P** cannot carry equipment in an illogical way, for example wearing two helmets, or a helmet and a hat.
- If an item has more than one version, for example a small / large shield, the **P** chooses which version they gain.
- If an arcana is not a weapon, activating it in combat is treated like using an item, which means it costs a move or an action as **PRESSURE** — unless the description states otherwise.

### Character creation

1. Choose a race.
2. Choose a name and age, describe your appearance, write a short history - choose what profession you practiced until now, for example courier, demagogue, shoemaker.
3. Where are you from?
4. Answer 3 questions:
   1. What matters most to your character?
   2. What are their ambitions and fears?
   3. What bonds do they have?
   4. What secret do they have?
   5. What connects them to the party?
5. Come up with one question about your history that two other players must answer, for example: you saw me turn into a beast, why did you not turn me over to the authorities?
6. Choose 5 **trait** that will start at level 2 and 2 that will start at level 3 (you cannot start with level 3 in **melee combat, aiming, arcana, and nature**). Let those traits reflect your past and training.
7. The rest of your **traits** are level 1 - except **arcana, technology, herbalism**, which require training to use - and if you did not invest in them, they are level 0.
8. Choose 1 identity.
9. At the start, you have specializations in two kinds of weapons.
10. Choose your equipment, in agreement with the GM. You may fill all 10 slots, but you do not have to. Explain narratively where you got that equipment from.
11. You know the common language + your race’s language (if you are human - choose 1 in addition to common).
12. You have 1 **destiny point**.

## End of session

At the end of the session, each **P** says how their character feels about what happened and asks one question to another **P** about their character, for example: what is their favorite meal? What else would they be doing if not for this whole situation they found themselves in? Do they have siblings? Every **P** should receive a question — it may also be a question about that P’s view of current events.  
The first part grounds the character in the world, and the second builds relationships within the party.  
We never skip this step!

Each **P** receives 2 **advancement points**.

One person — taking turns each session — commits to writing down in short bullet points what happened and adding it, with the date, to your shared online journal, which everyone can look into.

# Full rules

## Moves

- **Moves** are mechanics meant to make it easier for **P** to interact with the world. For example, instead of waiting for the **GM** to ask for a survival roll to look for tracks, a **P** may say they are examining the area and use the Discover Reality move to ask the **GM** questions about the scene.
- If the **GM** does not know how a **P** should pay for a partial success, the **P** pays an appropriately high amount of **fatigue** or receives a **splinter**.
  - for example, the **P** declares they are following tracks the party found and rolls a partial success. The **GM** tells them to pay 1 **fatigue** as the cost of that success. If the terrain were harsher (for example swamps, a storm, a blizzard), the **fatigue** cost might be higher. Particularly difficult conditions, like sneaking through an enemy camp, may additionally inflict **splinters**.
- **Narrative rule:** If a move gives you points, you may spend 1 point to propose your own effect or narrative direction. The **GM** decides whether it is possible in the game world and may offer an alternative.
- The **GM** always has the final say and may change the consequences written here for the sake of the narrative.
- These mechanics should be treated as aids — the **GM** does not have to use them if they have a better idea for resolving a situation.

### **I Act in Secret**

**Traits**: various

If, for example, the **P** have broken into some place, their actions are accompanied by a clock with X segments. If the clock fills, the Ps suffer appropriate narrative consequences, for example someone catches them and calls the guards.

Each of their rolls advances the clock as follows:

- critical success: -2 - you cannot reduce an empty clock below zero.
- full success - nothing
- partial success +1
- failure +2
- critical failure: +3

If some roll is not necessary, but the **P** may still have something to find, the **GM** asks a question like: you have a feeling there is something more there, do you risk checking? - for example, in a situation where an already opened chest has a false bottom that can be detected with **technology / investigation**.

Likewise, if there is a secret in the room, for example a loose plank with something hidden beneath it, the **GM** asks the same question about risk, so the **P** know there is something there, but will not discover what it is if they fail the test, which may advance the clock, meaning they have the option to stop while being close to discovery.

The same applies if a **P** wants to take an action that may give them something, but is not generally useful in the current situation, for example they found a letter and want to roll mind to see whether they remember the names in it from somewhere, then the **GM** may ask: you can try to think about it now, but it takes time, do you risk it?

Skipping a roll does not end the move. The move ends when the **P** clearly decide so or when the clock fills.

### **I Sneak**

**Traits**: **stealth**

The player tries to pass unnoticed or hide from enemies.  
Partial success: you must take a **mental status** or 2 **fatigue** and a **splinter**, or you will be detected.

### **I Act Behind Their Back**

**Traits**: **deception**

**P** buy points that can be spent on actions before time runs out. 3 points on a full success and 2 on a partial success. You can always buy 1 more point for 2 **fatigue**. Each point can be spent on an action such as - I quietly read a document / take a letter / take a robe from a wardrobe. **P** cannot exceed the limit - they are restrained by **natural authority** - the survival instinct - if they decide to do it anyway: they suffer immediate consequences, usually triggering an alarm or being noticed.

### **I Steal**

**Traits**: **sleight of hand**

Partial success:

- You fail to take the item, but no one notices that you tried - you cannot try again.
- You manage to take the item unnoticed, but you lose a reputation point, advance clocks (if any), and receive a **splinter** and 2 **fatigue**.

### **I Recall**

**Traits**: fitting knowledge (**mind, arcana, nature**, etc.)

Use when a **P** wants to check their knowledge about a religious ritual, an encountered monster, a historical figure, a special formula, and so on. A full success gives 3 points. A partial success gives 1 point, and you may buy 1 more for 1 **fatigue**. Ask the **GM** 1 question on the topic for each point, and the answer is your character’s knowledge. The questions must fit the situation.

- By default, only two people may make open tests, so if both of them manage to gain points from the **I Recall** **move**, they may ask different questions.
- Standard questions:
  - Do I know this creature / place / thing?
  - What stands out about this creature / place / thing?
  - Who / what is connected to this creature / place / thing?
  - How does it work?
  - How can I use it?
  - Does this creature / place / thing have any weak points or limitations?
  - What dangers are tied to this creature / place / thing?
  - Are there any legends, stories, or superstitions connected to it?
  - Who / what might know more about it?
  - Is this knowledge common, or rather secret?
  - What rules or laws concern this creature / place / thing?
  - Are there any known ways to protect against it?
  - Has anyone tried to use / destroy / understand it before? What happened then?
  - Your own

### **I Discover Reality**

**Traits**: **perception, survival, investigation, nature, mind**, and similar

Use when you are trying to learn something about a specific situation through a particular action - for example following tracks, carefully examining a fresco on the wall, studying a curse. A full success gives 3 points. A partial success gives 1 point, and you may also buy 1 more for 1 **fatigue**. Ask the **GM** 1 question on the topic for each point, and the answer is your character’s knowledge. The questions must fit the situation and the **trait** used for the test.

- By default, only two people may make open tests, so if both manage to gain points from the **I Discover Reality** move, they may ask different questions.
- Standard questions:
  - Who passed through here?
  - How far am I from the one I am tracking?
  - What happened here?
  - What in particular catches my attention?
  - Is there anything here that threatens me?
  - Is there anything here that may help me?
  - How long ago did this happen?
  - Do I see signs of a fight, flight, or haste?
  - Was something hidden or concealed here?
  - Do I see any repeating patterns, symbols, or markings?
  - Can I tell where someone went or where they came from?
  - What smells / sounds are unusual here?
  - Does anything here not fit the surroundings?
  - How can I best hide here or remain unnoticed?
  - Are there traces of magic, poison, illusion, or supernatural interference here?
  - Your own
- You may use this **move** with **mind** if the encountered problem concerns physics, chemistry, geography, psychology, anthropology, or another field of general knowledge not covered by another specific **trait**.
- This is the main move you will use most often.
- It should be used as the result of a specific action, for example I examine the corpse, and concern that issue.
- Hint for the **GM** - if there is important information in the scene that you want to give the players, use their questions from this move to reveal it - even if the answer goes slightly beyond the topic - be flexible for the sake of the narrative.

### **I Want Someone to Do Something for Me**

**Traits**: **persuasion, deception, intimidation, influence**

- The **P** must explain how they are trying to use the chosen **trait**.
- If the **P** does not have some kind of advantage over the target of the roll, they make the test with **disaadventage**. An advantage is, for example, true important information, or clout with the authorities.
- If the target of the roll is a representative of **dominant authority** or **privileged authority**, the **P** receive additional **disaadventages**.
- Example of use - _I want the guard to let me through the gate by lying to him that I am an envoy and showing him a stolen letter of safe conduct._
- You can never make someone do something that openly goes against their values.
- Partial success - you may suggest the GM’s choice, but they make the final decision - the **GM** should choose what best serves the narrative:
  - You fail to convince the target, but your attempt has no consequences and everything blows over.
  - The target wants treasure / an item in return.
  - You manage to convince the target, but must take a **splinter** and 2 **fatigue** and advance any relevant clock, and the **GM** decides whether the group loses a **reputation** point,
  - The **GM** tells you what favor this person demands in return.
  - You manage to convince the target partially, but it solves only one side of your problems - the **GM** tells you what changed - for example the dwarf agrees to lead you to his clan’s seat, but has no intention of recommending you or doing anything similar and leaves you to manage on your own.
- Failure - apart from immediate consequences, the **GM** decides whether the **P** actions advance the social clock / reduce reputation / similar.

### **I Want Someone to Change Their Mind About Their Values**

**Traits**: **persuasion**

A **P** may try to convince a being to think differently about a problem / situation that concerns them - the **GM** decides whether such an attempt is possible at all - for example persuading a bandit to betray their boss.

- The **P** must say what belief the target should replace their current belief with, and it must make sense.
- Partial success - you may suggest the GM’s choice, but they make the final decision - the **GM** should choose what best serves the narrative:
  - You fail to convince the target, but your attempt to influence them blows over and has no consequences.
  - You manage to convince the target, but must take a **splinter** and 2 **fatigue** and advance any relevant clock, and the **GM** decides whether the group loses a **reputation** point.
  - You manage to convince the target partially, but it solves only one side of your problems - the **GM** tells you what changed - for example the cultist agrees that it is not worth sacrificing the mayor’s daughter to the god, but another child must be sacrificed instead.

### **I Know Someone Here**

**Traits**: **influence**

A **P** makes an **influence** test that determines whether they have an acquaintance in a given location who could help with the problem before them.

- Only 2 **P** may try for a given location, and the roll cannot be repeated.
- If the acquaintance appears, the **P** decides who they are - but the **GM** has the final say.
- Full success - the acquaintance is someone of **privileged authority** and may help the **P** bypass one key obstacle.
- Partial success - the acquaintance is below **privileged authority** in the social hierarchy and may help the Ps bypass one non-key obstacle.
- Failure - the **P** gives the impression of a con artist - they pay a mental status to get out of the unpleasant situation they got themselves into.
- Using this move may have narrative consequences - people know the **P** received a favor and gossip about it.
- Having such a connection must make sense - for example a **P** likely knows no one in a backwater village or the wilderness.
- You cannot receive help on this test.

### **I Force an Event**

**Traits**: **influence**

When you want to speed up or force an event (for example an audience, a court decision, the arrival of guards, the acceptance of a bribe, the opening of a fair, the outbreak of a riot), you may make an **influence** test.

- The event you are forcing must have some basis in the community or narrative, unless it is minor (like finding the party lodging even though all rooms are taken).
- The **GM** may impose **disaadventages** if the forced event concerns people in power or is complicated.
- **Full success** – the event happens **here and now**, in a form favorable to you (for example the guards arrive on time and take your side).
- **Partial success** – the event is forced, but with a complication (the **GM** decides):
  - it happens later than you wanted,
  - it requires a price (a favor, payment, concession),
  - or the consequences turn against you (for example the guards are corrupt).
  - if the consequence is hard to determine - pay 2 **fatigue** and take a **splinter**.
- **Failure** – your attempts to force your way into the course of events provoke anger, and you are rejected - you receive a **mental status** - unless narrative consequences fit better, for example clock movement (the **GM** decides).
- You cannot receive help on this test.

### **I Gather Information**

**Traits**: **influence**, **performance**

Use this when you are trying to get information from strangers, for example you are trying to learn whether there is a place in the city where something illegal can be bought.

- **Full success** – the information you obtain is detailed and true.
- **Partial success** – you obtain information, but with a complication (the **GM** decides):
  - someone does not tell you everything,
  - it requires a price (a favor, payment, concession),
  - or the consequences turn against you (for example gossip spreads and someone hostile learns of your moves).
  - advance a detection / social / similar clock
  - if the consequence is hard to determine - pay 2 **fatigue** and take a **splinter**.
- **Failure** – you fail to get any information, people start becoming suspicious and unfriendly, and the **GM** decides whether:
  - rumors reveal your plans / other narrative consequences
  - you advance a detection / social / similar clock
  - you get a **mental status**, stressed by the situation you dug yourself into - for example a brawl.

### **I Feel There Is More Here**

**Traits**: **investigation**

When a **P** believes they may have failed to find all important information in a location, or that something escaped them, they may make a test to check whether they were right.

- Full success - the **GM** tells you of 1 thing you missed, if such a thing exists.
- Partial success - you know whether you missed something or not, but if something remains to be discovered, you must pay 2 **fatigue** to learn what it is. The **GM** may reveal only significant information this way - it is also a way for them to show the **P** something important they overlooked.
- Failure - you have no idea whether you missed anything or not.

### **I Evoke Fear**

**Traits**: **intimidation**

A **P** uses intimidation to provoke fear or submission in a target. On a full success or a partial success + 1 **fatigue** (or more if the target is resistant or the situation is unfavorable - **GM** decision) - you force the being(s) to - choose one:

- stop
- break off what they are doing
- refrain from taking the action they intended to take
- surrender - costs 3 **fatigue** and does not work on key NPCs, powerful enemies, or organized groups - the **GM** has the final say here.
- stand where you indicate and not move
- perform a simple task for you - for example carry something or serve as bait
- quickly move away
- attack you - costs 2 **fatigue** - the **GM** decides whether it suits the narrative
- do something against themselves - costs 4 **fatigue** on a partial success and 2 on a full success; it does not work on key NPCs, powerful enemies, or representatives of **dominant authority** - the **GM** has the final say here.
- your own ideas

### **I Put on a Performance**

**Traits**: **performance**

A **P** uses their artistic talent, whether in singing, dancing, acting, or another form, to create specific effects in the behavior and emotions of others.

- Full success - you gain 3 performance points
- Partial success - you gain 1 performance point and may buy 2 more for 1 **fatigue** each
- For each performance point (unless stated otherwise), you may:
  - draw attention to yourself - this gives others the opportunity to do 1 thing behind the audience’s backs or gives you an opportunity to speak to a crowd - may be chosen multiple times
  - gain a favorable glance - you gain advantage on social tests against them - costs 2 points
  - gain notoriety - you become recognizable in the area
  - learn rumors - the **GM** tells you 1 important piece of information about recent local events
  - gain an audience with the local authority - costs 3 points - GM’s decision.
  - give the listeners a minor suggestion they will follow (if it is logical) - costs 3 points - for example _go home_ or _you should support my candidate for mayor_.
  - stir specific emotions - for example you may make the crowd furious or defuse tension - costs 2 points.
  - gain a safe place - someone offers you a hideout - costs 2 points.
  - your own ideas.

### **I Foresee the Future**

**Traits**: **religion** or **intuition**

- With **religion** it requires an hour-long ritual and sacrificing a **component**
- With **intuition** it requires an hour of meditation and sacrificing a **resource**
- It cannot be part of a rest (others may rest, but not the **P** trying to foresee the future).
- Full success - you gain 2 prophecy points.
- Partial success - you gain 1 prophecy point.
- For each prophecy point, ask the **GM** one question about coming events, such as:
  - what can I expect?
  - whom will I meet?
  - what dangers await me?
  - what should I avoid?
  - what may surprise me?
  - what will be the consequences of my actions?
  - are my feelings right?
  - what place will be key to my journey?
  - what may prove to be illusion or falsehood?
  - what chances do I have?
  - your own ideas
- The GM’s answers do not have to be direct.

### **I Know How This Works**

**Traits**: **technology**

When a **P** interacts with a machine / tool, they may make a roll that lets them determine how familiar its operation is to them.

- Full success - you gain 3 technique points + you may buy 1 more for 1 fatigue.
- Partial success - you gain 1 technique point + you may buy 3 more for 1 fatigue each.
- For each technique point (unless stated otherwise), you may:
  - ask the **GM** a question connected to the machine / tool that determines how much you know about it. For example:
    - what is this for?
    - how does it work?
    - who created it?
    - how could this be useful to me?
    - how old is it?
    - is it usable?
    - what dangers come with using this machine?
    - is there something here I do not notice at first glance?
    - your own ideas
  - create a simple tool / machine - costs 2 points and a short rest devoted only to that.
  - repair a simple tool / machine familiar to you - costs 2 points.
  - repair a simple unfamiliar tool / machine - costs 2 points and a short rest devoted only to that. Once repaired, you know how to use it.
  - repair a complex tool / machine - costs 4 points and a long rest devoted only to that (this can be delayed if the item can be carried with you) - once repaired, you know how to use it.
  - use a simple unfamiliar tool - costs 2 points.
  - use a complex, unfamiliar tool (for example a vehicle) - costs 4 points.

### **I Guide the Way**

**Traits**: **survival**

One **P** may decide to lead the **exploration** - this roll immediately counts as their contribution to the party’s group **survival** roll and replaces their personal effect:

- Full success - 2 points
- Partial success - 1 point and you may buy 1 more for 2 **fatigue**:
- For each point gained on this test, the **P** may buy one of the following, but each point may be chosen only once:
  - you hunt - this does not stack even if 2 **P** choose this option - reduce the rations needed for the journey:
    - if it lasts up to a week - each person must pay 2 rations for the journey.
    - if it lasts longer than a week - each person must pay 4 rations for the journey.
  - ask the **GM** for a clue about what you are doing / the place / the situation / etc.
  - in case of failure on the exploration roll, if some **P** received a status, you may change it into 2 **fatigue** for them and 1 for you.
  - remove 1 fatigue from an ally.
  - you automatically find a component, resource, or material.
  - roll on the Treasure Table.
  - shorten the journey by 2 days.
  - propose your own narrative solution.
  - in a small exploration - give all remaining **P** **advantage** on the test - costs 2.
- Failure - **physical status** - describe what happened to you.
- Do not use this **move** during **small explorations**.

### **I Pretend to Be Someone Else**

**Traits**: *deception**, **performance**

- Full success - you are unrecognizable, but you will give yourself away if:
  - you ask more than 3 questions whose answers should, in your listeners’ eyes, be obvious to the person you are pretending to be.
  - you give more than 3 orders to the subordinates of the being you are pretending to be. No one will obey an order contrary to the values of the group you are trying to deceive, and such an attempt immediately gives you away.
- Partial success - every question or order you direct at the group you are trying to deceive costs you 1 **fatigue**. In total, you cannot spend more than 3 **fatigue** on them if you do not want to give yourself away. The limitations listed under full success and the logic of the fiction still apply.
- Failure - apart from immediate consequences, the **GM** decides whether the **P** actions advance a clock / reduce reputation / similar.
- The **GM** may impose **disaadventages** depending on how complicated a task you are attempting.
- You may try to gain **advantage** through creative disguise and roleplay.
- You cannot reset the number of questions / orders - they apply to the whole duration of the deception _operation_.

### **I Sell a Lie**

**Traits**: **deception**

The **GM** may decide that a specific attempt at lying would have no chance of deceiving the target - for example, a king will not believe that the queen has a lover because he knows her every move from his spies’ reports.

- A complicated, strongly accusatory lie, a lie aimed at privileged authority and above, and similar cases will not work unless the **P** has something they could present as evidence - for example a man’s nightshirt in the queen’s chamber that would never fit the king, or paid false testimony from a maid.
- Partial success - the **GM** chooses:
  - the target believes you, but the whole group gets **disaadventage** on further **social** tests against them.
  - you convinced part of the gathered people, and it is not enough to gain what you hoped for, but perhaps you can establish cooperation with them that opens another road?
  - the target demands more evidence.
  - you fail to convince the target, but your lie passes without consequences.
  - you take a **splinter** and 2 **fatigue** as the cost of stress, and you manage to quickly come up with something logical that ultimately convinces the target - possible only with lies that do not carry major narrative consequences.
- Failure - apart from immediate consequences, the **GM** decides whether the **P** actions advance the social clock / reduce reputation / similar.

### **I Perform a Ritual**

**Traits**: **religion, arcana**

Rituals require:

- sacrificing a **treasure, component, resource, and material**
- a **downtime action** (as 3 days of uninterrupted activity, after which the participants receive 6 **fatigue**)
- a place where no one will disturb them.

A partial success on this test means that if the **P** wants the ritual to succeed, they must additionally pay 4 **advancement points**.  
If the ritual concerns a person, you must have something close to them - for example some personal item.  
A ritual is the only path to gaining a magical effect for a longer period, for example an illusion lasting a month.

A ritual may allow the **P** to:

- Hold a short discussion with a high-ranking representative of a chosen deity - 3 questions.
- Remove a curse / disperse powerful magic.
- Place a curse on an item / being - the **P** and **GM** decide together what the curse is - the **GM** has the final say.
- Gain information important to the narrative - the **GM** must provide it directly, though they should dress it in a fitting form.
- Locate an item / person, if they are in the same geographical region (for example within the borders of a state).
- Fulfill a narrative requirement for a ritual.
- Summon an angel, demon, or nature spirit - 4 questions that decide their attitude - they may attack.
- The **P** declares that one non-key thing will definitely happen in the future - for example the vault door will be open when we break into the bank, or the NPC will still be alive when we arrive.
- The **P** opens a passage between dimensions for 1 day - they must have and sacrifice something tied to that dimension.
- Open / close a portal.
- The **P** erases one memory of a being.
- The **P** asks one key question about the past - and the **GM** must answer.
- The **P** creates an **arcana** - the **P** and **GM** determine its effect together - the **GM** has the final say.
- The **P** own ideas - the **GM** has the final say.

A ritual may be performed as a **group test**, but only people with at least level 3 in a **trait** that allows spellcasting (**arcana, nature, religion**) may take part. In that case it may only succeed or fail, so a full success is not required for it to succeed without paying **advancement points**, unlike a partial success when acting alone.

- On failure, each participant (whether solo or in a group) receives a **mental status**.

Rituals do not cause corruption.

### **I Grasp at a Razor’s Edge*

**Traits**: fitting one

A **P** decides to do something openly and severely dangerous, such as jumping over a chasm, slipping unnoticed between guards in plain sight, deceiving a bandit chief in his own stronghold where failure could end in violence, climbing a very slippery wall that is the only way out, swimming through a water corridor too long to cross safely, and so on.

- You may use it only when other methods have failed, as a last resort.
- Current **fatigue** does not affect this move, by the logic of adrenaline giving energy in a critical moment.
- Partial success - you succeed, but receive 4 **fatigue** and roll a **physical / mental status** (depending on the situation).
- Failure - you fail and, in addition to the normal consequences, receive 6 **fatigue** and roll a **physical / mental status** (depending on the situation).
- Instead of the above penalties, the **GM** may impose consequences that fit the narrative better.

### **I Seduce**

**Traits**: **persuasion, influence, performance, deception**

A **P** may try to seduce an NPC for one night (for longer relationships, see **downtime** actions) - for pleasure or gain. The **GM** decides how hard a given person is to seduce and applies appropriate **disaadventages** - usually this may be judged by the type of **authority** they belong to - dominant 3 **disaadventages**, privileged 2, executive 1, plus any additional **disaadventages** resulting from the narrative (the princess is never alone / fears strangers / the **P** undermined her values, etc.).

- Full success - means taking someone to bed / having a pleasant evening and possibly drawing one key piece of information from the NPC or obtaining a favor (if the NPC has such information or influence - GM’s decision). The **P** is out of action for at least 4 hours of fictional time.
- Partial success - means stirring interest without consummation and possibly gaining one non-key piece of information from the NPC or a small favor.
- Failure - apart from immediate consequences, the **GM** decides whether the **P** actions advance the social clock / reduce reputation / similar.

### **I Try to Read Them**

**Traits**: **intuition**

A **P** tries to guess whether their interlocutor is lying, hiding something, or similar.

- Full success - the **P** knows the NPC did not tell them everything, and gains **advantage** on the social test they use to draw it out.
- Partial success - the **GM** chooses:
  - The **P** knows the NPC did not tell them everything, but gets **disaadventage** on the social test they use to draw it out.
  - The **GM** gives the **P** one non-key clue about their problem - for example _It is hard to tell whether X is definitely taking on this task of their own free will, but you noticed they became irritable when you touched on the subject of their honor_.
  - The **GM** gives the **P** a clear answer that the NPC is hiding / faking something, but makes it clear there is no way to pull details out of them.
  - The **GM** reveals clear information, but not its explanation / reasons - for example _you are sure this man beat his child, but you know none of the details of the circumstances in which it happened_.
  - The **GM** reveals part of the information - for example _you suspect X had a letter from the baron in hand, but you have no way of confirming whether they opened it._
- Failure - the **P** believes the NPC has nothing more to add / is hiding nothing / cannot read anything from them.

### **I Push Through Difficult Terrain**

**Traits**: **resilience, athletics, survival, agility**

A **P** tries to climb a slippery rock, push through enemies blocking the way, cross a field of thorns or mud, and similar. Choose the **trait** that fits the situation.

- Full success - you may discard 1 failure from another P’s result on this test, but not if they roll only 1 die - you are doing so well that you help them - or use the narrative rule.
- Partial success - you must pay 1 + 1 **fatigue** for each failure on the dice above the number of successes for it to work, or you are treated as though you failed.
- Failure - narrative consequences or a **physical / mental status** (the **GM** decides).
  Use this move only outside combat. During a clash, a partial success in similar situations should simply cause an enemy reaction.

### **I Steer**

**Traits**: **animal handling, technology**

Sometimes a **P** is controlling a vehicle or mount in a risky situation, or wants to do something unusual while driving / riding - for example yank the reins so sharply that the horses pulling the wagon overturn it - then this **move** should be used.
Break the P’s action description into parts - for example:

- I jerk the horses into overturning the wagon
- I want the wagon to fall onto our enemies / block something
- I try to do it so no one inside gets hurt / the cargo is not destroyed
- I want the wagon not to be destroyed so we can still use it later.
  Full success - the **P** gets 3 points + may buy 1 more for 1 **fatigue**.
  Partial success - the **P** gets 2 points + may buy 1 more for 1 **fatigue**.
  Fulfilling each condition costs 1 point.

### **I Interrogate**

**Traits**: **persuasion, intimidation, influence**

When you try to extract information from someone unwilling.

- **Full success**: You ask 4 questions, which the **GM** must answer.
- **Partial success**: You ask 2 questions and may ask 1 additional one for 1 **fatigue**.
  - The **GM** may decide that one answer is a lie – but does not reveal which one. All the others must be true, though not necessarily direct.
- The questions must concern facts known to the target.
- This move cannot be repeated to gain more answers.
- Example questions:
  - Who gave you this job?
  - Where is the person / item?
  - How many of you are there?
  - What were you promised in return?
  - Who else knows about this?
  - Did you do this?

## Other mechanics

### Group Rolls

- Use this to determine how the whole group handled a shared action requiring one **trait**, for example everyone is sneaking past the guards, everyone is climbing a rope, everyone is trying to blend into a crowd, etc., or different **traits**, for example they are organizing an event or setting a trap.
- Everyone rolls their own dice pool and chooses the highest result, which they convert into points:
  - highest 1–3 → 0 pts
  - highest 4 → 1 pt
  - highest 5 → 2 pts
  - highest 6 → 3 pts
- Add up the points of all players — this is the group result (S).
- The group must beat the threshold **(2 × number of **P**) - 1** for the test to end in success. The **GM** may raise/lower this threshold in fitting circumstances if the challenge is relatively hard/easy.
- After learning the result S, the group may lower the threshold by spending **fatigue**:
  - 4 **fatigue** lower the threshold by 1 point.
  - the threshold can be lowered at most to **(2 × number of **P**) − 4** (by a maximum of 3 steps, even if the base threshold is raised by the scale of the challenge).
  - **fatigue** can be split between players in any way, but one player may spend at most 3 **fatigue** on this test.
  - fatigue does not increase result S, it only lowers the threshold.
- If S ≥ the current threshold, the test ends in success; if S < the current threshold, the test ends in failure.
- A group test is assumed to end in either success or failure. However, if the group only achieved success after lowering the threshold by more than 1 point, the **GM** may, for narrative purposes, treat the result as a partial success and introduce a cost or complication.
- The distribution below shows how often the group achieves success on group rolls in a typical situation (5 moderately advanced **P**, with dice pools: 1,2,2,3,4), with optimal fatigue spending:
  - S ≥ 9 → success without fatigue: ~56.8%
  - S = 8 → success for 4 fatigue: ~15.1%
  - S = 7 → success for 8 fatigue: ~11.8%
  - S = 6 → success for 12 fatigue: ~8.1%
  - S ≤ 5 → failure (the threshold cannot be lowered any further): ~8.2%

### Opposed Tests

When you need to resolve an opposed test, for example two **P** are wrestling, both sides roll using the **trait** that fits the situation and the one with more successes wins — in case of a tie, the side with more pips wins.

#### Opposition

Sometimes there are moments when one **P** wants to do something another **P** would object to. For example, they are sitting at the royal table and one of them says they are trying to pour poison into the queen’s cup, which the other **P** wants to prevent. They cannot talk about it, however, because the **P** trying to poison the queen only has a moment to act, and discussing murdering the king’s wife at his table may not be the best idea. Therefore, in such situations, **we pause game time**. This is a team game and, for the good of the narrative and the whole table, it is better for the party to discuss what decision they will make here, so it does not turn out to be their last shared decision before the campaign falls apart. The **GM** may help find a solution, for example by proposing a compromise. We are people who want to have fun together and build a story together. Maintaining a good atmosphere takes effort, and the best way to do that is through conversation. If conversation cannot produce a result that everyone will be satisfied with — stop playing together. Better not to play at all than to wear each other down.

This rule can be used not only in cases of opposition, but at any key moment.

### Exploration

| Situation                                                                                           | Mechanic                              |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| The road is narrative background, shows the world, consumes resources, but is not the main conflict | **Exploration (travel)**              |
| A single terrain obstacle or a short stretch of road                                                | **Minor exploration (single tests)**  |
| The road itself is a scene of tension or conflict                                                   | **Sequential Narrative** or **Chase** |

#### Travel

- It is used to play out a narrative journey by **P** from point A to point B that does not carry much narrative weight, meaning it is not very important.
- At each narrative stop, but no more than once per day, when **P** set out on a journey, especially through difficult terrain, such as deep snow, swamps, or dense forest, they make a **group survival test**, determining how they deal with nature along the way.
- The **GM** defines the scenery that the **P** are crossing, and they describe in turn in 1–2 sentences what happened to them during the journey according to the group’s overall result and their individual result. You can turn this into a short conversation between the characters.
  - This description is very important, because it builds fantastic narrative and fills stretches of travel with current events. It is what gives this process meaning. Never skip it.
- Resting during exploration:
  - During exploration lasting longer than 1 day, **P** may mechanically take 1 long rest for each full week of travel.
  - **P** cannot remove **fatigue** or heal **statuses** they gained during exploration during these rests.
- Success/failure in exploration is determined as a group, but each **P** also receives their own individual travel effect according to the result they achieved on their own.
- Critical results during exploration: by default, fate die/splinter.
- Weather and terrain difficulty may impose **disadvantages** during exploration.
- The whole exploration should take 15 minutes of session time. The key is quick rolls, short descriptions, and moving on to the next scene.
- Exploration and food:
  - If **P** are not in an urban area and are not using rented rooms, they must eat one food ration each day and mark it off their inventory, otherwise they receive **discomfort** from hunger.
  - If **P** are traveling between places that are a day or more apart, they must have food for each day.
  - **28-35 food rations cost 1 treasure** - one month of life for 1 person / one week of travel for 4/5 people.
  - If **P** do not have enough food - they get **disadvantage** on the exploration test and **fatigue** - as a result of being forced to hunt.
  - If the exploration represents fleeing/searching for someone - penalties for lack of food may be canceled if the other side is in the same situation - they negate each other.
- Before exploration, at most 1 **P** may use the **I Guide** **move**.
- This is the perfect moment for the **GM** to work information about changes in the world or consequences of the **P**’s past actions into the journey - for example:
  - **P** find notices saying they are wanted.
  - **P** come across a traveling caravan.
  - **P** run into an armed unit/checkpoint on the road.
  - **P** see troops/a monster/something unusual in the distance.
  - Word reaches **P** about the girl whom **P** helped/harmed in previous locations.

##### Structure - 15 min

1. The **GM** defines the conditions: the weather and terrain description, for example _your wagon struggles to roll through the snow, the blizzard lashes your faces, you are cold even under your furs, and yet merchant caravans still appear on the road, because people have to live off something, and society would collapse without transport._
2. Group roll for **survival**.
3. Team and individual effects (according to the clock guidelines):
   - Group success - the party gets through unharmed - individual effects:
     - **P** had a full success - choose 1:
       - you found something along the way - roll twice on the **Loot Table** and choose the higher result
       - ask the **GM** 2 questions, request 2 clues regarding the situation - this may take the form of a conversation with someone met on the road.
       - 1 roll on the **Loot Table** and 1 question/clue.
     - **P** had a partial success (if you had more failures than successes on the roll - you receive **fatigue**) - choose 1:
       - roll once on the **Loot Table**.
       - ask the **GM** 1 question, request 1 clue regarding the situation.
     - **P** got a failure - you receive 2 **fatigue** - you must throw away at least one item (you lose it), even if you have free inventory space.
   - Group failure - the party struggles onward - individual effects:
     - **P** had a full success - choose 1:
       - roll once on the **Loot Table**.
       - ask the **GM** 1 question, request 1 clue regarding the situation.
     - **P** had a partial success - an accident happened, you receive a **physical status**.
     - **P** got a failure - you barely made it, draw a **physical and mental status**.
4. Descriptions:
   - each **P** says 1–2 sentences about what happened to their character along the way, based on their own result, the group’s result, and the narrative framing: how they found an item, how they got wounded, how they addressed another **P**’s character, etc.
5. An event along the way / overheard information about changes in the world or consequences of the **P**’s past actions.
6. Ending: the **GM** sums up the expedition in two sentences.

#### Exploration chains / minor explorations

If exploration would happen often during one session (3+) or one after another, or if **P** only have a small stretch to cross, replace those shorter/less important/additional ones with a simple test for each **P** using a **trait** that fits the scenery being crossed.

For example, **P** are crossing a frozen lake - so each rolls **stealth**:

- failure - **physical status** / 3 **fatigue**.
- partial success - **fatigue**.
- full success - you may:
  - pay 1 **fatigue** to help a companion and raise their failure to a partial success
  - find one of the following: **material** / **component** / **resource** / **utility item**
  - ask the **GM** a question / get a clue from the **GM**
  - roll on the Loot Table
  - propose your own solution

More examples: climbing - **athletics**; wilderness - **survival**; labyrinth - **mind**/**intuition**; shaky bridge - **dexterity**; forcing your way through a herd of thousands of animals - **animal handling**.

**P** should still describe in 1–2 sentences how they managed.

You can still use this within a group test.

#### Exploration as a scene of tension

If exploration is not meant to be just travel from point A to point B, but an emotional scene of greater importance, for example climbing an icy mountain in time to light a fire that will call reinforcements, it can be played in two ways.

1. As a chase (see below) - the **GM** sets the segments, and **P** must beat time and their own exhaustion.
2. As a sequential narrative (see below) - the **GM** sets as many obstacles as there are **P**, and they come up with a plan for how to overcome them.

### Chase

- It consists of 3 to 5 segments. For example **A street full of people** → **A narrow alley** → **A fence** → **The sewers**.
- Each segment is led by a different player (in clockwise order). The first one is chosen randomly. The leader never repeats, unless for some reason everyone has already done it.
- The leader chooses the main **trait** of the segment, saying how they intend to use it, and each other **P** chooses a different **trait** with which they will support the leader’s action. All **traits** from the category of the **trait** chosen by the leader have **disadvantage**.
- **P** make a **group test**, rolling in turn using their chosen **traits** (the leader starts, then clockwise) and each describing in 1–2 sentences their contribution to the narrative, while the **GM** keeps the story coherent.
- For the purposes of a chase, the base threshold for passing the group test is reduced to **(2 × number of **P**) - 2**, but the **GM** may raise it if the situation is exceptionally dangerous.
- During the race, the same **trait** chosen by the leader can never be used again, even in the segment in which it was chosen.
- **P** cannot reuse **traits** they themselves used in previous segments.
- The **GM** adds **advantages** and **disadvantages** to **P**’s rolls to reflect the situation - for example, you are carrying a child while fleeing, so you roll with **disadvantage**.
  - If some conditions would impose a constant penalty on **P**, for example the pursuer is flying and nothing hinders their path, or the fleeing side must push through water while the pursuer does not - the **GM** rolls k6 at the start of the segment to determine how many **P** receive **disadvantage** because of it, where a result of 1 means 0, and 6 means 5 - however, this can never impose more disadvantages than there are **P**.
- To win the chase, **P** must come out ahead in more than half the segments.
- In case of a tie - play one additional segment.
- The chase ends early if the number of won segments gives the opposing side no chance to catch up.
- If **P** have **powers** that _speed them up_, such as teleportation, temporary flight, etc., they may use them with a limit of one per segment to gain **advantage**. Each such use imposes **fatigue** on them (even if it would normally cost more) and eliminates the possibility of reusing the same **power** in later segments.
- Using **traits** meant for combat - **melee combat, aiming, arcana, nature, religion** - in a way that could be seen as an attempt to injure the enemy is allowed, but it can never prevent the other side from continuing the chase, only hinder them, for example _an arrow hits the monster’s thick hide on its head, deals it no real harm, but the creature spends a few seconds snapping off the splinter blocking its vision and charges again, even angrier_.
- During a chase, help mechanics cannot be used to support other **P**.
- A critical failure, in addition to its usual effects, gives the **P** who got it **disadvantage** in the next segment - if it is the last segment, it gives -1 to the **group test** point pool.
- A critical success, in addition to its usual effects, gives the **P** who got it **advantage** in the next segment - if it is the last segment, it gives +1 to the **group test** point pool.
- Before starting the chase, establish what exactly winning and losing it means in the fiction. Usually this will mean catching the target or safely getting away from the opposing side, but the specific consequences should follow from the situation and the scene’s narrative.
- Sometimes not only the overall result matters, but the result of each segment - for example **P** are moving through a haunted house and failure in each segment may inflict corruption on them.
- The chase mechanic can be very easily adapted into narrative resolution of an **environmental threat** - surviving an avalanche, climbing a steep cliff, escaping a cycle, getting through a sandstorm, etc.

#### How to quickly resolve whether P caught the target without a chase?

- You can try to catch fugitives with **athletics** or **aiming**.
- When **P** roll **athletics**:
  - Full success - **P** catches up to and grabs the fugitive without trouble
  - Partial success - choose from the list:
    - **P** catches up to and grabs the fugitive, but the fugitive injures them, inflicting a random physical status.
    - **P** catches up to and grabs the fugitive, but the group gets **disadvantage** on social tests against them.
  - **P** may gain 1 **advantage** for each speed-enhancing **power** they use at its standard cost.
- When **P** roll **aiming**:
  - Full success - the fleeing target takes **P**’s full damage; if they survive, they fall unconscious.
  - Partial success - the fleeing target takes **P**’s full damage; if they survive, they fall to the ground, and choose from the list:
    - With their screams, they alarm bystanders.
    - The group gets **disadvantage** on social tests against them.
    - Other disaadventages - especially in the case of fleeing key NPCs.

#### Chase as a dispute

- You can use the chase rules to play out scenes such as a court hearing, negotiations, an argument, etc.
- The segments should then reflect the narrative, for example **P** appear as one side at a great assembly during which they have a chance to convince the crowd of certain arguments, so the action might represent something like: **a credible explanation of their role and position in the matter being discussed** → **presenting their arguments** → **countering the opposition’s attacks** → **controlling the crowd** → **presenting a solution**.
- Each segment should be played out narratively, just like in a normal chase, meaning **P** roll together and then, in clockwise order starting with the leader, describe their contribution to the narrative based on their own result and the group’s result.
- In a normal chase, what matters is gaining or shortening distance; in a dispute, what matters is: influence over the opposing side, represented by: narrative, arguments, evidence, emotions, logic, authority, credibility, status, influence over the crowd, etc. - the actions taken by **P** during segments should revolve around these domains.

Key mechanical differences:

- Every true argument made by **P**, whose strength the **GM** recognizes as genuinely affecting the narrative (not eloquence or logic, but an actual change in the fictional situation), gives **P** k6/2 (1-2: 1, 3-4: 2, 5-6: 3) **advantages**, which **P** may distribute however they want within the party.
- If the leader chose a **social trait** and achieves at least a partial success, the party gets +1 to the **group test** point pool.
- If no one uses a social **trait** in the segment, everyone has **disadvantage**.
- **Traits** **melee combat** and **aiming** are excluded, because they distort the dispute - unless for some reason it makes sense in the narrative, in which case they may be used with **disadvantage**.
- The **GM** rolls **disadvantages** (k6) for **P** because of a constant penalty in situations where, for example, the audience is hostile, **P** lack sufficient authority, the opponents are highly respected, **P**’s argumentation is completely fabricated, etc.

Examples of using non-social **traits**:

- **sleight of hand** - revealing evidence, demonstrating fraud
- **athletics** - physically presenting something to the crowd, humiliating the opponent by showing dominance
- **nature** - expertise, analysis, knowledge of natural processes
- **mind** - a chain of cause and effect
- **herbalism** - demonstrating a chemical reaction or poison

### Sequential Narrative Mechanic

- Sequential narrative is used to play out full, broad scenes in a story-driven way: from breaking into a palace to a synchronized sabotage operation and escaping a trap.
- This will probably be one of the most frequently used mechanics in the game.
- Before starting, establish a clear goal: for example entering a guarded building, obtaining evidence and escaping, as well as a serious consequence of failure, for example **P** are captured and imprisoned. The consequence should usually mean the end of the scene or a complete change in its nature, for example from we are trying to achieve something to we are trying to survive. If the consequence happens, its previously established scope is not up for discussion.
- The sequence works on the basis of two clocks, the **success clock** and the **failure clock**.
- The **GM** determines how many segments the **success clock** should have depending on the task’s difficulty - from 4 to 8 (8 or 6 recommended); if more are needed, split it into two separate sequences. The **failure clock** always has the same number of segments as the **success clock**.
- During the scene, the **GM** will indicate (before the roll) which specific tests will affect the clocks - these will always be tests of key importance, for example slipping past guards, disarming a trap, finding the way, etc.
  - No other rolls during this scene affect the clocks of this mechanic, but they still normally affect the party’s situation and capabilities.
- Each segment is one test of an appropriate **trait**, made by one **P**. Each **P** should take part in the scene if possible. Also, the same **P** cannot use the same **trait** twice, the test must be made by someone else, or **P** must come up with another way to move forward.
- The success clock cannot go below 0, but the failure clock can - to a maximum of half of all its segments, rounded down.
- A critical success always moves the failure clock back by 1, and a critical failure always advances it by an additional 1 step.
- The **success clock** and the **failure clock** move according to the table below:

| Result                | Effect                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| ❌ **Failure**        | **P** advances the success clock + rolls **k6 × remaining levels of the failure clock**, where each 1-3 on a die advances the **failure clock** by 1 OR **P** does NOT advance the success clock + rolls the same number of dice, where each 1-2 on a die advances the **failure clock** by 1 and a consequence stage appears. The appropriate approach depends on the narrative; if, for example, failing at some stage of the plan would make it impossible for **P** to continue, the consequence-stage variant should be used - **P** may also try to save themselves this way from the possible filling of the **failure clock**, reducing their chances of advancing its stages at the cost of an additional roll. |
| ◒ **Partial success** | **P** advances the success clock + rolls **k6 × remaining levels of the failure clock**. Each 1-2 on a die = the **failure clock** advances by **1**.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| ✅ **Full success**   | **P** advances the success clock + rolls **k6 × half of all levels of the success clock** (rounded down). Each 5-6 on a die moves the **failure clock** back by **1**.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |

- When advancing the failure clock, **P** never roll more dice than half of all segments of that clock, rounded down, + 1; that is: for eight segments - max 5 dice, for seven - 4, for six - 4, for five - 3, for four - 3.
- **Consequence stages** resulting from the actions of **P** force **P** to come up with another way to achieve what they intended, and therefore repeat the test for that stage - however, they cannot use the same **trait** as in the test that caused the consequence, nor do the same thing.
- Sometimes it is possible to predict all the rolls in the sequence before it begins - in that case **P** may prepare a detailed plan in advance.
- Sometimes the **GM** will present a scene in which there are only as many rolls as there are clock segments and will have them all prepared when the situation requires acting in a very specific way.
- It may happen that on a partial success by **P**, the success and failure clocks fill at the same time - in that case, **P** achieve their goal, but must suffer some fitting, narratively significant consequences.
- After their roll, it is **P** who describes how their action played out (in line with the result), and the **GM** only corrects that vision if necessary to fit the narrative.
- Each failure stage should introduce a narrative, not mechanical, disaadventage, for example **P** were noticed during the break-in, now the guards are searching for them inside and they must weave around through their descriptions, but it does not have to hinder their later rolls.
- If **P** are breaking into a guarded area, getting inside should be the first stage of the sequence, and getting out the last.
- The whole sequence is usually an improvised scene in which **P** take the lead. The GM’s role is mainly to describe situations and ask "what ideas do you have for moving forward?" and push **P** toward more credible / narratively fitting proposals.
- During a sequential narrative, **P** may once (for the whole party) discard one die advancing the narrative clock, if the one making the test takes on 4 **fatigue** and a **scar** (k6-1 roll) and describes their desperate move. This rescue cannot be used if only 1 segment remains before the **failure clock** fills.
- If the **failure clock** has filled, but **P** care deeply about continuing (for example the matter is personal) or it would be better for the narrative, they may make a major sacrifice to move that clock back by 1, for example by permanently using up a protective arcana or permanently crossing off 1 HP slot.
- **P** may use their **identities**, for example mobile, by paying **fatigue** normally, to add **advantage** to their test if it fits the narrative.
- **P** may use help mechanics only if it can be sensibly woven into the narrative.
- Interacting with a trap usually consists of two rolls - **investigation** (examining) and **technology/sleight of hand** (disarming/opening) - therefore, if interaction with a trap is supposed to move clocks in a **sequential narrative**, use the examining roll for that, not the disarming one.
- If **P** have arcana, special items, etc., that could reasonably help with their roll at a given stage, they may use them by paying **fatigue** (even if it would normally cost more) to gain 1 **advantage** from it - a specific aid can only be used once during the scene (for example, the same power cannot be used twice) - **P** includes the use of that aid in the description of their action.
- If the fiction develops in such a way that **P** complete their task before the success clock fills, simply accept it and do not force the rest of the events.
- **Sequential Narrative** is not a mechanic for everyday obstacles or ordinary scenes. Use it when the stakes are real and high – when failure may mean death, permanent maiming, losing a key ally, the fall of a faction, an irreversible change in the situation, advancing a key clock, etc. Overused, it loses its weight and stops building tension. In practice it should appear rarely – usually once per session, exceptionally twice, and only when the consequences are serious. For less important, transitional, or local scenes, use the **Pressure** mechanic.

#### EXAMPLE: Breaking into the Inquisition Vault

**Goal:** Obtain the list of spies and escape. **Consequence of failure:** The alarm is raised, and **P** are cornered in a dead-end alley (fight for survival). **Clocks:** Success 0/6 | Failure 0/6.

##### Stage 1: Getting over the walls (Stealth)

- **Player A** rolls a **Partial Success (4)**.
- **Mechanic:** The Success Clock advances (+1). The **Failure Clock** has 6 empty segments, so Player A rolls **4k6** (because that is the maximum number of failure dice for a 6-segment failure clock) for failure (a result of 1-2 is a failure point).
- **Failure roll result:** One “1” and one “2” come up. **Failure Clock: 2/6.**
- _Description:_ **P** climb the walls, but a guard heard something and is watching the courtyard more closely.

_Minor rolls changing the situation_

##### Stage 2: Getting past the guards in the courtyard (Deception)

- **Player B** (cannot use Stealth) rolls a **Failure (2)**.
- **Decision:** The **GM** chooses the variant: Success moves forward, but the risk is greater (1-3 on the dice).
- **Mechanic:** Success Clock: 2/6. The Failure Clock has 4 empty segments, so the roll is **4k6**.
- **Failure roll result:** Two “1s” and one “3” come up. **Failure Clock: 5/6.**
- _Description:_ **P** were almost caught by a patrol. They had to pretend to be drunken lackeys. The guards let them pass, but sent someone to verify their identities. Things are getting hot.

_Minor rolls changing the situation_

##### Stage 3: Opening the archive lock (Technology)

- **Player C** rolls a **Full Success (6)**.
- **Mechanic:** Success Clock: 3/6. Player C rolls to move the failure clock back. The pool is half of all segments of the success clock (half of 6 gives **3k6**). Each 5-6 moves the clock back.
- **Rollback roll result:** One “6” comes up. **The Failure Clock moves back to 4/6.**
- _Description:_ The lock gives way silently. **P** slip inside and bolt the door, gaining a brief moment to breathe.

_Minor rolls changing the situation_

##### Stage 4: Finding the list among chaotic documents (Investigation)

- **Player D** rolls a **Partial Success (5)**.
- **Mechanic:** Success Clock: 4/6. Failure roll: **4k6** (2 empty segments remain on the clock).
- **Failure roll result:** “2, 4, 2, 5” come up. This means **+2 Failure**.
- **Desperate Move:** The party decides to save themselves. Player D takes on **4 Fatigue and a Scar**. They discard one of the “2s”.
- **Final Failure Clock: 5/6.** (Because of the sacrifice, the clock did not fill.)

_Minor rolls changing the situation_

##### Stage 5: Escape across the rooftops (Athletics)

- **Player A** (cannot use Stealth) rolls a **Full Success (6)**.
- **Mechanic:** Success Clock: 5/6. Player A rolls 3k6 to move the failure clock back.
- **Rollback roll result:** One “5” comes up. **The Failure Clock moves back to 4/6.**
- _Description:_ **P** jump from the archive window onto the neighboring roof. Player A, despite their exhaustion, catches everyone and pulls them up before the guards can line them up with crossbows.

_Minor rolls changing the situation_

##### Stage 6: Losing the pursuit in the slums (Survival)

- **Player B** (cannot use Deception) rolls a **Partial Success (4)**.
- **Mechanic:** Success Clock: 6/6 (**GOAL ACHIEVED**). However, on a partial success, you still roll for failure. 2 empty segments remain, so the roll is **2k6**.
- **Failure roll result:** “1” and “4” come up. The “1” advances the clock by 1 point.
- **Final clock state:** Success 6/6 | Failure 5/6.
- _Description:_ **P** plunge into a maze of narrow streets. Player B successfully misleads the pursuit into a dead-end alley, buying the party time to enter their hideout. They slip into a safe hole moments before the main inquisition force seals off the district with a cordon. They are safe, but the city is now "hot".

#### Sequential narrative across time (long-term mode)

The sequential narrative mechanic can be used not only to play out a single dynamic scene, but also to represent processes lasting weeks or months, such as:

- hiding a person or artifact
- conducting underground activity
- surviving in an occupied city
- protecting a faction from being broken apart
- avoiding detection by the enemy
- building a support network

In this mode:

- one segment of the **success clock** does not represent a single action, but a significant episode in time (for example a raid, an infiltration attempt, a resource crisis, suspicious neighbors, illness, interrogations in the city).
- The **GM** and **P** establish together what the threat looks like in a given stage and what may lead to progress or failure - together they build it up fictionally, telling what is happening.
- The test is made by the **P** whose actions are key in that episode (organizing a hideout, counterintelligence, obtaining food, throwing off the trail, etc.), and they take the lead in the narrative.

Differences compared to a normal sequence:

- Stages describe events, not individual actions.
- **P** may perform one **downtime action** during such a sequence, as long as it is woven into the narrative of the ongoing process.

Example:
Hiding an NPC for 3 months may be one sequence in which the stages include:

- whether the hiding place remains secret (what do you do to secure it?)
- whether an informant gets into the group (how do you think you were infiltrated?)
- whether the NPC can be evacuated during a raid (how do you get them out safely?)
- whether the location can be changed safely (how do you conceal them during the journey?)

Failure should be significant and fitted to the narrative, for example losing the NPC, exposure, a manhunt, imprisonment, the death of someone important, the collapse of an allied faction.

### Pressure (short threads):

- This mechanic is used to play out scenes of lesser importance than **Sequential Narrative/Chase**. It helps resolve shorter threads connecting scenes, move the action from one location to another, and control the world’s local reaction based on the rolls and decisions of **P**.
- Use it when **P** are acting under pressure - they broke into someone’s house, are looking through cabinets behind someone’s back, are running out of time, are searching for someone in danger, etc. - they are accompanied by a **failure clock**, which has as many segments as there are **advancing rolls** +1 - and filling it means narrative consequences.
  - There should usually be 4-6 advancing rolls.
  - By default, **P** should not be able to get out of a situation under pressure unless they achieve full successes, avoid all branches that offer **decisions**, or abandon the action before they manage to interact with everything that could bring them potential value.
- This procedure can be open or hidden. If it is open, **P** should immediately know what consequences will fall on them when the clock fills, or at least what kind they are, for example someone will catch you and call the guards. If it is hidden, the **GM** should still know what is at stake.
- This is a procedure stretched out over time - **P** may move from place to place during it, even without being aware that it is unfolding in the background, but they should be aware of their situation, from which the pressure follows, for example that if they do not hurry, the evidence they are looking for will burn.
- Every test result in this procedure moves the **failure clock** in the following way:
  - critical success: -2 - an empty clock cannot be moved back.
  - full success - nothing
  - partial +1
  - failure +2
  - critical failure: +3
- **Decisions** - these are moments when the **GM** tells **P** that during their action they discover something more, for example another route, a hiding place, etc. - each decision is tied to a question to **P** and a clear note that this is an additional action, for example you see a hidden little door, but you know it does not lead in the direction your trail leads, do you want to check it or just pass it by? - if **P** decide to do something outside the scope of the main task to which the **Alarm Clock** applies, two things happen: the **failure clock** is advanced by 1 and **P** should receive / be able to gain through a roll outside the clock something valuable (information/clue/loot, etc.).
  - During the procedure there should be 1 **decision** for every 2 **rolls advancing** the clock, rounded down - these are the only moments when **P** can additionally advance the **failure clock** beyond the rolls that normally limit its range, so they should be used carefully, keeping in mind their double, deceptive role.
- The **GM** should have the **advancing rolls** and **decisions** tied to the stakes prepared in advance.
  - Improvisation is possible, but harder.
- **P** cannot roll the same **trait** twice during a pressure procedure.
- During the procedure there may be side rolls that do not advance the **failure clock**, such as figuring out the meaning of discovered things (**mind**), establishing knowledge about encountered elements of the world (for example **nature**), attempts by **P** to do something not directly related that does not take much time, etc.
- If **P** do something that clearly wastes time or is completely off-topic, despite being aware of the pressure, first make sure by asking that they know what situation they are in, and then advance the **failure clock** by +1 for each such thing.
- Example:
  - **stakes**: whether they can find the NPC before they die.
  - how quickly they reach the destination by following the trail - **survival**.
  - **decision** - one person’s tracks turn into a narrow alley, but you can see that most of them keep going toward the slums, do you check it or continue? - if they check: clock +1 and they find a gang whistle (evidence and **advantage** for drawing attention away later)
  - how much noise they make when opening the door - **sleight of hand/technology**.
  - whether they manage to draw away part of the gang - **performance/deception**.
  - **decision** - something glints behind the beam supporting the cellar ceiling, it may be worth quite a lot, but you do not know how long it will take to get it out, if you do not take it now, you will never find it again, but if you do, knives may start flying before you reach the place, what do you do? - if they take the treasure: clock +1 and they find 2 **treasures** and several gang outfits (they can blend in with its members).
  - whether they sneak close enough - **stealth** or **performance/deception** (preferably a **trait** that has not been used earlier) if they have gang outfits (on a success, they can get right up to the key NPC).
- If the procedure concerns something that does not need to have a clear ending - like: how much information can we pull from the archive before they realize we are here - **P** may stop it at any moment so as not to keep risking the clock filling.
- If during the procedure the decisions of **P** surprise the GM, they may swap an **advancing roll/decision** to better fit the current narrative.
- If the scene starts dragging or the **GM** feels overloaded - shorten the procedure, reduce the number of **advancing rolls**, and move to the consequences.

### Procedure for searching a room under pressure

Searching rooms in _Haunted Matter_ is meant to provide tension, risk, and a sense of progress. Every roll is a chance to uncover a mystery – but also a step closer to being detected. This procedure allows the **GM** to prepare a scene with sensible structure and consequences without writing item lists in advance.

Use it in scenes where **P** have sneaked somewhere in, are acting under cover or behind someone’s back / unnoticed, there is a risk of being discovered, etc.

#### How to use it - one P

- Define what kind of room it is (for example a guard’s office, a cell, a bedroom).
- Choose or roll **4–5 objects** from the object table.
- Assign contents to each object – first place **plot-relevant information** there, and roll/choose the remaining results from the table or add your own.
- Add an **Alarm Clock**. The clock has a number of segments equal to the number of objects +1, and **P** know this, so they can back off at the right moment or take the risk. By default, **P** should not be able to check all objects unless they roll only full successes or skip all secrets.
- Determine the **NPC reaction** to being caught (reaction table).
- **P** begins interaction with the room using the **move** **I Act Behind Someone’s Back** or **I Act in Hiding**. The **GM** may enforce that only 1 of them is available in a given scene.
- Each object is searched with appropriate tests, which may advance the **Alarm Clock**. If an object requires more than one roll, each of them may advance the clock. Example links between tests and action types:
  - Locked, without a trap – **sleight of hand**/**technology**.
  - Looking for documents, rummaging through junk – **investigation**.
  - Small items, details, covered things, looking into a wardrobe – **perception**.
  - Moving, prying, lifting – **athletics**.
  - Reading traces – **survival**.
  - Mechanical puzzles, riddles – **mind**.
  - You may add no test at all to an object if its contents are meant to be easily accessible.
- Traps
  - They work according to their own rules.
  - Only the second trap test, for disarming it, advances the **Alarm Clock** (the first cannot be triggered without the second, according to trap rules).
  - If a trap protects the contents of an object, no test is ever needed anymore to find those contents.
- Secrets
  - A secret is not a separate object, but a hidden layer of one of the objects (for example a false bottom, a built-in drawer, a false wall).
  - An object can contain only 1 secret.
  - The **GM** adds secrets to objects personally - no more than 1-2 per procedure.
  - A secret is not revealed automatically. After discovering the object’s obvious contents, the **GM** informs **P** that the object may contain something more and asks whether **P** risk searching further.
  - Discovering a secret requires an additional test (**perception** or **investigation**), which follows all the rules of searching under pressure and may advance the **Alarm Clock**.
  - If the secret is hard to access, the **GM** may require another test of an appropriate **trait**.
  - Only the first test for a secret, the one to find it, advances the **Alarm Clock**; if there is a second one, it does not touch the clock.
  - Secrets are the only part of the procedure that increases the number of possible actions **P** can take beyond the number of segments in the **Alarm Clock** - and that is exactly what they are for - so they should be used with their nature in mind, though they do not necessarily have to hide something related to the plot.
- When **P** uses the **I Act Behind Someone’s Back move**
  - they do not have to roll for tests assigned to objects if nothing protects them - for example they are not locked, hidden, or protected by a trap - they simply get the information/loot right away - **P** still has a limited number of interactions in that case
  - however, when they do test something (for example a lock/secret): a partial success gives them **2 fatigue** and a failure gives no access to the object’s contents, but **P** may repeat the attempt, using up their interaction point resulting from the **move** being used.
    - traps are the exception - they are handled according to their own rules.
- If an entire building is to be searched, for example a house that **P** broke into, the procedure stays the same, the objects just need to be divided into rooms.
- Not every test made during this procedure has to advance the **Alarm Clock** - if something does not take time, does not affect the surroundings, etc., note that it does not touch the clock (for example **P** finds a letter and rolls **mind** to see whether they connect what far-reaching consequences what they read may have) - such tests usually concern specific information added by the GM, not randomly generated things.

#### Team searching

If more than 1 **P** takes part in the procedure, use the rules below.

- It always works according to the rules of the **I Act in Hiding move** (partial success advances the **Alarm Clock** by 1, failure by 2).
- The **GM** presents the names of the objects in the room/rooms.
- The team chooses a leading **P**. The leader chooses an object first, makes the test, and drops out of the procedure.
- The test result indicates the next acting **P**, counting clockwise from the current one:
  - full success: +1
  - partial success: +2
  - failure: +3
  - When indicating, skip **P** who have already acted or dropped out of the procedure.
- Once during the whole procedure, the team may swap the currently acting **P** for another one, even if that one has already acted or dropped out of the procedure.
  - The swapped-out one drops out of the procedure and does not make the test in this cycle.
  - The one doing the swap receives **fatigue**; if they already acted earlier, they also drop out of the procedure.
  - The swap may happen after the nature of the object is revealed, but before the test is made.
  - If the object requires more than one roll, the swap must happen before the first one, unless it is a secret.
- If all **P** have already made one action each (except the swapped one), and the **Alarm Clock** has not been filled, the procedure may begin another cycle with the same order.
- The procedure may be interrupted at any moment — every next roll is a risk that **P** consciously agree to take.

### Money

#### General Rules

- **Coins are not tracked** — the **P** share a **wealth level**, representing their lifestyle and purchasing power.
- To buy anything, the **P** must visit a **market**.
- A **market** can be visited only once per stay in a settlement.
- If **P** are in hiding, their **wealth level** is reduced by 1 when using **market**.
- In small settlements (villages, hamlets), the maximum usable **wealth level** is **With a Coin** — higher levels are treated as **With a Coin** while there.
- **Expensive purchases** (e.g., heavy armor, wagon) are **orders** — ready after 3 days.
- **Scrolls, elixirs, arcanas, and Blood of the Earth** are found through story events, not bought — they’re too rare except in special places like faction vaults or grand markets.
- Renting a room automatically includes food.
- Prices are fixed unless the **GM** explicitly allows negotiation.

#### Wealth Levels

- **To advance** from one **wealth level** to the next, the party must deposit the amount of **treasures** listed for that tier.
  - Depositing **treasures** can be split into multiple visits.
  - Deposits are only possible in settlements that have a bank, during a **market** visit.
  - Advancing to the next **wealth level** costs a **downtime action** - signing paperwork.
  - You can deposit not only **treasures**, but also others goods, but they can only be reclaimed in the same location where they were stored, unless they’re interchangeable resources (e.g. Earth Blood).
  - **Treasures** deposited for previous levels do not count toward the requirement of a new level.
  - You can only advance one level at a time and skipping levels is not allowed even if you have enough resources.
- **P** use their **wealth level** through checks.
- Wealth Level Equivalent Withdrawal — **P** may collectively reduce their **wealth level** by one step to receive a number of **treasures** equal to the amount required to reach the level they are dropping to. For example, **P** choose to drop from **Common** to **Poor** and receive 3 **treasures**. You cannot drop below the **Poor** level this way. An equivalent withdrawal may be made in any agglomeration that has a bank and requires a **downtime** action. You cannot both withdraw an equivalent and advance to a higher **wealth level**, or vice versa, during the same stay in an agglomeration.

##### 1. Barely Surviving

- The **P** are starving, can’t afford an inn or any purchase.

##### 2. Poor _(starting level)_ (requires depositing **3 Treasures**)

- The **P** eat but can’t afford luxuries or lodging.
- May obtain **2 rations** and **1 common item**.

##### 3. Common (requires depositing **5 Treasures**)

- May obtain **2 common items**, **3 rations**, and **1 healing/material/component/resource item**.
- Can afford the worst inn room.
- As a group:
  - buy a two-wheeled cart (6 slots) with a donkey — it’s not for riding, only for carrying gear → **wealth –1**.

##### 4. With a Coin (requires depositing **8 Treasures**)

- May obtain **3 common items**, **6 rations**, and **2 healing/material/component/resource items**.
- Can afford a decent inn room.
- As a group:
  - merge **1 exotic material** (e.g., Hydra scales) with an item.
  - buy 1 expensive item (e.g. heavy armor, wagon with an ox (10 slots, speeds up travel by 1.5 days per hex)) → wealth –1.
  - hire **1 person** for a simple task.

##### 5. Doing Well (requires depositing **12 Treasures**)

- May obtain **5 common items**, **9 rations**, and **2 healing/material/component/resource items**.
- As a group:
  - merge **2 exotic materials** into items.
  - buy 1 expensive item (e.g. heavy armor, wagon with horses (10 slots, speeds up travel by 1.5 days per hex)).
  - hire **up to 5 people** for a specific task.
  - gain **1 major favor** (e.g., from an executive authority) → **wealth –1**.
  - gain **1 contact** within **privileged authority** → **wealth –1**.
- Can afford an apartment.

##### 6. Wealthy (requires depositing **20 Treasures**)

- As above, but:
  - no limit on common items or food (limited only by inventory); **3 healing/material/component/resource items**.
  - As a group:
    - merge **3 exotic materials**.
    - gain a **favor from privileged authority** → **wealth –1**.
    - hire a **mercenary unit** → **wealth –1**.
    - buy **1 property** → **wealth –1**.
    - fund **a public event**, e.g. tournament → **wealth –1**.
- Can afford a luxury suite.

##### 7. Magnate (requires depositing **30 Treasures**)

- As Wealthy, plus:
  - gain **a favor from dominant authority** → **wealth –1**.
  - buy **a major property** → **wealth –1**.
  - **found an organization** (up to 30 members initially) → **wealth –1**.
  - use spies to make **faction moves** (GM-approved) → **wealth –1**.

#### Treasures

- Collecting **treasures** increases wealth.
- A **treasure** = any valuable physical item (e.g., jewels, goblets, tapestries).
- A treasure takes **½ slot** (light item).
- **Bribes** are always paid in **treasures**.
- One **treasure** equals one month of provisions for one average person.

#### Goods Table

- Buying goods/services for **treasures** happens during a **market visit**, outside the limits of the party’s wealth level.
- Buying elixirs, scrolls, or arcanas is nearly impossible except at rare markets, though they can always be sold.

### Downtime

#### **Standard actions**

1. **Read a book** – you gain 1 important piece of plot information.
2. **Build a contact** (roll **influence**):
   - Full Success: **privileged authority**.
   - Partial success: **executive authority**.
3. **Proper rest** – you recover **lost trait dice** from one group.
4. **Throw a party** – roll **persuasion/influence** to improve **reputation**.
5. **Send a message** – it will reach a civilized place.

9. **Obtain a rare item** – through the **market** or connections.
10. **Gain a development point** – only **once per real month of play**.
11. **Gain information from the underworld** – roll **persuasion/influence/deception**.
12. **Plant a rumor** – roll **deception**.
13. **Intimidate someone** – roll **intimidation**, you gain a favor (**executive authority**), but lose **reputation**.
14. **Commission equipment repairs** – costs **treasure**.
15. **Romance** (→ details below).
16. **Get treatment from a barber-surgeon** - removing the effects of scars, mutations, etc. - costs 3 **treasures**.
17. **Deposit treasures in a bank** - raise your wealth level - banks are only available in the largest settlements.
18. **Another idea** – propose it to the GM.

#### **Romance**

- To seduce someone once, use the **I seduce** **move**.
- To develop an **emotional bond**, gather **3–5 bond points** (the difficulty is set by the GM).
  - Each point = **full success** on a roll using a chosen **trait**, described in a romantic context.
  - You may use the same **rait**, but not the same approach.
  - **Critical success** = +1 point; **Critical failure** = –1 point.
- Each bond point can be used once as **advantage** on a future roll.
- **Negative bonds** are possible.

### Reputation

Before you even begin playing, ask your **P** to imagine that they are members of a court in the presence of a king. Would they risk laughing at him and insulting him? Of course not. Characters played by **P** who are part of a similar world feel exactly the same way. Any swaggering rogue who mocks a ruler, disregards their authority, and is not specifically appointed to do so as a fool with social permission, skilled enough to balance words on the edge of their own life, ends up with their head on a pike. The characters of **P** do not have plot armor, and there are few better ways to ruin your day than by stepping on the tail of an influential person, so it would be good if **P** took that into account in their roleplay. It simply ruins the atmosphere of the game.

**P** must know that one must bow to a king, or suffer the consequences. If someone jokes about the empress in the presence of her court and fails to do it subtly, they may lose their head or be exiled. When someone is a recognized member of a given group, they may ask for favors normally unavailable to others. If you want to find a home, society cannot be afraid of you. The reputation system below is meant to help the **GM** handle situations like these.

Assign each faction/community in the world a level of regard it holds for **P** and track it using the table below. The starting level is 0. You may also track their global reputation rating.

| Level | Name         | Meaning                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ----- | ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| +5    | honored      | the authorities will fulfill one request / grant one favor of any kind, as long as it does not endanger them / directly go against their interests.                             |
| +4    | recognized   | the authorities will fulfill one limited request / grant one favor, as long as it does not endanger them / directly go against their interests.                                 |
| +3    | influential  | **P** may spend money as if they were one wealth level higher.                                                                                                                  |
| +2    | known        | Access to rare goods at the **market** (for example an elixir), as a party cost.                                                                                                |
| +1    | recognizable | **P** may receive an answer to one important troubling question tied to each sphere of influence - within reason - from friendly inhabitants of those areas, without any rolls. |
| 0     | neutral      | normal wealth level.                                                                                                                                                            |
| -1    | outsider     | **P** must spend money as if they were one wealth level lower.                                                                                                                  |
| -2    | suspicious   | **P** must spend money as if they were two wealth levels lower.                                                                                                                 |
| -3    | distrustful  | **downtime** actions cannot be performed.                                                                                                                                       |
| -4    | hostile      | banishment and pursuit within their sphere of influence, if they know about the presence of **P** on their land, with a reward for delivering them to the authorities.          |
| -5    | hatred       | pursuit even beyond their borders without knowing where **P** are, with a significant reward for their living heads.                                                            |

Each consecutive level of positive or negative reputation also brings all the consequences of the previous levels.

For the **GM** - ask **P** to track the consequences of their reputation level in a given area themselves - only tell them when that level changes - you already have enough on your plate.

#### Influencing reputation

- Every mission successfully completed for a faction/community raises the reputation level of **P** by 1.
- If **P** unknowingly do a faction a favor (for example kill someone they were looking for or help their interests), their reputation level rises by 1.
- If a mission performed for a faction ends in failure, their reputation level drops by 1.
- If a mission performed for a faction has both good and bad effects for it, **P** makes an **influence** test to determine the society’s final attitude toward the results of **P**’s actions.
  - full success - reputation rises.
  - success with cost - reputation stays unchanged.
  - failure - reputation falls.
- If **P** do something directly against the interests of a faction - even unknowingly - their reputation level drops by 1.
  - examples:
    - They are proven to have murdered an innocent person.
    - They are caught stealing.
    - They make a deal in which the faction loses.
    - They publicly belittle values or deities worshipped by the faction.
    - They treat ordinary inhabitants with contempt or disrespect.
    - Other similar things.
- Gaining positive reputation is intentionally, and for obvious reasons, harder.
- +2 for a very great success / -2 for blatant betrayal.

### Respecting Authority and Social Values

#### Types of authority

Authority can be divided into - - **natural** - for example the survival instinct - **dominant** - for example a king, an empress - **privileged** - for example a bishop, an officer - **executive** - for example a guard, a tax collector - **threatening** - for example a criminal

When authority gives an order and **P** does not want to obey it, they must make an **influence** roll.

Authority cannot afford to lose its standing, and it is hard to resist its influence, which is why **influence** rolls in its presence may end with consequences even on a **full success**.

Defying authority becomes harder the greater its authority is, meaning the higher it stands in the division above.

If **P** has a strong narrative opposition to a given authority, they receive **advantage** on **influence** rolls against it. **Advantages** and **disadvantages** can be gained in a similar way depending on the current favor of the authority.

If **P** is openly hostile toward the values of a faction, for example spits on an altar in the presence of people who consider it holy, or directly insults the king, **P** immediately lose 2 reputation levels and executive authority is called to capture them. If there is none nearby, **P** have a chance to flee. If they do not do so and do not surrender willingly after the guards arrive, a clash with the appropriate forces takes place. If **P** lose such a confrontation, they are thrown into a guarded cell, and all their equipment is immediately liquidated to pay rewards/compensation to the guards / their families whom **P** injured. If they win, they know that the only thing they can do is flee the area of influence of the faction they offended. Either way, their reputation level toward that faction becomes -4.

If **P**’s outfit does not fit the event - they will not be let in (unless some circumstances allow it).

#### Natural authority

If **P** do something absolutely stupid with obvious catastrophic consequences, like murdering people during a holy ecstasy, they are stopped by **natural authority**, meaning the body’s primal impulses, such as the survival instinct. Fear of being torn apart with bare hands by a crowd of religious fanatics effectively stops them. Some, in a sudden fit of self-preservation, restrain the others. It is extremely hard to resist the commands of natural authority. This is a sort of **GM** lockpick against actions by **P** that openly destroy the narrative - like killing merchants and casting spells into a crowd. Whether this rule fits the situation is always the GM’s decision.

When the **GM** decides that **P**’s action is extremely illogical, contrary to the survival instinct, or socially impossible, they may force them to make a **will** test against **natural authority**.

- **Tier 1: Minor obstacle** _(for example a shopkeeper begs for their life, there are a few witnesses around)_ → Roll **1k6** against a threshold of 5+.
- **Tier 2: Significant consequences** _(for example the authorities will hunt **P**, a crowd will throw itself at them, they are acting against their own values)_ → Roll **2k6**, success only on 11+.
- **Tier 3: Extreme risk** _(for example publicly casting spells in a state that forbids magic, murdering people in front of the royal guard, destroying a holy place)_ → Roll **3k6**, success only on 16+.

**If the test fails, P is unable to take the action, held back by their own survival instinct.** They may try to come up with another strategy or act in a less obvious way.

If **P** passes the test - apart from the obvious consequences - they lose **reputation** on a scale set by the GM. In the case of tier 2 and 3 - they also receive a **madness point**.

This mechanic is meant to protect the **GM** against **P** who openly ruin the narrative through their actions.

##### Actions inconsistent with character

If **P** commits an act blatantly contrary to their identity, oath, professed values, or the way they have played the character so far (for example a paladin kills a defenseless child, a priest of the Journey of Souls binds a soul, a defender of the innocent conducts torture), they draw a **mental status**.

#### Tests Against Dominant Authority

Any act of defiance immediately reduces the party’s reputation by 2 and inflicts **fatigue** on **P** as an appropriate effect of stress, because they understand they are getting themselves into trouble.

When, for example, a guard orders **P** to bow to the king sitting behind him on the throne, and **P** wants to defy him, before their action becomes reality, they make an **influence** test.

- full success - **P** does not submit to the order, and their confidence makes the authority lose some of its resonance, but there is conflict in the air and the whole group receives an additional **disadvantage** to social tests in the faction’s area of influence until they improve their reputation by at least one point. The king may say something like: _Let us see what these savages have to say before I have them kicked out_.
- success with cost - **P** does not submit to the order, but there is strong conflict in the air and the whole group is immediately driven away from the authority’s presence. If they resist, a clash occurs.
- failure - **P** submits to the force of authority and must behave as the authority demands.

#### Tests Against Privileged Authority

Any act of defiance immediately reduces the party’s reputation by 1 and inflicts **fatigue** on **P** as an appropriate effect of stress, because they understand they are getting themselves into trouble.

If, for example, a bishop requires **P** to treat the place of his worship with respect, and they say they are telling some filthy joke, before their action becomes reality, they must make an **influence** roll.

- full success - **P** does not submit to the authority and their confidence makes the authority lose some of its resonance, but there is conflict in the air. The bishop may say something like: _May god forgive me for tolerating your presence for his sake. Speak, what do you come with before I lose patience_.
- success with cost - **P** does not submit to the order, but there is strong conflict in the air and the whole group is immediately driven away from the authority’s presence. If they resist, a clash occurs.
- failure - **P** submits to the force of authority and must behave as the authority demands.

#### Tests Against Executive Authority

Defiance against executive authority does not lower the party’s reputation unless it is expressed through direct disrespect. Otherwise, the party’s reputation drops by 1.

If, for example, a guard tells **P** to surrender their weapon before entering the castle, and they refuse, they must make an **influence** test.

- **Full success** – **P** does not submit to the order, and their confidence makes the guard lose his resonance for a moment. He hesitates, but may then pass the matter to a higher authority, or say something like: _Fine, but do not blame me later if the ruler orders your beheading._
- **Success with cost** – **P** does not submit, but the guard immediately becomes aggressive and calls for support, which leads to **P** being removed from the place or to a clash, or he orders them to pay a bribe / do something in return - whatever fits the GM.
- **Failure** – **P** gives in and must **behave according to the order**, though they may later try to get around the rules in another way.

#### Tests Against Threatening Authority

Threatening authority is authority built by force by someone who is threatening us. Defiance against it is assumed, so it never causes reputation loss.

If, for example, 5 knife-wielders order **P** to come with them while surrounding them in a tavern, and they say they laugh in their faces, before they do it they must make an **influence** roll.

- **Full success** – **P** does not submit to the order, and their confidence makes the bandits lose their resonance. In addition, **P** gains **advantage** on social tests against them. They may try to negotiate with them.
- **Success with cost** – **P** does not submit immediately, but they know they have only bought themselves a little time. They have one chance to get out of the situation - they must come up with it. On failure, they give in and go with the bandits, though they may later try to get out of trouble in another way.
- **Failure** – **P** gives in and must behave according to the order, though they may later try to get out of trouble in another way.

## Crafting

- Creating items is done during a long rest, which means it restores 2 fewer **fatigue**
- When a failure causes **P** to lose a **critical injuries die**, it represents a costly mistake at work - chemical burns, spontaneous combustion, an explosion, etc.
- The **GM** and **P**, in cooperation with the GM, may create **their own** elixirs/wonders of Jaruga/arcana/etc.

### Alchemy

**P** may create elixirs. To do so, they must:

- Pay 2 **components**.
- Roll **arcana**:
  - Have at least level 3 in the **arcana trait**.
  - Critical success - you create 2 copies of the chosen elixir - agree it with the GM.
  - Full success - they succeed and choose which elixir they create.
  - Partial success -
    - They succeed, but roll which elixir they created.
    - Or they choose which elixir they create, but receive 3 **fatigue**.
  - Failure - they fail, waste the **component**, and lose a **critical injuries die** (if they have none - they die).
- Alchemy may trigger a magical catastrophe.
- **P** may create **scrolls** and **runes** - but this requires 3 **components** and a full success - on a partial success, nothing happens.
- **P** may create arcana - this requires 6 components and a full success - on a partial success, nothing happens - a critical success means **P** recovers 3 components.
- Every **critical failure** - two ones on the dice - results in advancing the **Corruption Clock**

---

### Herbalism or apothecary

- **P** may create **healing items**. To do so, they must:
  - Have at least level 2 in **herbalism**.
  - Spend a **resource**.
  - Roll **herbalism/apothecary**:
    - Critical success - you create a combination of two chosen effects, ignoring their costs / the created item has 2 uses, or **P** creates 2 copies of the same item.
    - Full success - they succeed and choose which **healing item** they create.
    - Partial success -
      - They succeed, but roll which **healing item** they created.
      - Or they choose which **brew** they create, but receive 2 **fatigue**.
    - Failure - they fail, waste the **resource**, and lose a **critical injuries die** (if they have none - they die).
- **P** may create **brews** - exactly the same as when creating healing items.
- **P** may combine items with **exotic materials** - however, this requires a full success - on a partial success, nothing happens - a critical success returns the **resource**.
- **P** may create **Wonders of Jaruga** - exactly the same as when creating elixirs - but this requires level 3 **herbalism** and 2 **resources**.
- Every **critical failure** - two ones on the dice - results in advancing the **Corruption Clock**

---

### Creating technology

- **P** may create **tactical gear**. To do so, they must:
  - Have at least level 2 in **technology**.
  - Spend a **material**.
  - Roll **technology**:
    - Critical success - you create a combination of two chosen effects, ignoring their costs / the created item has 2 uses, or **P** creates 2 copies of the same gear.
    - Full success - they succeed and choose which **tactical gear** they create.
    - Partial success -
      - They succeed, but roll which **tactical gear** they created.
      - Or they choose which **tactical gear** they create, but receive 2 **fatigue**.
    - Failure - they fail, waste the **material**, and lose a **critical injuries die** (if they have none - they die).
- **P** may create ordinary utility items - such as a shovel or rope - exactly the same as when creating **tactical gear** - but on a partial success they always choose which item they create and receive 1 **fatigue**.
- **P** may make/repair weapons/armor/items - exactly the same as when creating **tactical gear** - but:
  - on a partial success they always choose which item they create and roll a **physical status**.
  - it costs a downtime action instead of a long rest.
  - creating heavy armor requires 4 **materials**.
- **P** may create **technology** - exactly the same as when creating **tactical gear** - but they must have at least level 3 **technology**, it costs them 2 **materials**, and they must possess an **energy sphere** to power their tool.
- Every **critical failure** - two ones on the dice - results in advancing the **Corruption Clock** as a result of electrical shorts / the action of chemicals.

## Interactive Elements

### Traps

- Traps are left behind to kill or effectively stop someone. They are meant to inspire fear, so they should always be lethal or inflict statuses.
- When **P** interacts with something protected by a trap, they immediately realize there is danger - however, an **investigation** test shows how much they discovered:
  - Making this test triggers the sequence: **investigation** -> disarming/opening - **P** cannot withdraw from the disarming/opening roll after making the **investigation** roll related to the trap - it is treated as one action initiated by the original decision - however, they may completely give up the **investigation** test so as not to take the risk, but that also closes off their chance to discover what the trap is guarding.
  - Results of the **investigation** test:
    - Full success - **P** understands the mechanism and has no **disadvantages** to disarming it.
    - Partial success - **P** finds the trap but has **disadvantage** to disarming it.
    - Failure: **P** finds the trap but has double **disadvantage** to disarming it.
- You cannot help with understanding the trap’s mechanism or with disarming/opening it.
- Disarming/opening is treated as one test.
- **P** disarms the trap with the **technology/sleight of hand trait** -> they take the appropriate **status** / roll on **critical injuries** according to their rules, and on failure they die (lose a destiny point).
- If the trap went off and was not single-use, another attempt to disarm it now requires only a **technology/sleight of hand** roll and is always made with double **disadvantage**
- If **P** enters a trapped area during combat and does not know it is there -> **survival** test. If they enter and know it is there -> **athletics** test (jumping over/avoiding it) with **advantage**.
  - full success - **P** neatly avoids the hazard and may keep acting
  - partial success - **P** triggers the trap (physical status), but may keep acting afterward, or does not trigger the hazard, but keeps acting with double **disadvantage**.
  - failure - **P** triggers the trap -> k12 damage.

---

### Puzzles and riddles

If **P** have no idea how to solve a riddle or obstacle prepared by the GM, they may try to overcome it by making a **group mind test** (the version with partial success).

- Success - **P** solve the riddle.
- Partial success - **P** solve the riddle, but a cost appears (chosen by the GM):
  - a trap activates (if there is one),
    - if the trap can affect only one **P**, it affects the one with the worst result.
  - they lose important information or part of the reward,
  - they advance a danger clock
  - the alarm goes off / they are noticed
- Failure - **P** do not solve the riddle, and one of the consequences of a partial success occurs.

## For the GM – How to Run

- The following advice is meant to help you create the best possible **grim-dark, low-magic dramatic experience** for the **P**.
- Most important: when preparing anything, remember — this is the **P story**. They matter most!

---

### Session 0

Before you start playing, organize one relaxed meeting where you simply talk about each other’s expectations. As the GM, you should use this moment to present **P** with the outline of the campaign you are going to run for them - set the frame, talk about the world to ground **P** in it, say what will be possible and what probably will not, ask for character backstories, etc.

Also listen to **P** - this is very important, because this will be their story - and align your intentions with their expectations. For example, **P** may have hoped to play a powerful mage, and in this system that is rather impossible, so this needs to be said clearly so that no one feels disappointed later.

Session 0 is also the ideal place to talk about potential boundaries. Tell **P** what difficult topics your campaign will touch on and ask whether they agree to that - for example brutal violence, slavery, racism, discrimination, mass graves, slaughter, etc. If there is no agreement on any of these elements, you must either change something or part ways with the **P** who does not want it.

This is the moment of synchronization. Making sure the game will flow smoothly later instead of grinding with conflicts at the table.

Remember - the main goal is to have a good time together - after Session 0, everyone should leave convinced that this is exactly what awaits them when you meet for the actual game.

### Let P participate in creating the details of the world

When you present the world, leave room for **P** to expand it by asking questions like:

- What is the strangest thing you see in this inn?
- How did your character meet this NPC?
- Why does this place make you feel afraid?
- What in this scenery reminds you of your childhood?
- what does the room you entered look like?

### How to build an NPC?

Below you will find the fastest way to create a believable NPC ready to be used in an adventure.

Write down:

- name
- who are they?
- what do they want? - one goal.
- one sentence describing how they look - including one distinctive trait (for example constantly scratching the back of their neck).
- what one weakness do they have that can be used to influence them? - one sentence - not required, but helpful.

Example:

| **Character** | **Who they are** | **Description and distinctive trait**                                                                     | **Motivations**                                                                | **What they avoid**           |
| ------------- | ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------- |
| **Keledan**   | Hermit           | A gaunt man cracked by toxins, who listens to the vibrations of the earth before answering. Slightly mad. | Wants the gigatornado to cleanse the world – even at the cost of his own life. | People asking about the past. |

- You do not need anything more - an NPC created according to the rules above is completely sufficient to use them for the purpose you intend and to improvise if needed.
- This method will even let you create NPCs on the fly during a session.

### Gambling

The favorite gambling game in the world of Zaya is a group vs group game called: Sequences.

The entry fee is 1 **treasure** - so the starting pot for two teams is 2 **treasures**. The entry fee can be negotiated with tests of appropriate **traits**.
**P** make a group test, but each participant may roll using one of 4 **traits**: **mind, intuition, deception, sleight of hand**.
You must arrange the dice into two identical sequences, for example with 4 **P**: 1-2-3-4 and 1-2-3-4 or 6-5-4-3 and 6-5-4-3. The sequence must have as many digits as there are players.
Partial success → change one of your dice or someone else’s by 1 pip for 4 **fatigue**.  
Full success → change two dice by 1 pip or one die by 2 pips (your own or someone else’s, your choice) for 2 **fatigue**.
If there are:

- 2 sequences → victory
- 1 sequence → the **GM** rolls 2k6 for the opponents’ morale; if the highest result is 3, the opponents give up and **P** win; otherwise **P** must add a **treasure** to the pot to continue or lose, because the opponents have already added theirs.
- none → defeat
  The people who achieved the best and worst result describe how the group handled the round narratively.
  If another round takes place, no **P** may test a **trait** they already tested in previous rounds.
  Only **P** make the rolls, but narratively they are playing against another team.
  **Treasures** do not have to be the only currency in this game - some in Zaya lose their freedom or life in it.

### Unofficial Entrances

Sometimes **P** want to enter a guarded area, and you do not have any options prepared in advance for how the fiction could properly allow it. In such situations, you may use the lists below - you can also simply use them as inspiration.

They are meant to help build a shared narrative of how **P** got into the guarded area.

#### STEP 1 - How many unofficial entrances are there?

**P** make a group **investigation** test:

- failure - **P** draw 1 entrance and each must pay **fatigue**
- partial success - **P** draw 2 entrances and may uncover one more if each pays **fatigue**.
- full success - **P** draw 4 entrances.

#### STEP 2 - What kind of entrance is it?

For each entrance, roll k6

1. **Hidden** - sewer, cellar, tunnel, back room - **investigation/stealth**.
2. **Physical** - door, grate, window, hatch - **athletics/dexterity**.
3. **Semi-legal** - doorman, pass, ritual, password - **deception/intimidation**.
4. **Social** - invitation, crowd, confusion, group - **persuasion/influence**.
5. **Unusual** - magic, workaround, structural risk - **perception/survival**.
6. **Human** - mistake, routine, negligence - **mind/intuition**.

#### STEP 3 - What specific entrances are these?

#### 1. Hidden (k12)

They physically exist, but **are not meant to be used**.

1. Technical passage connecting two buildings
2. Cellar with an exit to a neighboring property
3. Tunnel under the floor/ground
4. Former back area, now a “dead zone”
5. Old refuse chute
6. Passage through a warehouse that should not be passable
7. Double wall with a service space
8. Unused staircase
9. Buried corridor with a narrow gap
10. Inspection opening leading farther than it should
11. Connected cellars between buildings
12. Hidden door camouflaged by furnishings

---

#### 2. Forceful (k12)

It can be passed, but **something has to be overcome or destroyed**.

1. Spare door with a weak lock
2. Grate rusted from the inside
3. Window high above the ground
4. Technical hatch with a seal
5. Emergency door with an alarm
6. Cellar window with bars
7. Metal warehouse door
8. Door locked “just for a moment”
9. Gate requiring strength or tools
10. Blocked emergency stairs
11. Covered service passage
12. Structure that can be broken through at the cost of noise

---

#### 3. Semi-legal (k12)

Officially controlled, but it is possible to slip through.

1. Tired guard
2. Pass that no one checks closely
3. Password known from routine
4. Ritual / procedure performed mechanically
5. Entrance “staff only”
6. Attendance list without face verification
7. Inspection focused on one direction
8. Shift-change entrance
9. Gates opened in batches
10. “Conditionally accessible” zone
11. Procedure no one likes to enforce
12. Authorization based on appearances

---

#### 4. Social (k12)

The entrance exists because **people stop paying attention**.

1. Crowd at a side entrance
2. Official event
3. Organizational confusion
4. Queue without control
5. Carrying equipment
6. Practice or fake evacuation
7. Argument drawing attention away
8. Gathering of onlookers
9. Guests entering in waves
10. Shift-change chaos
11. Pressure to “not make a problem”
12. Constant movement

---

#### 5. Unusual (k12)

Uncommon, but **still believable in the world of HM**.

1. Short-lived magical passage
2. Workaround through an unstable structure
3. Entrance requiring breaking safety rules
4. Passage through a danger zone
5. Structure that will only hold once
6. Place requiring precise timing
7. Entrance accessible only from the proper direction
8. Area under prohibition, but unguarded
9. Entrance requiring sacrificing equipment
10. Can be passed/jumped to from another place
11. Shortcut known only to a few
12. Entrance that works at the cost of health or fatigue

---

#### 6. Human (k12)

Entrances resulting purely from human behavior.

1. Guard steps away for a moment
2. Someone forgot to lock it
3. Routine cutting of procedures
4. Employee doing something “like always”
5. Shift that is late
6. No one checks the rear
7. Someone assumes “someone else already did it”
8. Fatigue and inattention
9. Someone does not want conflict
10. Someone thinks **P** have permission
11. Superior turns a blind eye
12. Mistake that will be noticed later

#### STEP 4 - How do **P** use that entrance?

When **P** decide on one of the available options or split up to try more than one, the **P** assigned to a specific entrance describes a believable way in which they exploit the opportunity and makes a test of the fitting **trait**, based on whose result they and the **GM** together continue the story of how they managed to get inside and at what cost.

A partial success on such a roll usually means:

- advancing the recognition/failure clock
- a bribe
- a **fatigue** cost
- an appropriate fictional change to the detriment of **P** - for example the guard says **P** may enter, but he will accompany them
- other **GM** ideas best serving the narrative

If **P** found more than one entrance and failed at one, they may try the others, though the failure clock should already be working against them, reducing freedom of movement and applying pressure.

This step fits perfectly with a **sequential narrative**, if **P** are planning to infiltrate some space and this will be the first roll advancing the clocks.

### Building Fights

- When, as the GM, you find yourself in a situation where you need to quickly prepare a confrontation, or are simply looking for inspiration, you can use the ready-made solutions below, which will help you build a dynamic, engaging, and memorable challenge for **P** in a moment.
- The fight types below obviously do not exhaust all possibilities, but they show how many scenarios can be handled.

#### Assumptions

- Never introduce confrontations that have no narrative justification.
- Combat is a risky last resort and **P** should almost always have the option of choosing another narrative path.
- Practically every fight should have an objective - a goal beyond simply eliminating enemies - your **P** do not come for a slaughter, they are trying to achieve something.
- But do not be afraid to improvise fights if necessary - if **P** are asking for an aggressive reaction from the world through their actions - show them that they need to be careful about what they do.
- Fights are hard, brutal, and deadly - **P** practically has no chance alone against a crowd of enraged peasants, even if they were a soldier all their life - tell **P** this directly so they know the stakes, and remember it yourself when planning skirmishes.

#### Example fight types

##### 1. Protection

- Goal: Defend an NPC, object, or place from X damage, transport something to the other side of the map.
- Mechanics: If X successes hit the NPC / object, **P** lose.
- Rolling 1-2 on the **main die** means the enemy always tries, as much as possible, to deal damage to the protected object/person.

Examples:

- Defending a caravan from bandits.
- Guarding a magical ritual from being interrupted.

---

##### 2. Difficult terrain

- Goal: Win a confrontation in severely unfavorable weather/geographical/etc. conditions.
- Mechanics:
  - Rolling 2-3 on the **main die** (5-6 for enemies) means consequences appropriate to the scenery: splashed by lava from a geyser, slipping on ice, getting tangled in thorny vines, being knocked over by a crowd, etc., which changes the fiction in a way both sides must take into account or inflicts damage.
  - Both **P** and enemies can change the fiction with their actions by using this difficult terrain to their advantage - for example pushing someone into a geyser.
- This kind of confrontation can often be combined with others.

Examples:

- A clash among erupting geysers.
- Falling into a trap in a mountain gorge.
- Fighting in a crowd.

---

##### 3. Holding position

- Goal: Gain and hold a key position.
- Mechanics:
  - The key zone / adjacent zones - **P** must push all enemies out of the area.
  - If the enemy pushes all **P** out of the position, **P** lose.
  - The enemy attacks in waves. Rolling 1 on the **main die** means a new enemy arrives.

Examples:

- Capturing an archer’s tower during a siege.
- Holding a terrace carved into rock above a chasm.

---

##### 4. Time pressure

- Goal: Succeed before disaster strikes.
- Mechanics:
  - **P** do not advance the victory clock - they must change the fiction in a specific way.
  - All **P**’s moves advance the **victory clock** by 1 more, but at the start of each of their turns they receive **fatigue** equal to the current round (1 in the first, 2 in the second, 3 in the third).
  - Before the fight ends, X **P** must get past the barrier.

Examples:

- Stopping a necromancer before they raise an army.
- Closing a portal before a monster comes through it.

---

##### 5. Destruction

- Goal: Destroy something instead of fighting to the end.
- Mechanics:
  - The object must be hit with X successes of **VIOLENCE**.
  - Enemies do everything they can to push **P** away from their main goal.
  - The environment can be used (for example knocking a column onto a statue) to increase hits on the object.
  - Rolling 1 on the **main die** means someone has reinforced/repaired **P**’s target and they must hit it with 2 additional successes.

Examples:

- Destroying a pillar supporting the roof of a cave.
- Burning all key documents in a fortress.

---

##### 6. Boss Fight

- Goal: Defeat an exceptionally powerful enemy who has special phases.
- Mechanics:
  - Fight phases – every X successes of **VIOLENCE**, the boss changes their **powers** to stronger ones.
  - Weak points – every boss has weak spots or a power source (usually 2), which can be discovered with a full success on **perception** or appropriate knowledge, as **PRESSURE** - once such a point is known, you can inflict 2 wounds on the boss at once instead of one, or destroying both allows you to automatically win the fight.
  - A critical failure means the boss gains an additional change to the fiction.
  - Their changes to the fiction may be spectacular, for example destroying an entire zone with a blow.

Examples:

- Two enemies who merge into one in the second phase - and then split again.
- A golem that can be weakened by destroying the magical runes on its body.

#### Note

Remember that the primacy of fiction supports you and allows you to run combat in practically any conditions. If a confrontation takes place on wagons riding side by side, moving platforms, against invisible enemies / illusion, underwater, above a chasm, or in any other unusual circumstances for which you do not have ready mechanics, always focus on making the fight a story that strives for constant changes in the fiction, not on counting points. **P** will remember whether they jumped over a chasm and pushed an enemy into it, not whether they advanced a clock by 1 or 2 spaces.

If you decide that a scene is much more suited to being a story than a combat procedure, you can always play it out as a **sequential narrative** or **chase**, focusing on its cinematic nature.

### Combat Descriptions - the OTE method

If engaging combat descriptions are difficult for you, use the OTE method (orientation, tension, emotion).  
It is a simple framework for improvising smooth, vivid descriptions of confrontations without stopping the game.

**OTE — three steps of description**

1. **Orientation — _Scene and Position_**
   > A crouching assassin leaps out from behind one of the toppled obelisks of the ruined druidic circle.
2. **Tension — _Audio and Action_**
   > You hear his quick breath and the clatter of steel; he feints with one blade to mislead you, then immediately tries to drive the second into your stomach.
3. **Emotion and continuity — _Human detail and Background_**
   > Your body reacts before you think. You step back at the last moment and from the corner of your eye see other shadowy figures leaping among the ruins — their movement startles a flock of screeching ravens.

**Another example:**

1. **Orientation — _Scene and Position_**
   > Mist pouring through the ballroom window wraps around the knight standing on the stairs.
2. **Tension — _Audio and Action_**
   > “I’ll feed your corpse to the dogs!” he roars, trying to kick you. Then a swing, a whistle, the blade comes for your head.
3. **Emotion and continuity — _Human detail and Background_**
   > Your legs bend slightly as his blade throws sparks against your shield. Almost all the guests have already fled the hall.

- 3-4 sentences are all you need - in the heat of action there is no time for more anyway.
- Keep the sentences short, abandon poetic language, go for precision and pace.
- You can use this in every **P** turn.
- It takes a little practice, but once OTE becomes a habit, the descriptions will begin to form on their own.

### Factions

The actions of **P** do not happen in a vacuum - they always move the world’s complex system, which reacts in a way appropriate to the authorized actors.

The main actor of the world of Zaya is political and religious factions, between which there is a constant tug-of-war over lines of influence.

The factions whose fiction is directly concerned always react.

Factions react in 3 cases:

- world logic → in response to the actions of **P** that require immediate changes in the fiction - for example **P** humiliated a representative of a faction; or it became public that they came into possession of an item crucial to its plans; or **P** have been wanted for a long time and their deeds are still heard about here and there across the country, so the king sets up checkpoints at every crossroads.
- planned events → when a clock tied to a given faction fills - for example the **GM** runs an event clock for **P**, caused by their decisions, which determines people’s views on declaring war on the neighbors; if that clock fills, the king marches with the army; or the **GM** runs a clock for **P** tracking their progress in proving the church’s corruption, and if that clock fills, the church’s authority collapses - faction clocks represent large/key changes in the fiction and serve as plot points.
- autonomously → by carrying out their own plans, even without the participation of **P** - they must spend a geopolitics point to do so.
  - factions can gain geopolitics points in the following way:
    - The **GM** may buy 1 for any faction for 3 of their fate dice (generated from excess **splinters** of **P**), but should do so with what is best for the fiction in mind.
    - After every session the **GM** rolls k6 in front of **P** - a 1 means an enemy faction receives a geopolitics point, and a 6 means an allied faction does - the specific enemy faction is chosen secretly by the GM, the allied faction is chosen by **P** (they may also suggest what it could use it for).
  - such a point may be spent on any action that makes sense for that faction and aims to improve its position in the fiction, for example the faction’s spies automatically beat **P** in a race to a key place; or the faction sets up a bloody ambush for **P**; or the faction pulls an ally of **P** to its side / kidnaps them; or a faction supported by **P** drives its opponents out of a region, etc.
  - a faction must spend its geopolitics point between the session in which it received it and the next one.

This is the membrane of your world. This system wants it to pulse, and for **P** not to go on adventures separated from reality, but to become entangled through their actions in matters greater than themselves, over which they can never have full control.

### Structure of a Single Session

To know exactly what the structure of your session should look like, you need to get to know the pace of your **P** well - but for the purposes of this presentation, I will assume that the party usually gets through 5 scenes during a standard 4-hour game.

It does not matter whether it is a one-shot or another session of a campaign - usually the structure of the session will be very similar.

- Goal - every session should have one main problem or question that will be resolved or whose meaning will change - one change in the fiction should be central.
- Strong opening - an event that engages **P** as quickly as possible instead of easing them slowly into events - for example **P** meets an NPC to whom they have a strong emotional bond.
  - The most important thing is that the scene has real meaning for **P** - high stakes / serious consequences / personal ties, etc.
  - Avoid combat here, unless it follows directly from the previous session and has real meaning.
  - Sometimes hard to pull off if this is a continuation of events from the previous session - you do not always need a strong opening, it is only good practice.
  - Sometimes you can play out a scene before **P** know exactly why they are doing something, and afterward / in the meantime, once emotions have settled a bit, play a flashback where **P** actually establish the terms of what they took on - this works best in one-shots; in a campaign it disrupts the continuity and credibility of events.
- 2/3 intense scenes (one of them may be the strong opening) - real action with high stakes:
  - The following mechanics work best for this:
    - sequential narrative
    - pressure
    - chase
    - combat
  - This is the backbone of events - the outcomes of these scenes change the course of the story.
  - If possible, they should not happen one right after another.
  - Ideally, the session contains 1 very important action scene (sequential narrative / combat / chase) and one important, but less intense one (pressure / social conflict)
- 2/3 calm scenes - conversations / gathering information / strengthening bonds with NPCs / travel / roleplay / rest
  - Especially important to give **P** mental and mechanical time to thaw out after an intense scene
  - These scenes are the glue of the story, connecting its individual elements into a coherent, believable whole.
  - The fact that these scenes are meant to be calm does not mean they cannot have tension - on the contrary, they often will - it is more about the weight of events.
- 1 unexpected or strange event / plot twist / major decision
  - A scene that ideally overturns **P**’s perspective - for example they learn that someone betrayed them or that the nature of a mutation is different than they believed.
  - This can be either an intense scene or a calm one.
  - It is impossible to have a strong plot twist every session - you can try to introduce them as often as possible, but remember that their credibility is the most important thing, and that is built by side scenes.
  - If there is no sensible and believable way to shake **P**’s perspective, put something strange or morally ambiguous in front of them that requires a decision with real consequences.
  - This is the main moment of **P**’s agency - do not give them clues and do not suggest solutions, this is meant to be entirely their moment and their responsibility.
- The order of scenes does not matter much apart from the opening - just remember to give **P** time to catch their breath after action - this will not only balance the tempo, but also let properly important events resonate.
- Key rules you should stick to:
  - You provide the skeleton of events and their meaning, not a script with ready-made answers - **P** decide what really happens within the frame you gave them and how they interpret it.
  - Do not invent solutions - you prepare the problem, for example treasure locked in a safe and the scenery of a bank, but **P** come up with how to deal with the obstacles along the way - you may try to predict what they will do in order to prepare for it, but the story belongs to **P**.
  - Example:
  - context - **P** are asked by a local judge to help find his wife and daughter, who were supposedly kidnapped by a gang operating on the outskirts of the city. The judge suspects that the gang wants to force him to drop the case he is conducting regarding the massacre of two prisoners who recently disappeared from his prison.
  - Scene 1 - strong opening - intense scene - **pressure** - **P** appear under cover at the gang’s headquarters to gather information about the women being held there. To their surprise, the place does not look like a hideout for people holding hostages - it is calm, no one seems nervous, and in conversations there is mention of preparing new documents and a false identity for someone.
    - pressure - clock 6:
      - entering in disguise and maintaining a false identity during conversations (**P** must come up themselves with what exactly they came for) - **deception**
      - entertaining gang members to give another **P** time to glance into another room - **performance** - another **P** may move to a side room and look through the papers on the table, finding nothing matching the case, nor even any orders connected to attacks by the rival gang, nothing illegal even apart from an order for false documents, an extortion ledger, and a note: "Gong finishes preparing for departure tomorrow night."
      - **decision** - in sight there is a document sealed with the gang’s symbol, but there is no more time, do you risk looking at it? - "the judge’s wife helped us in a way, even if by accident, I approve her matter"
      - sensibly negotiating their business (probably illegal) - **mind**
      - casually asking a gang member about a woman with a child - **persuasion/influence** - they will oddly say that supposedly that daughter died a few months ago, but you cannot tell it by looking at her
      - **decision** - do you risk asking for details, which may raise suspicion? -
      - **P** notices that there is a good atmosphere among the gang members - **intuition** - no one is stressed, even though they supposedly are holding the wife of a judge who could destroy them all if this gets out - no one is in a combative mood despite the supposedly ongoing gang war.
      - result:
        - success - **P** leave while maintaining their false identity and without arousing suspicion.
        - failure - **P** are seen through, **combat** begins, during which **P** must get out of the building and disappear - additionally they get **disadvantage** on the first two tests during scene 3.
  - Scene 2 - calm scene - flashback of the conversation with the judge. He claims that the gang is probably torturing his family to force him to dismiss the case of the two missing prisoners - members of a rival gang - whose deaths may spark another war between criminal factions. He begs **P** to find his wife and daughter before they are harmed. He asks **P** how often they pray to Dream asking about the holy journey of their own soul.
  - Scene 3 - intense scene - **sequential narrative** - **P** break deeper into the gang’s headquarters. During exploration, they discover information that does not fit the kidnapping story: gang members mention that the woman is paying them for protection, and the inhabitants of a nearby village are demanding her death for practicing forbidden magic. The gang is preparing a new identity for her and her child so they can disappear.
    - sequential narrative - clock 8:
      - entering in disguise - **deception** / bribing a guard - **influence** / breaking in through the back entrance - **sleight of hand/technology**
      - finding the way inside an unfamiliar building - **survival** - a note pinned to the wall with a knife: keep watch on her, the villagers demand her death for practicing forbidden magic; we do not care, but it is better to stay alert
      - getting past the guards - **stealth**
      - overhearing conversations - **perception** - gang members talk about how well the woman pays them for protection, with money she stole from her husband
      - door with a trap - **investigation**
      - moving through a window along the outer wall to the upper floor - **athletics/dexterity** - in the room there are documents being prepared with a new identity for the woman and child matching the judge’s description
      - quietly incapacitating/killing the guards watching the women - **melee combat** - they are talking: they were our enemies, but the demon took their souls, I am not saying I pity them, but this goes beyond our conflict, it strikes at the journey of souls, I do not know whether the boss did the right thing by taking that woman in
      - convincing the women not to scream when they see **P** / incapacitating them - **persuasion/athletics**
      - results:
        - success - **P** have infiltrated the target unnoticed
        - failure - **P** left too many traces - they know they will be found soon - they no longer have a choice, whatever they decide regarding the judge’s wife, they must flee immediately if they want to live - they receive 1 **disadvantage** to the chase for every question they ask the judge’s wife beyond 3 (openly).
  - Scene 4 - calm scene - plot twist - **P** find the judge’s wife and her daughter. The woman admits that she was not kidnapped - she paid the gang for protection. She reveals that her daughter died a few months earlier of consumption. In despair, she made a pact with a demon, which, in exchange for the souls of two murderers held in her husband’s prison, whom she freed by stealing the key, brought the girl back to life by pulling her soul out of dream and returning it to her body. The girl looks completely normal, but her existence is a violation of the natural journey of souls.
  - Scene 5 - the finale depends on **P**’s choice - if **P** decide to hand the woman over to justice, it means a **chase** - where losing means death. If they decide to help her, they will have to leave with nothing, lie to the people demanding justice and restitution for the fact that the demon was fed with souls and will now have the strength to destroy human lives - because that is what it feeds on, despair, the very thing the judge’s wife was trying so hard to escape. The judge is torn both ways in all this - he wants neither one nor the other.
    - if **P** left the gang headquarters normally and left the judge’s wife there, his men find them - if **P** begin running from them, play a chase where losing means death for supporting demonic magic - if they want to lie to / persuade the townsfolk that they are wrong, play a short scene with 4 rolls on social **traits**, where the same **P** cannot roll the same **trait** twice - if they fail, move to the chase.
  - This is, of course, only a skeletal example - a true session breakdown can be much more extensive - in particular, what is missing is a breakdown of how **P** actually gain the information that influences their final decision - but the more comfortable you become with improvisation as a GM, the less preparation you will need, and you will see that the example above is not actually that far from basically everything you need apart from a few details - the system’s ready-made mechanics and tables will help you with the rest.
  - You can play this session out to test the rhythm of play it proposes.

### How to Write Scenarios - the Thought Process Through an Example

This chapter is not a theory of scenario writing. It is a practical guide on how to build a working adventure for _Haunted Matter_ out of an idea - from the first thought to a finished structure.

I will walk you step by step through how I create an adventure for my own table.

We will use the example from the handbook - the scenario: The Twenty-Seventh Among Twenty-Six.

#### Assumptions

- The system will let you prepare very quickly on the mechanical side. Creating a deep story - drama-action with moral decisions - cannot, however, be sped up.
  - It usually takes me a few hours.
- Do not invent endings and scene solutions - the **GM** provides the frame/structure - what exactly happens depends on **P**.
- Every scene should lead to at least two others.
- If the adventure you are creating will be part of a campaign, try to include threads connected to the stories of at least 2 of your **P** - this is one of the most important techniques for anchoring **P** in the plot and the world - almost nothing else has such great immersive power.
  - The adventure below is a one-shot, so we do not have that here.
- Every adventure is a story about your **P**! - they are the ones playing, and they are the most important.

#### Core Motif

- Always start with the core idea/motif - mine was: the community has one more member than the count says it should, and no one knows who it is.
  - Coming up with the main axis sometimes takes longer than preparing the whole adventure - do not worry if you do not have ideas right away.
- If you want to stay true to the tone of the system: the motif of the adventure should revolve around serious drama without clearly good solutions, tragedy, preferably touching religious threads in a mystical way. We want it to feel mysterious, difficult, dirty, strange, ritualistic, dangerous.

#### Build a logical structure, not a linear one

- What does my idea mean and what follows from it? In other words, logically branching out the foundation of the story.

##### The extra member follows from the count.

- Better if the number of people to count is not too large - 27.
  - In such a small community, everyone knows everyone, so how is it possible that no one knows who the extra one is?
    - Who is the extra one? - a girl, Darra.
      - She probably should not have her own house, so people cannot just go to her - homeless, a drifter.
        - How to justify that? - lots of people in the world live as farmhands, working for bread and a piece of floor in other people’s homes and farms.
      - How does she hide? - this is one of the most important points - I admit I needed a few days to come up with something that felt logically believable and satisfied me as an interesting element of the world - Darra is not human, but a local species of giant moth in its larval form.
        - _Lunae Noctivora_ - a symbiotic parasite. It takes the form of a representative of a nearby community and builds a cocoon from materials gathered from that group. It often lives among herds of mammals. A human individual is an exceptional case. During development it creates an illusion of identity; victims do not remember it clearly. Usually larvae of the same generation try their luck among different species to increase their chance of survival, but if they can, they help one another, for example a larva in the form of a mole may show a larva in the form of a bird where worms gather so it can feed. Their intelligence depends on the form adopted, but never exceeds a certain low ceiling.
        - Are there other larvae? - yes: 2 wolves and a badger - one wolf has already transformed into a moth and flown away, the other is still gathering materials for a cocoon, and the badger’s transformation failed.
        - Where did they hatch? - in the swamps - **P** may find eggs.
          - How to mislead **P** so the trail is not obvious? - another local species.
            - Lumbricina Gigantalis - a semi-aquatic larva with an exceptional ability to mimic color and scent. It lives in wetlands. At rest, it enters into symbiosis with peat moss and lichens, becoming practically invisible. It feeds on organic matter and sometimes carrion. It can produce spots on its skin that look like moving animals when it itself moves - this scares away predators. A non-intelligent organism, it reacts mainly to heat and smell. It can imitate movement, but not voice.
        - Where do the animals make their cocoons? - Darra in an abandoned mill, the transformed wolf in a clearing in the forest, the badger in another part of the forest, the second wolf among the rocks beyond the swamps.
        - How can **P** learn about this in a credible and logical way?
          - Olven, a half-mad herbalist studying local insects.
            - He discovered what Darra is, but she is a fascinating specimen to him and he studies her life - he does not want to expose her - he says he liked her company because the insects in his house did not bother her, so she worked for him more often than for others.
            - Better if he lives on the edge of things - that will suit his character and give **P** more freedom.
              - He used to live with the others, but they did not accept his strangeness, so he moved to the edge of the forest, though the village women still come to him for remedies.
              - Since he lives on the outskirts, he can live near the badger’s cocoon - his house will be presented as a possible point to investigate, so by going there they may also discover a clue about the larvae - a dead badger looking like an empty shell, a burst balloon, tracks leading to its cocoon.
            - In his house, in a hiding place protected by a trap, there are descriptions of local species - including the larvae.
            - If **P** show him the badger shell and/or its cocoon, he will lie that he does not know exactly what it is, but badly wants to learn more, because that is what he studies.
          - Questioning the villagers:
            - They do not remember what Darra looks like, though they swear she worked in each of their homes for lodging and food.
            - She arrived probably a few years ago, but they do not remember exactly when.
            - She often went to Olven.
            - Sometimes she lingered around the abandoned mill.
            - She gathered various unused trinkets - old clothes, food scraps, bits of wood.
              - Such traces can be found on the road - they lead to a crossroads - the old mill / the clearing with the wolf cocoon that already transformed.
            - No one knows where she is now.
          - Searching for Darra is an ideal candidate for **sequential narrative**.

##### Who counted it?

- The scribe Pelin (he can read and write, which is something exceptional).
- When were they counted? - 2 days ago.
- Pelin makes lists every year because the administration requires it - he goes from hut to hut and checks who lives in them, or asks if needed.
- He has the previous lists, but when **P** come to him, it turns out they have been stolen.
  - Janis - in love with the girl, without knowing what she really is - he knows she is strange and suspects the villagers may mean her, but he does not think she is evil, so he steals and destroys the lists of his own will to impress her.
- On the current list, 3 names are written in a different handwriting - Olven, Ojran, and Darra - Pelin explains it by saying he simply does not like them - for the GM, an opportunity to point **P** in a direction.
- In the last year one person died, and 2 were born.
- Pelin mainly handles administrative matters and reads/writes letters for people.

##### Why is anyone worried about this?

- Everyone in such a small community knows each other well; they cannot understand how it is possible that the numbers do not match when everyone knows every face and no one is new. They suspect evil forces; that a demon lives among them.

##### How do **P** learn about it?

- In the Rib Inn from its owner Ojran.
  - Why from him? - he is the self-proclaimed leader of the village, he grabbed authority.
    - Because of that he is disliked by Pelin, who does not recognize him.
- Quite a few people gathered in the tavern - including Burto with his caretakers - it is clear they gather here in the evenings to build community.
- If **P** immediately tell him to gather everyone and count them, he replies that it is not possible right away, because some people work at night and he does not know where they are - but he can give the order for people to gather under the inn tomorrow after work, though it is not clear whether Olven will come.
- This fits the **pressure** mechanic.

#### Moral dilemma

- A moral dilemma should follow from the logic of the situation, not from an arbitrary choice. The **GM** does not force it, but allows it to arise from the facts the players uncover themselves.
- How to add a moral dilemma here?
  - Adult moths feed on moonlight - in places they have bitten away, natural night light will never shine again, and artificial lights will become much dimmer - sunlight works normally.
    - For people, if the scale of this phenomenon became large, it would mean major hardship.
    - How to show it? - in the clearing with the wolf cocoon that already transformed, there is a lone tree and at night moonlight falls everywhere there, but not on the tree, even though nothing blocks it - the area was bitten away by the hatched moth before it flew off.
      - Consequence? - cocoons are visible only in moonlight.
      - At the inn there is an underdeveloped boy, Burto, under his parents’ care, who claims the extra person in the village is one of the gods walking among people - probably the Shepherd, god of dreams - he saw the lack of light in the clearing and can lead **P** there.
      - **P** may recall religious myths that the god of sleep will one day send animals that will devour all night light, to force the faithful to dream.
  - Darra needs only a little more time to transform - her cocoon is almost ready in the abandoned mill - a clock will decide whether she is ready.
    - This creature has hurt no one, but in its adult form it constitutes a strange kind of potential threat - when **P** discover the truth, will they allow her to transform, or eliminate her?
    - Her death is not, however, the end - the cocoon itself is a threat - pure dream floats inside it - if Darra does not transform, the dream will spill over the area around the cocoon and transform it in an unforeseen way - either like a dreaming fantasy or like a nightmare.
    - To keep the story from ending too quickly, we need to make destroying the cocoon harder, so Darra has a chance to transform even if **P** do not want that - it is resistant to fire and physical force - in fact it can only really be untangled, and it defends itself with a hallucination-inducing liquid - pure dream.
- How will the people react to that?
  - They will see it as a sign of evil forces and ask **P** to kill Darra - if they refuse, the villagers will do it themselves - they can of course be talked out of it / stopped, but it is difficult.
- The dilemma should not only be the question ‘whether to kill’, but the disputed issue of whether the natural life process of an animal, which interferes at least somewhat with the human world, can be sufficient reason to kill it. And at the same time whether stopping it is worth the risk of dream spilling out.

#### Setting

- Where does the action take place, what kind of scenery fits it?
- The smaller the area you choose, the fewer factors you have to take into account. Do not be afraid, however, to place the action in wild, fantastic, or strange locations - this is fantasy, after all, even if low-magic - just remember that they should fit the tone and content of the situations you are preparing in them.
- Here we choose a small village.
  - What does the village live off? - it is very poor - it survives mainly through logging and hunting.

#### Strong opening

- Engage **P** from the very beginning - introduce a challenge/conflict/mystery.
- In this adventure: **P** arrive in the village of Gob at night. Tired, they are returning from a previous job. They still have a long road ahead, but they must stop somewhere to rest. The place seems empty. No lights burn in the huts. Wolves howl from the nearby forest. All traces lead to the Rib Inn, where in the evenings, for company, most of the villagers gather - whoever can. Ojran notices **P** from afar, comes out to them, and invites them inside. Everything should look like a trap, even though it is not. The innkeeper gives **P** free food and stands over them watching them eat. Everyone stares at them, but does not want to say what they want. They seem embarrassed. They say later, after they eat. That it is a very strange matter. And finally they tell them about the evil powers that brought an extra person into their village, something hard to explain and harder still to understand.

#### NPC list

- Keep it separate, so you can easily glance at it.
- In the case of this adventure, it is:
  - Darra - resembles a girl, but on closer look shows signs of otherness - a Moon Moth larva in human form - soon ready for transformation.
  - Ojran - a powerful innkeeper - self-proclaimed leader of the village, which turns some villagers, especially Pelin, against him.
  - Pelin - a skinny scribe in glasses - he thinks he probably made a mistake rather than evil forces coming to the village, but he does not have the power to convince the god-fearing, simple people who live here.
  - Olven - a half-mad herbalist with twisted locks of hair - he studies the local flora and fauna, mainly insects - he moved to the edge of the forest because the others did not accept his strangeness.
  - Janis - a young red-haired boy torn by hormones - he loves Darra, but does not know what she really is.

#### Scenes and locations

- So what hooks/scenes do we have?
  - inn - strong opening - **pressure** - tension scene
  - clearing with devoured moonlight - calm scene
  - Pelin’s hut - calm scene
  - Olven’s hut + badger cocoon - **procedure for searching a room under pressure**
  - search for Darra - **sequential narrative** - tension scene
  - abandoned mill + Darra’s cocoon - calm scene
  - swamps + Moon Moth eggs - calm scene
  - gathering the people for the count - possible **chase** - tension scene
- Note: it does not matter in what order **P** go to these places or what exactly they do there - and that is what we want - to give **P** freedom - we have built situations, not a queue of events.

#### Motivation

- How do you encourage **P** to get involved?
  - A strange story + a **treasure** reward or a cow from the villagers.

#### Combat

- To add a potential armed conflict:
  - A hunted Darra may gain the help of a wolf pack (5), led by her brother-larva.
    - She will probably try to hide that it was she who called the wolves.
  - Olven will defend himself if **P** break into his house.
    - He has a scroll under the floor - he will use it as a tornado, regardless of the consequences.
      - Since we already have one scroll and know that Olven wants to protect Darra, let us assume that in the hiding place next to it there is an empty space for a second scroll - he gave it to the girl so that she could cast the illusion of a swarm of giant spiders if needed - she will do so in the mill if necessary.
    - In one of the rooms there is a huge greenhouse full of local mosquitoes the size of fists - when opened, two swarms will fly out.
- You can prepare a map beforehand, but you do not have to - sketching a few zones takes only a moment.

#### Time the adventure begins

- Does the starting time of the adventure matter?
  - Here, yes: it is better to begin at night, so **P** cannot immediately gather all the villagers to count them, and so they might postpone going with Burto to the clearing of devoured light.

#### Rewards

- You do not need to prepare in advance any things **P** will find - if you feel that it is the right moment, that something should fall into their hands, for example they opened a hiding place, just tell them to roll on the **Loot Table** - if the find should be more valuable, let them roll twice and choose the higher result.

#### But what if **P** disrupt my structure?

- This can always happen, and it happens to every GM.
- Do not be afraid to improvise - the more you run games, the easier it will become - but try to do it in such a way that you eventually return **P** to the frame of your story.
- If you know your NPCs well enough and know what your adventure is ‘about’ - you will manage in any situation.
- Remember - it is impossible to be prepared for everything, and you should not even try - you need to have imagined enough to feel comfortable, the rest, with the help of **P** and their moves, will come into being along the way.

#### And where is the mechanics?

- There is nothing mechanical here - and that is deliberate - in the specific chapters you will find descriptions of how to use traps, search rooms, run a chase, etc. - once you learn that, you will not need to write any of those things into your structure, and examples of how to do it can be found in the full version of the adventure below.
- Of course, without the mechanics you will not manage, and you will need to spend time learning how to use the specific rules together with **P**, and at first I would advise you to prepare some mechanical threads in your adventures in advance - for example objects for the room-searching procedure for specific locations - but once you have absorbed that, as I said at the beginning, mechanical preparation will take literally a few moments, and you will be able to devote all the rest of your energy to credible, logical situations - which is the most important thing.

#### Summary

- This is basically everything you need - you have a logical, credible, and interesting structure within which **P** can move freely using moves.
  - Of course, it can all be organized much better, but here I wanted to show you how to build a story as a chain of cause and effect.
- When you read the full version of the adventure, you will see the differences:
  - There are many more details there - but everything additional beyond what we did here are only extras - they are not required - when you become comfortable as a game master, you will be able to do without them.
  - Creating an official adventure for print follows its own rules and must be far more exhaustive than what a **GM** should prepare for their private table.
  - There are specific tests there - this is a completely unnecessary crutch for beginner **GM**, who may at first have trouble conveying all important information to **P** through moves - the moves themselves, however, are entirely sufficient and give **P** much more freedom and influence over the narrative.
    - If, however, you feel safer writing down all the tests - do it - just remember that it takes time.
- The most important things:
  - Always begin by asking what the moral core of the story is.
  - Build a logical structure, not a linear one – situations, not plot.
  - Do not write down ready-made scene solutions – the **GM** is supposed to react, not read a script.
  - Every scene should lead to at least two others.
  - Introduce a strong opening.
  - Refer to the stories of your **P**.
  - Every adventure is a story about your **P**! - they are the ones playing, and they are the most important.

# Adventure - The Twenty-Seventh Among Twenty-Six

## What Is Happening

- Darra Mej is a Moon Moth pupa that takes the form of a member of the community it feeds on.
- For a long time, she lived among the villagers as one of them - she slept in their homes, ate with them, took food, clothes, planks, and various bits of junk from them, and used those to build her cocoon in the abandoned mill.
- Darra does not act consciously - she functions instinctively, like a parasite, gathering resources and maintaining the appearance of being “one of them.”
- Animals react to her presence (dogs howl, birds fly away), but people do not connect this with Darra.
- The aura of dream she gives off makes people unable to remember her well - they know that they know her and that “she is from here,” but cannot assign a face, voice, or specific memories to her.
- That is why the village did not notice the problem for a long time - Darra was an extra person, but her presence blurred in people’s memory.
- The matter only comes to light when the scribe Pelin makes a census of the villagers and discovers that there are 27 people in the village instead of 26.
- The villagers do not know what Darra really is - they are convinced that some foreign, evil being is hiding in the village and want **P** to identify the intruder and then get rid of it.
- **Janis** suspects it is Darra, but does not see her as something evil - he fell in love with her, helped her in secret, and stole the old residents’ lists to make discovering the truth harder.
- Olven knows more about Darra than the others. He sees her at once as a wonder of nature, an object of study, and something that should be protected. He helped her and will hide the truth as long as he can.
- If the **transformation clock** fills, Darra will return to the cocoon and transform into a huge moth that feeds on moonlight. The areas it “devours” will sink into permanent, unnatural darkness at night - they can no longer be lit by any light source.
- Most important: killing Darra does not solve the problem.
- Her cocoon is already soaked with dream. If Darra dies before the transformation, or does not manage to return to it in time, the dream may spill out of the cocoon and turn the surrounding area into something entirely inhuman, like a nightmare or a fairy tale.
- This means **P** are not dealing with a simple “find the monster and kill it” problem, but are thrown into a situation where every decision may be wrong. In the end, **P** may:
  - allow Darra to transform,
  - kill her and risk the dream spilling out,
  - try to untangle the cocoon,
  - offer someone as a substitute sacrifice,
  - or stand against the villagers, who want a simple solution.
- The main axis of the adventure is: who Darra really is, and whether she should be killed, saved, or stopped before she transforms.
- From the moment the adventure begins, Darra does not appear in the village even once, because she has already gathered enough material to finish shaping her cocoon. She is now working on the final touches and waiting until enough dream gathers inside it - which is shown by the **transformation clock**.
  - no one in the village finds this strange, because she was living like a stray anyway, moving from place to place.

## Skippable

- Not every clue needs to come up in one playthrough - if **P** quickly understand that the trail leads to Darra and the mill, you can skip some of the side discoveries.
- The swamps are not mandatory. They mainly serve to build atmosphere, show the broader context of the Moon Moths, and add unease.
- The Great Swamp Larva is not necessary to understand the adventure - it is an element of the world and atmosphere, not a main pillar of the scenario.
- Darra’s brother in the body of a wolf is an addition that broadens the picture of the phenomenon. You can use him, but you do not have to. The adventure also works without expanding that thread.
- The trail with tracks leading to the wolves’ lair can be skipped if the pace is sagging, or if **P** already have enough information to move on.
- The clearing of devoured light is a very good image and suggestion, but it is not essential to running the finale.
- Hallucinations after contact with the “flax,” dream visions, and similar images are optional - use them when you want to strengthen the atmosphere or hint at the truth indirectly.
- You do not have to play out all possible tensions around Pelin, Ojran, Janis, and Olven. It is enough that each of these characters fulfills their function:
  - Ojran - represents the will of the villagers,
  - Pelin - triggers the problem and legitimizes the credibility of the situation,
  - Janis - complicates the matter emotionally,
  - Olven - hides part of the truth and deepens the dilemma.
- If **P** get to the heart of the matter quickly, you can reduce the number of conversations with villagers to a few of the strongest ones and not play out the entire cross-section of the village.
- Breaking into Olven’s hut and the procedural search are not mandatory - they are an additional path to gaining knowledge and resources.
- The scroll, Dream Crystal, side loot, and some of the discoveries in the mill can be treated as bonus rewards, not the core of the scenario.
- The illusion of giant spiders in the mill is optional - it is useful if Darra slips away from **P**, or if you want to emphasize the defense of the cocoon more strongly.
- Not every clue has to give a hard answer. Some of them can be only an unsettling suggestion that something unnatural is happening in the village and the surrounding area.
- If the session is getting too long, cut first:
  - side explorations,
  - additional creatures and discoveries,
  - some of the visions and descriptions,
  - everything that does not lead to: Darra, the cocoon, the villagers’ decision, or the finale.

## NPCs

- Darra Mej - a Moon Moth pupa - in human form she looks like an ordinary, not very pretty blonde girl - people take her for a simpleton.
- Ojran Briri - powerful and fat owner of the Rib Inn, self-proclaimed leader of the village, which earns him enemies, an alcoholic.
- Pelin - fifty-year-old, thin as a stick, gray-haired scribe - writes letters for people, keeps accounts, talks to officials - he went to school in the city, but returned, which he regrets.
- Olven Hat - herbalist, a short weirdo with twisted hair, moved to the edge of the forest because people could not stand his insect breeding.
- Janis Dwojar - young trapper, in love with Darra because he does not know what she is.

## Helpful Information

- Each point takes 1/3 of a day - every time 24h passes, advance the **transformation clock** by 1
- Tests described in the following way: **dexterity: status/death** - mean that on a partial success, the option on the left happens, and on a failure, the one on the right; if the outcome is rather positive, notation like something/something means that a partial success gives the result on the left, and a full success gives the one on the right.
- Remember to ask **P** guiding questions: why does your hair stand on end when you enter this room? You have never seen anything like this before, what feelings does it stir in you?
- Remember about **moves** and the rule that when a test is general and everyone could make it, only 2 people may do so.
- Maintain a folk-horror atmosphere.

## Start - arrival in the village of Gob

- **P** arrive in the empty village of Gob at night. It seems deserted, only dogs howl. They found it by chance, returning along the road after successfully completing a task (they safely delivered a sculpture to a magnate’s house), on their way to collect their reward (1 treasure), but they still have 2 days of travel left, so they must stop somewhere to rest. They enter the Rib Inn, invited by the owner - everything feels like a trap - the atmosphere is thick, no one wants to say what is going on, there are no smiles, children are not playing, everyone stares hard at the newcomers and their weapons, as if both fearing them and looking to them for hope. **P** are given a free meal and a promise that they will hear what is going on once they finish eating - it is an embarrassing matter. Everyone watches them while they slurp the sludge.
- Run this using the **Pressure** mechanic (6 hidden clock segments):
  - Ojran, the powerfully built owner, reeking clearly of alcohol, comes out in front of the inn to meet **P** and invites them inside, calling from afar - he says he has food for them, and they can talk about payment later - he seems suspicious - inside there are a dozen or so people, all quiet, grim, staring intensely at **P**.
    - checking whether the villagers mean harm, reading their posture - **intuition** - they are more frightened than hostile, they carry no weapons, but something hangs in the air.
  - **perception** - **P** see that the owner’s wife, Ludmiła, hastily added something to their soup before it was served, though it is hard to tell whether it is dangerous, some herb maybe, perhaps nothing harmful; everyone is watching.
  - **decision** - do **P** ask what is in the soup? - Ludmiła added local swamp algae, a flavor enhancer, to soften them up.
  - conversation with the villagers about what really happened.
    - the villagers want **P** to help identify the extra person in their village. The village of Gob has 26 inhabitants, but the last entry showed that there are 27. They suspect evil forces, which terrify them. The intruder must be gotten rid of - the demon must be killed. In return they offer one **treasure**, contributed by the whole village, or a cow (worth more).
      - explanation - the villagers were unable to realize that Darra was the extra person, because her dream aura made them forget her immediately, and whenever they mention her, she seems “one of them,” though the memory is blurred.
    - main reason for hiring **P**? - the villagers believe in evil forces, need someone who could use force if necessary, and they themselves do not even have weapons, let alone any skill.
    - things **P** can learn one way or another:
      - the villagers have this year’s list, but the documents from previous years vanished - though there are no signs of a break-in - as if unclean forces had acted
      - the scribe Pelin made the census the day before yesterday, repeated it today, and tomorrow or the day after, after work, they planned to gather all the villagers.
        - Ojran will not agree to hold the gathering immediately - he does not want to frighten the villagers and does not know where everyone is, and it is dark.
      - **P** can go to Pelin in the morning, sifting through papers by candlelight will not help much anyway.
        - Ojran will not agree to bothering the villagers at night - he does not want to spread panic - people will be afraid to live normally - nothing bad has happened yet, we have time - besides, Pelin may get furious if someone comes to him at night, especially sent by Ojran, because they dislike one another and he will not open anyway.
          - Pelin indeed will not open at night - he will say this matter is some mistake, he can help in the morning.
    - **influence** test - each success gives another piece of information, **P** may also pay 1 **fatigue** for each one even on a failure.
      - Ojran does not like Pelin - he says reluctantly that he is a stuck-up know-it-all, thinks that because he can read he is better than everyone else, has no friends in the village, but the people need him to write/read letters and talk to officials for them.
      - the annual censuses are required by the provincial office.
      - over the last year, one old woman, Murta, died, and 2 babies were born to the Lofts.
  - Burto speaks up - the local disabled man with a dull gaze and slightly unclear speech - he says loudly, uninvited, that it is a god walking among them, but no one listens to him.
    - his parents say he talks nonsense and ask that no one listen to him - if **P** insist that he lead them to the place he speaks of, they ask that at least it not be at night, and add that they must go with him to watch over him, because he is not independent.
    - when young he fell from a cart and struck the back of his head on a stone - he is not trustworthy.
    - getting more information out of Burto works either way, but **persuasion** determines how much he says - 1 piece of information per 1 success - if **P** cannot get anything more from him, he shuts down in fear (as if he suddenly changed his mind):
      - he will reveal that he saw a place near the forest where at night no light shines, even though nothing blocks it - a clearing with a lone tree.
      - he used to go there alone when no one looked after him, because it is a pretty place and before everything was normal there, though once he saw a lone wolf there from a distance, and wolves do not walk alone.
      - he never told anyone about the place, it was his solitude, but now he is afraid to go there.
  - **decision** - do **P** want to force Burto to take them to the clearing immediately, at night? - this gives them a chance to see the lack of light, but advances the clock.
  - Olven’s name is mentioned reluctantly - digging further requires **persuasion** to make people talk - 1 piece of information per 1 success:
    - some suspect the oddball Olven, who lives by the forest, but do not really know why - after all, they know him, so he cannot be foreign - but he breeds those insects of his and prefers sitting with them to people
    - he moved out of the village a few years ago, after people had had enough of his huge mosquitoes.
    - women still go to him for herbal remedies, although no one will openly admit it.
  - result:
    - success - advance the **villagers’ trust clock** by 2
    - failure - advance the **villagers’ distrust clock** by 2
- Beyond everything else:
  - Janis is in the inn - **P** see him immediately as they enter (describe him as sitting nearby).
    - he knows there is something wrong with Darra and that she may be the one whose death the villagers want, but he does not believe she is anything evil, because he spent much time with her (he does not know what she really is).
    - when he sees the villagers hire armed outsiders to solve the matter, he realizes things have gone far and begins to waver over whether he did right helping Darra - he knows others will hold it against him, maybe even exile him if it turns out they were right.
    - he slips out unnoticed - **P** notice that someone is gone, but do not know who - if they ask, the villagers answer that Janis left, the local young trapper - from that point on he is never home - if **P** decide to break in, they find love poems he wrote for Darra, but never gave her.
    - he goes beneath the mill to watch Darra, to convince himself whether she is truly evil, whether he was wrong to love her, though he hopes he was not.

## Clearing of devoured light

- **P** can get here either led by the local disabled man, Burto, or by following tracks breaking away from Darra’s trail toward the abandoned mill.
- In the clearing, under a lone linden, if one comes in the evening/night - it is clearly visible that moonlight does not fall on a broad circle around the tree, even though nothing blocks it and it falls normally on the grass around it.
  - this is not visible during the day.
  - this place is the remnant of the cocoon of a Moon Moth Larva from the same family as Darra, which had previously been a wolf, but already completed its transformation, devoured the light around the cocoon after transforming, and flew away.
  - there are no remains of the wolf’s shell, because that changed into the moth’s body.
- What can be learned with the **move Discover Reality** or by making individual **trait** tests:
